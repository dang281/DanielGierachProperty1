## 1. Off-Market Is Convenient. It Is Not Usually Optimal.

### Original

> Off-market sales are a regular feature of the Brisbane inner east. An agent calls with a buyer ready to go, no campaign required, settle quickly, no open homes. For a vendor who values privacy and convenience, it sounds compelling.
>
> The trade-off is buyer competition, and limiting buyer competition almost always affects price.
>
> A property that sells off-market transacts with one buyer. That buyer's offer reflects what they are prepared to pay without competition. A property that runs a proper campaign and attracts three or four serious buyers will almost always produce a higher result, because competitive buyers commit to their best number rather than an opening position.
>
> The gap between an off-market result and a campaigned result in the inner east regularly runs to tens of thousands of dollars on a standard property. On a riverfront home or a large character block, it can be significantly more.
>
> There are scenarios where off-market makes sense. A vendor with genuine privacy concerns, a property with circumstances that make a campaign difficult, or a case where the vendor has a long-standing relationship with a buyer and the price is agreed at a level both parties find fair. These are real situations.
>
> What they are not is an instruction from an agent that "we have a buyer ready to go." That is a service to the agent's buyer, not to you as the vendor. The question to ask is: what price does this buyer become competitive?

### Tone assessment

Structurally sound — Paperclip gets the argument right. The issues are around hook and specificity:

- Opening is descriptive (off-market sales are a feature of the inner east) rather than pointed. Daniel's hooks typically push.
- "The mechanism" middle section reads like a textbook — accurate but generic.
- "What they are not is..." is a manufactured contrast that sounds like AI.
- "The question to ask is: what price does this buyer become competitive?" — good line, but buried at the end.
- No specific example. Tens of thousands of dollars "on a standard property" is hand-wavy.
- Missing Daniel's typical close (invitation + site).

### Revised (ready to schedule)

> An agent rings you with a buyer ready to go. No campaign, no open homes, quick settlement. On a quiet street in Norman Park or a riverside home in Hawthorne, the pitch lands easily.
>

>

A campaign works with three or four serious buyers each committing to their best number because they know they are competing. On a standard inner-east house the gap between those two results is often $40k to $80k. On a riverfront or a large character block it is higher.

>

## 2. Pricing Strategy Drives Buyer Competition: Or Kills It

### Original

> The single biggest lever a seller controls in a property campaign is not the timing, the styling, or the marketing spend. It is the pricing strategy.
>
> A property priced to attract three competing buyers will almost always sell above what a property priced to attract one buyer achieves. That sounds obvious until you are actually in the room deciding on a campaign number, and the temptation to anchor high is very real.
>
> The mechanism is straightforward. When a buyer sees a property and believes other buyers are also looking seriously, they commit to their best number. When they believe they have time and no competition, they start low and negotiate. The campaign strategy has to create the first situation, not the second.
>
> In the inner east, where stock is limited and buyers have often been researching for months, a well-priced property in the right condition gets traction fast. The first two weekends of any campaign are the most important. Buyer interest peaks early, and it does not come back at the same level if the first two opens pass without an offer.
>
> Pricing to attract competition is not the same as underquoting. It means setting an expectation that reflects market value accurately and presents the property as accessible to the right buyer pool. That distinction matters, and it drives campaigns that perform.
>
> If you are weighing up a campaign this year, the pricing conversation is the one to have first.

### Tone assessment

- Opens with a tricolon negation ("not the timing, the styling, or the marketing spend") — classic AI scaffolding.
- "The mechanism is straightforward" is textbook-speak.
- Several "not X, Y" constructions back-to-back.
- Generic close.
- Good substance but no specific number, no anecdote, no sharp edge.

### Revised

> There is one number on a campaign plan that decides whether you get three offers in the first fortnight or no offers at all.
>
> It is not the commission. It is not the marketing spend. It is the price you launch at.
>
> Launch at the market, and inner-east buyers who have been searching since January turn up at the first open ready to compete. Launch above the market, and those buyers keep watching from their phones, waiting for the price reduction they know is coming.
>
> The gap between those two campaigns is usually visible by the end of weekend two. A property that does not have an offer by weekend three is almost always dealing with a launch price that was set for the vendor's wish, not for the buyers actually in the room.
>
> Pricing to create competition is not the same as underquoting. It is setting the number where the right pool of buyers treats the property as accessible. Done properly, it produces bidding at or above the guide. Done carelessly, it invites a slow campaign and a reduction.
>
> If you are weighing up a sale, the price conversation is the first one, not the last.
>
> danielgierach.com
>
> #BrisbaneProperty #InnerEastBrisbane #SellingProperty #PricingStrategy #DanielGierach

Word count: ~220. Specific campaign cadence (weekend two, weekend three), sharper opener, retains the "not underquoting" clarification.

## 3. What Days on Market Is Actually Telling You

>
> A property that sells in under 21 days in Brisbane's inner east has usually attracted competitive interest. Either the price does not reflect the market, the property has a condition issue that buyers are walking away from, or the campaign strategy has not connected with the right buyer pool.
>
> This matters for buyers because a property with 60+ days on market often has room to negotiate that a freshly listed property does not. Understanding why it has sat is the key question. If it is a price issue and the property itself is sound, that can be an advantage for a well-prepared buyer.
>
> For sellers, days on market data on comparable properties is the most honest guide to where your price needs to be. If the properties that sold quickly were priced at a certain level, and the ones that sat were priced higher, the market has already given you the answer.
>
> In the inner east, the gap between a well-received campaign and a slow one is usually a pricing conversation that needed to happen before launch, not after.

- Opener is assertion-based but reads slightly lecturing ("most buyers and sellers do not pay enough attention to it").
- The middle uses a three-part diagnosis ("Either the price..., or the property..., or the campaign...") — a tricolon.
- Splits into "for buyers" / "for sellers" sections — essay structure, not LinkedIn cadence.
- Closes on a repeated Daniel trope ("pricing conversation that needed to happen before launch, not after") — the line is actually good.

>
> For a seller, the days-on-market numbers on comparable sales are the most honest pricing guide you will get. The properties that transacted quickly told you where buyers were willing to commit. The ones that sat told you where they were not.

> The pricing conversation that avoids a long days-on-market is the one you have before launch, not the one you have after 30 days of silence.

## 4. List Price, Reserve, and Market Value Are Three Different Numbers

### Original

> List price, reserve, and market value are three different numbers. Buyers and sellers who understand the difference make better decisions at every stage of a campaign.
>
> Market value is what a willing buyer and a willing seller agree on in an open and competitive market. It is not what the vendor needs to achieve, not what they paid, and not what a neighbouring property sold for three years ago. It is a current number derived from current comparable sales in similar condition, configuration, and position.
>
> A list price (or price guide in Queensland) is the number used to attract buyers to the campaign. In a well-run campaign, it is set to be honest and to attract the widest relevant buyer pool. An inflated list price attracts the wrong buyers or no buyers. A list price that accurately reflects the market brings the buyers who can actually transact.
>
> A reserve at auction is the minimum the vendor will accept under the hammer. It is set before auction day and is not disclosed to bidders. Setting the reserve correctly is one of the most important decisions in an auction campaign. A reserve set too high passes the property in; a reserve set at market value allows the auction to find the price the market is prepared to pay.
>
> These three numbers should be close to each other in a well-run campaign. When they diverge significantly, the campaign usually encounters problems.

### Tone assessment

- Clear and instructive, but reads like an article summary, not a LinkedIn post.
- Dense with parallel structures ("not what the vendor needs to achieve, not what they paid, and not what a neighbouring property sold for") — tricolon.
- Definitions-first structure is a scholarly pattern that Daniel's posts usually avoid.
- No specific example makes the point land.

### Revised

> Three numbers run in parallel on every campaign. Sellers who confuse them rarely get the result they wanted.
>
> Market value is what a willing buyer and a willing seller land on today, on comparable evidence from the last 90 days. Not what the neighbour sold for in 2022. Not what the vendor paid. Not what the vendor needs.
>
> List price (the price guide in Queensland) is the number that attracts the right buyers to the campaign. Honest guides bring the buyers who can actually transact. Inflated guides bring browsers, and then silence.
>
> Reserve is set on auction morning, not disclosed to bidders, and is the minimum the vendor will accept under the hammer. Too high, the property passes in. At market, the room finds the number.
>
> In a well-run campaign, all three numbers are close. When they are far apart, the campaign is usually telling the vendor something the vendor has not been ready to hear.
>
> danielgierach.com
>
> #BrisbaneProperty #AuctionBrisbane #InnerEastBrisbane #SellingProperty #DanielGierach

Word count: ~175. Much tighter. Same definitional content but LinkedIn-paced.

## 5. How to Read a Comparable Sales Report

### Original

> A comparable sales report is the primary tool for understanding what a property is worth. Most people read them incorrectly.
>
> The most common mistake is treating all comparables as equal. A three-bedroom home on Riverview Road in Balmoral is not comparable to a three-bedroom home on a busy road in the same suburb. A renovated character home is not comparable to the same floor plan unrenovated. A property that sold 18 months ago is not comparable to the current market in the same way a property that sold six weeks ago is.
>
> The comparables that matter are the ones that share the same physical position (elevated vs flat, corner vs mid-block, quiet street vs main road), the same configuration (bedroom and bathroom count, garage), the same renovation level, and that sold within the last 90 days. That list is often shorter than it looks.
>
> If there are no strong comparables in the immediate suburb, the analysis expands to the neighbouring suburb with similar characteristics. If those properties are consistently selling at a certain level, and the subject property is materially different in some way, that difference needs to be quantified and explained.
>
> The agent presenting a comparable sales report should be able to explain every adjustment they have made. If they cannot explain why one comparable was included and another was not, the report is not doing the work it needs to do. Ask the question.

### Tone assessment

- Strong substance. Good Balmoral specificity in one line.
- "Most people read them incorrectly" is instructive-toned.
- Triple parallel in the middle ("A renovated character home is not..."/"A property that sold..." — two more negations after the first).
- The "ask the question" close is good Daniel, but the sentence is dangled.

### Revised

> A comparable sales report is the single most useful document in a listing conversation. Most of them are read the wrong way.
>
> Not every sale is a comparable. A renovated house on an elevated quiet street in Balmoral is not the same as the unrenovated floor plan on a main road in the same postcode. A sale from 2024 is not a read on the market in April 2026. A three-bedder on a 600sqm block is not a proxy for the same three-bedder on 405sqm.
>
> The comparables that matter share the same position (elevated or flat, quiet street or main road), the same configuration, the same renovation level, and settled in the last 90 days. In the inner east, that filter often leaves three or four sales, not thirty.
>
> When a suburb does not have strong recent comparables, the analysis moves to the next suburb with similar characteristics. Bulimba and Hawthorne borrow from each other. So do Norman Park and Camp Hill. Each adjustment has to be quantified and explained.
>
> The test for your agent: can they explain, out loud, why each comparable was included and why the others were left out? If not, the report has not done the work.
>

>
> #BrisbaneProperty #InnerEastBrisbane #PropertyValuation #RayWhiteBulimba #DanielGierach

Word count: ~220. Replaces the triple negation with a cleaner rhythm and adds neighbourhood-borrow pairs.

## 6. What a Buyer's Agent Does: and What They Don't

### Original

> A buyer's agent works exclusively for the buyer. Their job is to find, evaluate, and negotiate a property on behalf of someone who is either too time-poor, too unfamiliar with the market, or too emotionally invested to negotiate effectively themselves.
>
> For buyers, the value proposition depends on the situation. Interstate buyers purchasing in a market they do not know well benefit significantly. First-home buyers who have not been through a negotiation before often find the fee worthwhile. Experienced local buyers who have done several transactions may not need one.
>
> The fee structure varies. Most buyer's agents in Queensland charge either a flat fee or a percentage of the purchase price. On a $1.2M property, a 1.5% fee is $18,000. That fee needs to be weighed against what the buyer's agent actually achieves in negotiation and access, not just what they say they will achieve.
>
> For sellers, understanding that your buyer may be represented changes how you approach negotiation. A buyer's agent who presents an offer is working to minimise the price their client pays. That is their job. Knowing who is actually on the other side of the negotiation, and what their brief is, is useful information for any vendor.
>
> Buyer's agents do not have access to any information a diligent buyer could not find themselves. What they have is time, market experience, and emotional detachment. Whether those are worth the fee depends on the buyer.

### Tone assessment

- Educational throughout.
- Multiple tricolons ("too time-poor, too unfamiliar with the market, or too emotionally invested" / "Interstate buyers, First-home buyers, Experienced local buyers" / "time, market experience, and emotional detachment").
- Ends on a weak generic "depends on the buyer" line.
- Does have a useful number ($18,000 on a $1.2M property).
- Needs a sharper angle. Most agents who write about buyer's agents do so defensively. Daniel can afford to be direct.

### Revised

> A buyer's agent works for the buyer, and their fee on a $1.2M inner-east purchase lands around $18,000. If you are a vendor, understanding what they do (and what they cannot do) changes how you read an offer.
>
> A buyer's agent has time, distance from the emotion, and a track record negotiating. They do not have access to off-market stock a diligent buyer could not find themselves with a few weekends and a network.
>
> They add genuine value for interstate buyers who are searching blind, first-home buyers who have never been through a real negotiation, and time-poor professionals who will miss the right property otherwise. They add less value for experienced local buyers who have already done a few transactions and know their patch.
>
> When a buyer's agent presents an offer on your property, their brief is to get it for the lowest number that gets it across the line. That is the job. It does not mean the offer is bad, but it does mean you should assume the first number is not the ceiling.
>
> If you are a vendor and the offer comes through a buyer's agent, the useful question to ask your own agent is: what signals are they giving us about the real ceiling?
>

## 7. When a Contract Falls Through, the Campaign Is Not Over

### Original

> A property going back to market after a contract falls through is not a failure. It is a situation that needs to be handled correctly.
>
> The first question is why it fell through. A buyer who exits due to a finance condition failing had a lending problem, not a property problem. A buyer who exits after a building and pest report found something significant is telling you something you need to address, either through pricing or remediation. Understanding the reason determines what happens next.
>
> The way the property returns to market matters. A vendor who goes back publicly immediately with no change in price or presentation is signalling that nothing has changed. Buyers who passed on the property before will pass again. A vendor who takes two weeks to address whatever the contract revealed, adjusts the presentation or the price as needed, and relaunches with a clear reason why now is a better opportunity is positioned differently.
>
> Days on market accumulates even when a property is under contract. A property that has been on the market for 90 days total, including a period under contract that fell through, needs a strong reason for the next buyer to proceed with confidence.
>
> The practical answer is transparency. Buyers who understand exactly what happened and why a property is available again, combined with a price that reflects the current situation, tend to transact. Buyers who feel they are being kept in the dark do not.

### Tone assessment

- "Not a failure" opener is an AI negation-framing pattern.
- "The way the property returns to market matters" is filler.
- Long — could lose 40-50 words without losing substance.
- Good underlying framework (diagnose reason, pause, relaunch).

### Revised

> A contract falling through is not the end of a campaign. How you handle the next two weeks is.
>
> Start with the diagnosis. A buyer exiting on finance had a lender problem, not a property problem. A buyer exiting on a building and pest report found something real, and the market will find it too if you do not address it. The reason determines what you do next.
>
> A vendor who throws the listing straight back up publicly, same photos, same price, is telling the market nothing has changed. The buyers who passed before will pass again. A vendor who takes two weeks, addresses what the inspection revealed, adjusts price or presentation where it is needed, and relaunches with a specific reason the new campaign is stronger, lands in a different conversation.

> The rule of thumb: transparency beats silence. Buyers who know exactly what happened and can price it in will transact. Buyers who sense information is being hidden will not.

## 8. How to Negotiate After a Building and Pest Report

### Original

> A building and pest report is not a price renegotiation document by default. Whether it gives a buyer genuine grounds to negotiate depends on what the report actually says.
>
> Building and pest reports have two categories of findings that matter. Active defects are things that are broken, failing, or actively deteriorating now. General maintenance items are things that are worn, aged, or due for attention in the normal course of ownership. These two categories require completely different responses.
>
> Active defects, particularly active timber pest activity, significant structural movement, or a failing roof, are material findings that affect the value of the property. A buyer who discovers an active termite issue after signing has found something that was not apparent at inspection and that affects the price they agreed to pay. That is a reasonable basis for discussion.
>
> General maintenance items, aged paint, minor weathering, small cracks in render, worn gutters, are features of every property of a certain age. Using them to renegotiate price on a 1950s Queenslander in Hawthorne is not a negotiation strategy, it is a reason for the vendor to reopen the campaign to the next buyer.
>
> The buyers who negotiate well after a report read it carefully, identify the genuine active issues, quantify what remediation would cost from a licensed tradesperson, and present a specific, reasonable adjustment to the vendor. The buyers who lose properties use the report as a generic lever and get told no.

### Tone assessment

- Good structure. Two-category framework lands.
- 1950s Queenslander in Hawthorne reference is exactly the kind of specificity that works.
- Final paragraph has a quadruple parallel ("read it, identify, quantify, present") that reads as AI.
- Could lose 30 words.

### Revised

> A building and pest report is not a price-renegotiation lever by default. Whether it gives a buyer real grounds for a discount depends on what the report actually says.
>
> Two categories of findings matter, and they are treated differently.
>
> Active defects are things failing now: timber pest activity, significant structural movement, a failing roof. If the report finds one of these and it was not disclosed, the buyer has reasonable grounds to open a conversation about price or remediation.
>
> General maintenance is aged paint, minor weathering, small cracks in render, worn gutters, flaking window sills. These are features of every 1950s Queenslander in Hawthorne and every post-war brick in Carina. Using them to ask for a price reduction is not a negotiation, it is a reason for the vendor to walk to the next buyer.
>
> The buyers who negotiate well after a report bring a specific defect, a specific quote from a licensed tradesperson, and a specific ask. The buyers who lose the property bring a generic list.
>
> If you are on either side of this conversation, the quality of the report and the specificity of the ask are what decide whether anyone budges.
>
> danielgierach.com

## 9. Presenting Your Home for Sale Is Not the Same as Renovating It

A cracked roof, a failing retaining wall, a significant pest damage issue, these are not presentation problems, they are condition problems, and they will show up in a buyer's building and pest report. Fixing genuine defects protects the existing value of the property. Ignoring them typically shows up as a price renegotiation after contract.

## 10. The Sales Campaign Starts Long Before the First Open Home

### Original

> Most vendors think a property campaign starts when the listing goes live. The agents who get the best results know it starts four to eight weeks before that.
>
> The preparation phase is where the price is made. Styling decisions, photography scheduling, building and pest reports for vendor disclosure, Form 6 review, method of sale discussions, comparable sales analysis, all of this happens before a single buyer walks through the door. Vendors who rush this phase and launch before they are ready usually pay for it in the result.
>
> Photography is the most underestimated item. In the inner east, the majority of buyer research happens online before anyone attends an open home. The photographs are the campaign's first impression. Properties with strong photography attract more enquiry, more attendees, and more competition at open homes. Better competition produces better results.
>
> The timeline that works is roughly this: four to six weeks of preparation, photography and listing material finalised, launch on a Wednesday or Thursday so the first weekend has full online exposure, two or three open homes over the first two weekends while buyer interest is highest, then offer and negotiation.
>
> The first two weekends are critical. Buyer interest peaks at launch and does not come back at the same level. A campaign that has not generated offers by weekend three is usually dealing with a preparation or pricing problem that should have been solved before launch.

### Tone assessment

- Long. Can lose 50 words.
- "The preparation phase is where the price is made" is good.
- The six-item list in paragraph 2 reads as filler.
- Strong operational specifics (Wed/Thu launch, first two weekends) — keep these.
- Opener is fine but could land harder.

### Revised

> A property campaign does not start when the listing goes live. It starts four to eight weeks before.
>
> The preparation weeks are where the price is actually made. Pricing strategy, method of sale, photography, styling decisions, Form 6, pre-market comparable sales analysis, seller disclosure, pest and building readiness. The vendors who rush this phase pay for it later, and the discount is almost always bigger than the saving.
>
> Photography is the item most under-spent on and most under-prepared for. In the inner east, 80% of buyer shortlisting happens on a phone before a single Saturday inspection. The photographs are the shortlist.
