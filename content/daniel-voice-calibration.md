## POSITIONING: CONSULTANT, NOT SALESPERSON (added 2026-05-30)

**Pattern:** Daniel wants to be read as a thoughtful advisor explaining his craft, not an agent driving for the close. Every post should help the reader understand the field better, even if they never become a client.

**Apply this by:**
- **Define terminology when first introduced.** "Auction reserve, the minimum acceptable bid the seller sets before the auction…" rather than assuming the reader already knows the term.
- **Show the reasoning, not just the conclusion.** Why this works, what the trade-off is, what changes the answer. The reader should leave the post understanding the mechanic, not just the outcome.
- **Frame links as "resources for thinking through your own situation"** rather than "here's my tool / read my article". Advisor sharing a resource, not founder pushing product.
- **Treat the audience as people learning the field**, not insiders who already speak the jargon. The right reader is a homeowner thinking about selling in 12-24 months who wants to understand the moving parts before they pick an agent.
- **Avoid first-person founder framing** like "I built a calculator". Prefer "There's a calculator that walks through the inputs in plain English" or "I put a tool together to make the maths transparent".

**What to avoid:** Anything that sounds like a marketing CTA. Anything that assumes the reader is already a seller. Anything that flatters the reader to soften an ask.

**Why this exists:** Confirmed 2026-05-30 alongside the LinkedIn strategy pivot to a poll-first weekly arc. Daniel's positioning bet is that consistent advisory-tone content over 6-12 months produces stronger inbound from quality sellers than any number of sales-tone posts. The content has to earn the audience's understanding before it earns their business.

## LANGUAGE: SOFTEN DEFINITIVE CLAIMS

**Pattern:** Don't state outcomes as certainties. Use "can", "often", "in many cases" rather than "always", "almost always", "regularly".

**Why:** Property outcomes depend heavily on individual circumstances. Definitive claims feel like agent spin. Daniel's credibility comes from being the honest voice, not the one overselling.

**Examples of what changed:**

| Before (wrong) | After (right) |
|---|---|
| "limiting buyer competition almost always affects price" | "limiting buyer competition can affect price" |
| "the gap regularly runs to tens of thousands" | "the gap can be significant" |
| "almost never earn back their full cost" | "may not earn back their full cost in all circumstances" |

**Apply to:** Any post that makes a claim about what the market does, what prices do, what buyers do, what renovations return. Add nuance unless it is a documented fact with a source.

---

## RENOVATION: NUANCED, NOT A BLANKET RULE

**Pattern:** Never say renovation is "never" worth it or "always" the wrong call. Some buyers want a blank canvas. Some properties benefit. It depends.

**The correct framing:**
- Renovation is a different calculation from presentation
- Some buyers at this price point want to choose their own finishes: they won't pay a premium for yours
- Some buyers specifically want a property they can make their own: a full renovation before sale may not produce a higher price because that buyer intends to redo it anyway
- The decision depends on the property, the buyer pool, and what the work actually costs

**What to avoid:** "Inner east buyers who can afford to pay $1.4M can also afford to renovate it to their own taste. They are not paying full price for your taste.": Too direct, reads like an attack on the vendor.

**Do use:** Frame it as a strategic question, not a moral judgment.

## FEES AND COSTS: ALWAYS SHOW RANGES, NOT SINGLE EXAMPLES

**Pattern:** When mentioning any fee, cost, or price point, give a range rather than one specific figure.

**Why:** A single example feels arbitrary and may be wrong for the reader's situation. A range is honest and more useful.

**Examples:**
- Buyer's agent fees: "flat fees typically run from $10,000 to $25,000, and percentage-based fees generally sit between 1% and 2.5%"
- Kitchen renovation: don't give a specific figure: say "significant cost" or give a range if you have one verified
- Property styling: don't pin to one price

## GEOGRAPHY: EAST-SPECIFIC, NOT GENERIC BRISBANE

**Pattern:** Always anchor to the inner east when writing about market conditions. Not "Brisbane" generically.

**Suburbs to name where relevant:** Hawthorne, Norman Park, Bulimba, Balmoral, Morningside, Camp Hill, Coorparoo, Carina, East Brisbane, Cannon Hill.

**Why:** Daniel's audience is inner east homeowners and buyers. Generic Brisbane references feel irrelevant. Suburb-specific references feel like local expertise.

## AUDIENCE: ALL PROPERTIES, NOT JUST HIGH-END

**Pattern:** When writing advice posts (preparation, presentation, off-market, etc.), frame the advice as applying to all properties in the east, not just $1.2M+ or $1.4M+ homes.

**Why:** Daniel's database includes sellers across all price points. Framing advice as only relevant above a price threshold excludes a large portion of his audience.

**Exception:** When the specific audience IS high-end (e.g. a post about premium campaigns, riverfront listings), it's fine to be specific.

## BUYER'S AGENTS: ACKNOWLEDGE THEIR ACCESS ADVANTAGE

**Pattern:** When writing about buyer's agents, include that their network of agent relationships gives access to pre-market and off-market properties before they hit the public portals.

**Why:** This is a genuine advantage Daniel wants acknowledged. It adds nuance to the "is a buyer's agent worth it" question and is factually accurate.

---

## PREPARATION ADVICE: AGENT'S-EYE ROI FRAMING

**Pattern:** When writing about what a vendor should do before selling, include the idea that the options are extensive and it comes down to the seller's appetite: but an experienced agent's eye identifies the work that earns its cost back (2.5x or more return) versus the work that won't.

**Why:** This positions Daniel as the person who helps sellers make smart decisions, not just a checklist enforcer. It is also the honest truth.

**Apply to:** Any preparation, presentation, or pre-sale improvement post.

---

## ESTABLISHED HOMES: CALL THEM OUT SPECIFICALLY

**Pattern:** When preparation or presentation advice applies especially to older/established homes (not new builds), say so explicitly.

**Why:** Most of Daniel's vendor clients are selling established homes, not new builds. The advice is different and the audience relates better when it's named.

---

## OFF-MARKET: FRAME AS TRADE-OFF, NOT VERDICT

**Pattern:** Off-market is not "bad": it is a trade-off. Less competition can affect price, but there are legitimate reasons to go off-market. Frame it as a strategic consideration, not a definitive verdict.

**Correct framing:**
- Off-market can suit vendors with genuine privacy concerns
- It can suit properties where a campaign is difficult
- The trade-off is buyer competition, which can affect the final price
- The question to ask is whether the convenience justifies that risk for your specific situation

**What to avoid:** Definitive statements that off-market "always" costs money or "always" serves the agent more than the vendor.

---

## BUYER'S AGENT: NOT A HIGH-END-ONLY SERVICE

**Pattern:** Never frame buyer's agents as being "for" high-price buyers or a specific price bracket. The real driver is competition and difficulty finding stock, not price point.

**Why:** Buyers across all price ranges use buyer's agents in Brisbane. The $1M+ framing is factually wrong and excludes a large portion of Daniel's audience. The honest reason people hire buyer's agents is that stock is tight, good properties move fast, and having someone with agent relationships and market access makes a difference regardless of what you are spending.

**What to avoid:** "particularly for buyers in the $1M+ range" or "interstate buyers and those purchasing at the premium end"

**Do use:** "The main driver is not price point. Stock is tight, good properties move quickly, and having someone with agent relationships and genuine market knowledge can affect both what you find and what you pay."

---

## LINKEDIN GEOGRAPHY: BROADER THAN INNER EAST

**Pattern:** For LinkedIn posts (as opposed to Facebook suburb spotlights), frame content around Brisbane broadly, not just "Brisbane's inner east." LinkedIn's audience is national and professional. The inner east framing is appropriate for Facebook and suburb-specific posts, but limits the relevance of LinkedIn content unnecessarily.

**Why:** LinkedIn reaches a much wider professional audience including interstate buyers and investors who are not specifically searching the inner east. Posts that only mention the inner east feel irrelevant to most of that audience.

**Apply to:** LinkedIn captions that are not specifically about a named suburb or the inner east market specifically. Use "across Brisbane" or "in Brisbane" rather than "Brisbane's inner east."
