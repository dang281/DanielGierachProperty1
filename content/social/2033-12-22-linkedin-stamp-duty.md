## Caption

On a $1.1M owner-occupier purchase in Queensland, transfer duty adds approximately $43,350 to the purchase price. Most buyers making that offer have not run that calculation. The figure sits outside the deposit, outside the borrowing, and outside the loan-to-value ratio. It comes due within 30 days of settlement, payable to Queensland Revenue Office, and it does not add to the equity position in the property.

Transfer duty is paid by the buyer on a tiered rate that increases with the purchase price. It must be budgeted alongside the deposit, not treated as a line item to calculate later. A buyer who has stretched their deposit to make an offer competitive can find that the transfer duty figure creates a cash shortfall they did not anticipate. The right time to run the calculation is before the offer is made, not after the contract is signed.

The first-home buyer concession applies on eligible purchases up to $700,000 in Queensland, with a tapering threshold that reduces the benefit at higher prices. The reality for most buyers purchasing in Brisbane's inner east is that the threshold makes the concession inaccessible. Properties in Bulimba, Camp Hill, Norman Park, Hawthorne, and Balmoral are routinely transacting well above $700,000. Buyers should not budget on the assumption that a concession applies without first confirming eligibility.

The authoritative source for the exact figure is the Queensland Revenue Office transfer duty calculator. Estimates from third-party sources vary and the QRO calculator is the only figure that should be used to plan the budget.
