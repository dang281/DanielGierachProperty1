## Caption

The main residence capital gains tax exemption is not automatic. Most vendors assume it is, and some find out otherwise at the worst possible time: after the campaign has launched and an accountant has finally been called.

The exemption applies fully when the property has been your principal place of residence for the entire period of ownership. When a property was rented at any point, even for a short period and even years ago, a partial CGT liability can apply to those years. The calculation is based on the proportion of the ownership period during which the property was not your main residence. A two-year tenancy in a property held for fifteen years still produces a taxable fraction.

There are other situations that affect the exemption. Properties acquired before 1985 are CGT-exempt entirely. Properties that were rented after 20 August 1996 without the six-year absence rule applying correctly can generate unexpected assessments. Owners who moved out, treated another property as their main residence for the absence period, and then returned face specific rules about how that election is recorded and what happens if they did not make it formally.

None of this is an argument against selling. It is an argument for knowing your position before you sign an agency agreement, not after you have accepted an offer and the settlement date is fixed. An accountant familiar with property CGT can usually work through the exposure quickly once they have the dates and the property history.

The article covers the main scenarios and the questions to bring to your accountant before you list.
