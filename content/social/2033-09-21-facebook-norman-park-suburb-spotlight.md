## Caption

Norman Park's spring market draws buyers who have done their homework on the inner east corridor and concluded that Norman Park is one of the better-kept pricing stories in this part of Brisbane. The suburb sits between Camp Hill and East Brisbane, with access to the Norman Creek parkland and the riverfront paths to the north, but consistently at a price point that remains lower than its immediate neighbours on both sides. Buyers who identify that gap tend to act on it quickly.

The Norman Creek green corridor and the connecting pathway network give Norman Park residents access to a linear parkland that runs from the suburb's western boundary toward the river. For households with dogs, children, or daily walkers, that green access is an amenity that influences suburb decisions in ways that do not always show up in the comparable sales data but are consistently mentioned at open homes.

The character housing stock in Norman Park, predominantly pre-war and interwar Queenslanders on established blocks, appeals to a buyer who has made a specific choice to pursue original character over the renovated and rebuilt stock available in the adjacent suburbs. The blocks in Norman Park are generous by inner east standards and provide the land area that buyers with renovation ambitions find difficult to secure in Camp Hill or East Brisbane at the same price.

For vendors considering a spring campaign, September is Norman Park's strongest window of the year.
