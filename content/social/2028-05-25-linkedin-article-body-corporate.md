## Caption

Most buyers look at the body corporate levy amount on a listing. Very few look at the sinking fund balance before they buy. That is the wrong way around.

The levy tells you what you will pay each quarter. The sinking fund tells you whether the body corporate actually has the money available to cover major works when they arise. A healthy sinking fund means the levy you see is likely to remain stable. An underfunded sinking fund means a special levy is coming, and special levies are not small. Depending on the work required, they can run to tens of thousands of dollars per lot, and they are issued with little warning.

The body corporate disclosure statement, which Queensland law requires a vendor to provide before exchange, contains both the levy schedule and the sinking fund balance. Knowing what to look for in that document is one of the most practical things a prospective buyer of a strata property can do.

Levy amounts in Brisbane's inner east vary considerably by property type. Townhouses and boutique complexes tend to carry lower levies than large buildings with lifts, pools, and shared amenities. The levy is a legitimate holding cost that affects yield for investors and borrowing serviceability for owner-occupiers, so it deserves more attention than a single line on a listing sheet.
