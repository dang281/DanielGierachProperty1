## Caption

The cliche about December is that everyone goes on holiday and the market shuts down. The reality is more specific than that.

Open home numbers drop in December. That is true. The volume of new listings drops too. But the quality of the buyers who are still attending inspections in early December is high. These are people who are there because they have to be. They have a settlement arriving, a lease ending, a school catchment commitment to honour, or a relocation that is happening regardless of what the calendar says. They are not browsers. They are buyers with a specific deadline.

Because both sides often want to be finished before Christmas, transactions in December tend to happen quickly. A buyer who wants a December settlement and a vendor who wants a December settlement are aligned in a way that the September market is not. That alignment creates momentum. Negotiations that might take two or three weeks in other months can conclude in days.

Vendors who are willing to be flexible on settlement timing in December, particularly those who can offer a January settlement if the buyer needs it, often find that flexibility is well rewarded. It reduces friction for the buyer at a point where they are trying to eliminate uncertainty, and buyers in that position will sometimes accept a price they would have negotiated harder on in a less time-pressured situation.

December is a smaller market. It is not a poor market. The distinction matters.
