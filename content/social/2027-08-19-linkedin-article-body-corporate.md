## Caption

Body corporate levies for a small townhouse in Brisbane's inner east typically run between $2,000 and $4,000 per year. A mid-rise building with a lift and common facilities can run $6,000 to $14,000 or more annually. Most buyers check that number and factor it into their holding cost calculations. That is good practice, but it is not enough.

The number that actually tells you the financial health of a body corporate scheme is the sinking fund balance. The sinking fund is the pool of money set aside for major capital works: roof replacements, lift overhauls, repainting, structural repairs. A scheme with a well-funded sinking fund can absorb those costs without issuing special levies to owners. A scheme with an underfunded sinking fund cannot, and the owners pay the difference when the work becomes unavoidable.

A body corporate disclosure statement will show you both the levy schedule and the sinking fund balance. Reading both, and understanding what a healthy ratio looks like for a scheme of that age and building type, is the due diligence step that separates buyers who understand what they are buying from buyers who find out the hard way two years after settlement.

This matters more in older buildings, buildings with expensive common infrastructure, and any scheme that has deferred maintenance. I have written a full breakdown of body corporate considerations for buyers in Queensland, including what to look for in the disclosure documents.
