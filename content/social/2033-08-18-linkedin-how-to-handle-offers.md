## Caption

The first offer a vendor receives is rarely the offer they sign. The question is whether the response to that first offer keeps the negotiation alive or closes it.

Rejecting a low offer outright terminates the conversation. The buyer walks away with the clear message that the vendor is not interested, and in most cases they do not return. The vendor is left with the principle of not accepting a low offer but without the deal that might have been reached if the conversation had continued.

A counter-offer serves a different purpose. It says: this is not the number, but here is what the number is. The buyer now has information they did not have before. They know the vendor's floor, or close to it. They can decide whether the gap is bridgeable or whether to move on. In most cases, a buyer who has taken the step of making an offer on a property has enough interest that a counter-offer brings them back to the table.

The conditions in an offer matter as much as the price. A low unconditional offer sometimes represents a better starting point than a higher conditional offer, because the buyer's genuine readiness is visible. The conditions that can be negotiated are different from the conditions that are immovable.

The article covers the full offer response process, how to read what a first offer is telling you, and the negotiation principles that produce better outcomes for inner east vendors.
