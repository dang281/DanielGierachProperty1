## Caption

The method of sale is the most consequential decision a vendor makes before launching. Most make it based on what the neighbour used.

That is a reasonable starting point but not a sufficient one. A neighbour's property and your property share a street, not necessarily a buyer demographic, a price point, or a level of buyer competition. The method of sale should follow the property and the buyer pool, not the recent local trend or the agent's preference for one format over another.

Auction works best when the buyer pool is genuinely broad and competitive. Multiple buyers who want the same property, who are ready to transact, and who are willing to bid against each other produce the conditions under which an auction delivers a strong outcome. Without that competition, an auction is a public declaration of value that caps the result at whatever the single motivated buyer was prepared to offer privately. Private treaty suits properties where the buyer demographic is narrower, where conditions on the contract matter to the likely buyer, or where the vendor needs more certainty around timing than an auction date provides.

Expression of Interest is the third option and it is frequently misapplied. EOI suits genuinely unique properties where the buyer value range is wide and where creating a structured competitive process is the goal, but where the open-ended nature of an auction is unlikely to attract participation. It is not a default choice for properties where auction or private treaty would work better.

The article covers how to evaluate the right method for your property rather than the right method for the suburb.
