## Caption

Most buyers open a building and pest report and go straight to the summary page. That is the wrong place to start.

The summary categorises defects but it does not explain severity, urgency, or cost. A report that lists twelve items under "major defects" sounds alarming. A report that lists three items under "major defects" sounds manageable. Neither reading is necessarily accurate without understanding what those items actually are.

Active timber pest activity and a routine note about a rusted gutter are both reportable findings. They require completely different responses. Active termite damage in a structural element is a negotiation point, a potential deal-breaker, and something that needs a specialist quote before you can assess the true cost. A rusted gutter is a weekend job.

The other thing buyers miss is the difference between items that require immediate action and items that are typical for a property of that age. An older Queenslander in the inner east is going to have wear, movement, and maintenance items. The question is not whether the report has findings, but whether the findings are consistent with the age and type of the property, or whether they represent something outside that range.

A building and pest report is only useful if you know how to read it. The full guide to interpreting one, asking the right follow-up questions, and knowing when to negotiate is here: danielgierach.com/insights/how-to-read-a-building-and-pest-report
