## Caption

"We have a buyer ready to go" is the most common phrase in a pre-market approach. It should be the phrase that prompts the most scrutiny.

A vendor who accepts an off-market approach avoids the inconvenience of a campaign: no open homes, no styling, no weeks of uncertainty. That convenience is real. What is also real is that limiting the buyer pool almost always affects the price achieved, and the gap between an off-market result and a well-run campaign result on a $1.2 million inner east property regularly exceeds $50,000.

The ready buyer is usually a convenience offer. It is convenient for the agent who can close the transaction quickly without running a full campaign. It is convenient for the buyer who avoids competing against other motivated purchasers. The vendor is the party in this arrangement whose interest is least well served. Competition between buyers is the mechanism that drives price. Removing competition to make a transaction simpler for everyone else is not a vendor-first decision.

There are circumstances where an off-market sale makes sense. A vendor who has a strong personal reason to avoid a public campaign, or who has specific privacy requirements, may weigh the price difference against those factors and make a clear-eyed decision. The problem is when vendors are not given that choice clearly: when the off-market approach is presented as an opportunity rather than a trade-off.

The article covers how off-market pricing compares to campaign pricing, what the agent's incentive structure looks like in each scenario, and how to evaluate a pre-market approach when one comes to you.
