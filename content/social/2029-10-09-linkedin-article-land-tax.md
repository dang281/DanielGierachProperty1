## Caption

Queensland land tax is not assessed on each property you own individually. It is calculated on the combined unimproved land value of all your Queensland holdings.

That distinction catches investors. You might own two properties, each sitting below the threshold on their own. But Queensland Revenue Office adds the land values together, and if the total exceeds the threshold, you have a land tax liability on the combined figure. The rate then applies to that combined amount above the threshold, not just the marginal portion.

Inner east Brisbane land values have moved significantly over the past several years. Investors who purchased in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Norman Park, or Morningside five to ten years ago may find their combined holding has crossed into land tax territory without any deliberate decision on their part. The first sign is often the annual assessment notice arriving and the figure being higher than expected.

The threshold and applicable rates are set by Queensland state government and can change. Understanding where you sit before you decide to hold, sell, or acquire another property changes the analysis meaningfully.

The article I've linked below covers how Queensland land tax works, what triggers an assessment, how the rates apply to combined portfolios, and what questions investors in the inner east should be asking their accountant right now.
