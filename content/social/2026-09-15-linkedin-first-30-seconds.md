## Caption

Running opens across Brisbane's inner east for years, there is something that happens before buyers reach the kitchen, the bathrooms, or the main bedroom. They've already decided how they feel about the property. Not consciously, in most cases. But the emotional frame that shapes everything that follows is set in the first thirty seconds.

What I watch at every open: how buyers walk up the path. The pace, the body language, whether they slow down to look at the garden or walk straight through. By the time someone reaches the front door, they've already processed the street presentation, the gate, the front fence, the garden, the approach. When a property nails this, buyers arrive in the inspection open and receptive. When it doesn't, they arrive already composing their list of negatives.

The smell when the door opens is the next signal. Fresh air, a clean house, something that smells lived-in well rather than left-alone. Then the light in the entry. Wide hallways and light entries in Queenslander and post-war homes are among the first things buyers respond to.

Vendors who spend money on a great front garden, fresh exterior paint, clean gutters, and a well-presented entry are not spending on aesthetics. They're investing in the frame through which every subsequent feature of the property gets evaluated. A beautiful kitchen lands differently when buyers arrive already engaged than when they arrive already cautious.

The interior matters, but the arrival experience is what sets the tone for everything that follows. It deserves the same attention.
