## Caption

Short-term rental looks more profitable on paper. For the same inner east property, the gross income from a short-term rental can be 30 to 50 per cent higher than a standard long-term tenancy. That headline figure is what attracts investors to platforms like Airbnb.

The problem is that gross income and net return are two very different things for a short-term rental.

The extra costs of running a short-term rental are significant and ongoing: professional cleaning between every booking, consumables that need regular restocking, higher insurance premiums, platform fees and management fees, accelerated wear on furnishings and appliances, more frequent maintenance callouts, and the periods of vacancy between bookings that do not exist in a long-term tenancy. Together, those additional costs typically add $15,000 to $25,000 per year compared to what a long-term tenant would cost to manage.

The net return comparison for a typical inner east property is often closer than the gross figures suggest, and in some cases the long-term tenancy produces a better net yield with far less management time.

There is also a regulatory dimension that has tightened considerably in Brisbane. Body corporate rules on many complexes now restrict or prohibit short-term rental entirely, and Brisbane City Council has its own registration requirements. Whether a property is even eligible for short-term rental is something that needs to be confirmed before purchase, not after.
