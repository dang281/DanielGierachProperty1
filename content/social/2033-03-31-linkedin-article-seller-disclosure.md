## Caption

The Property Law Act 2023 (Qld) changed the seller disclosure framework in Queensland significantly. Under the Act, a vendor is required to provide a completed Seller Disclosure Statement to the buyer before any contract is entered into. This is not optional. It is not something that can be done after the buyer signs. It must happen before.

The consequences of getting this wrong are serious for vendors. If the Seller Disclosure Statement was not provided correctly, or if it contained inaccurate information, the buyer has the right to terminate the contract at any time before settlement. Not within the cooling-off period. Before settlement. That is a window that can extend weeks or months depending on when the problem is discovered.

What the disclosure statement covers: title, zoning, any encumbrances, body corporate information for strata properties, any notices or orders affecting the property, and other prescribed matters. Completing it accurately requires both attention to detail and access to the relevant documents. It is not a form that should be filled in casually.

Most vendors rely on their solicitor to manage the disclosure process, which is appropriate. What matters for a vendor is understanding that the disclosure obligation exists, that it has teeth, and that getting it right at the start of a transaction costs far less than the alternative.

For anyone selling in the inner east in 2033, the disclosure framework is not a technicality. It is a core part of the transaction.
