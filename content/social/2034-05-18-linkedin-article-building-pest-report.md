## Caption

Most buyers read the summary page first. That is the wrong starting point.

The summary is a brief overview that groups findings into categories. It tells you that defects were found. It does not tell you which defects matter, which ones are routine maintenance, and which ones require immediate action or specialist assessment.

The section that matters is the active defects section. Active timber pest activity and significant structural findings are categorically different from maintenance items and minor defects. A long list of minor items is normal for any Queensland home built before 2000. Active termite activity is a different conversation entirely.

The practical test: before reading the summary, find the sections on active timber pest activity and structural integrity. Assess those first. If both are clear, the remaining items are maintenance decisions rather than deal-breakers. If either flagged an active issue, that finding needs to be understood fully before the contract position is reassessed.

Inspectors write reports to be comprehensive. Buyers often read them as if comprehensive means alarming. The skill is in separating the items that require a response from those that are simply disclosure of the building's current condition.
