## Caption

Off-market means limiting buyer competition. Limiting competition almost always affects price. The argument for off-market is rarely about the vendor's interests.

That is the sentence most agents don't say out loud, and it is worth understanding before you agree to sell your home this way.

Here is the mechanics of it. A full market campaign, with professional photography, portal listings, signage, and open homes, exposes your property to the widest possible pool of buyers. Competition between buyers, when it exists, is what creates price tension at auction or at the negotiating table. Price tension is how vendors achieve results above reserve or above asking.

An off-market sale short-circuits this process. You are selling to a subset of buyers: those the agent happens to know who are looking for something like your property. That can work. Occasionally it produces an excellent result, particularly when the agent has deep local knowledge and a specific buyer lined up. But the vendor has no way of knowing whether the price achieved was the best available price, because they never tested the market.

There are genuine reasons to go off-market. Privacy concerns for high-profile vendors. A specific buyer in mind who the vendor knows will pay well. A property with unusual characteristics that doesn't suit a broad campaign. These are real scenarios.

But if an agent suggests off-market as the first option without a clear rationale, the right question is: whose interests does this actually serve?
