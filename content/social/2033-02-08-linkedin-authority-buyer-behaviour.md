## Caption

Reading a room at an open home is a skill that takes time to develop. Not everyone who attends is a buyer. Not every buyer who asks questions is a qualified one. The signals that matter are subtler than group size.

What tells me a buyer is serious: they come back for a second inspection within four days, they bring someone new (a partner, a parent, a builder), and they ask about the settlement date and whether the vendors are flexible. Those questions are not idle curiosity. They are someone working out the logistics of a purchase.

What tells me a buyer is not yet ready: they compare the property favourably to one three streets away, ask about price but do not ask about contract conditions, and leave without asking for the contract or section 36 certificate. They are gathering information, not making a decision.

In February's inner east market, the ratio of serious to curious visitors at open homes gives me a real-time read on how the season is tracking. ⚠️ VERIFY Right now in suburbs like Norman Park and Camp Hill, the proportion of buyers asking contract-level questions in the first week is a reasonable indicator of where offers will land by week three.

Sellers watching their agent's open home numbers should be asking a different question: not how many people came through, but what conversations happened while they were there.
