## Caption

Days on market is one of those figures that appears in every market summary and gets treated as a general health indicator. A low number means the market is good. A high number means it is soft. That reading is too simple to be useful.

In the inner east right now, the days on market figure for well-presented, correctly priced properties is short. Those properties are clearing inside the first campaign week in many cases, often before or at auction with competitive bidding. That number tells you something real about buyer depth and price alignment.

The properties sitting on the market for six weeks in the same suburbs are not telling you the market is soft. They are telling you something specific about those listings: a pricing expectation that buyers have declined to meet, a presentation standard that is reducing the pool of interested parties, or both. The suburb does not explain the extended campaign. The property and the strategy do.

This distinction is important for sellers approaching the autumn market. Your comparable days on market figure is not the suburb average. It is determined by how your property is priced, how it is presented, and how the campaign is run. Sellers who treat those three elements as separate from the result they are hoping to achieve often end up providing the data point for the next agent's "extended campaigns" conversation.

A fast, clean result in autumn 2032 is available to the right properties. Understanding what makes a property the right one is the starting point.
