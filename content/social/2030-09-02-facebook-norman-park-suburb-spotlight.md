## Caption

Norman Park sits on one of the most protected ridges in the inner east, bookended by Whites Hill Reserve to the south and the Brisbane River corridor to the north. That geography is not incidental: it shapes the street network, limits infill development, and keeps listing volumes consistently low. Buyers who have set their sights on Norman Park often spend six months or more monitoring the market before the right home appears.

The buyer profile here is predominantly families who have done their research. Brisbane State High School catchment is the anchor, but the local Norman Park State School and the proximity to Seven Hills bushland add texture to what is already a compelling argument for the suburb. They are not impulse buyers, and they tend to compete hard when a well-presented home arrives.

Elevated Queenslanders on streets like Bennetts Road and Harts Road draw the sharpest competition. Post-war homes on larger blocks attract a second tier of buyers who want the address and the land without the renovation scope of a character home. Both categories move quickly when priced correctly, and the supply side rarely gives buyers the relief they are looking for.

If you own in Norman Park and have been weighing the idea of selling, current conditions reward quality presentation and precise pricing. Stock shortages favour sellers who take the time to get it right.
