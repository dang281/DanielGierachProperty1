## Caption

The price a seller puts on a property is a signal, not just a number. Buyers read that signal very carefully, and what they read is not always what the seller intended to say.

Setting a price above what comparable sales support does not protect a seller's position. It undermines it. Buyers who are active in a market and tracking comparable sales recognise an overpriced property immediately. They draw one of two conclusions: either the seller is unrealistic about what their property is worth, or there is something about the property that explains the gap. Neither conclusion generates the kind of interest that leads to strong offers.

The buyers who would pay the most for a property are generally the buyers who know the market best. They have done the research. They have been to the open homes. They are not going to overpay for a property relative to what the evidence says it is worth, and a listing price that contradicts the evidence does not persuade them otherwise. It tells them the campaign is not worth attending seriously.

A well-priced property, by contrast, signals confidence. It tells buyers that the seller understands the market and is ready to transact. That signal attracts the buyers who have been waiting for a property like this at a price that reflects reality. Those are exactly the buyers you want competing on your property.

Pricing is a strategic decision, not an emotional one. Getting it right from the start is far more effective than starting high and adjusting later. danielgierach.com
