## Caption

Every year, the same pattern plays out. In the weeks leading up to June 30, investment property owners and potential upgraders are focused on tax returns, depreciation schedules, and conversations with their accountant. The question of selling gets shelved until the financial year is settled.

Then July arrives, and the decisions start.

This is not a theory. It is something I have seen consistently across every mid-year cycle I have worked through in the inner east. The vendors who call in late July and August are often people who have been thinking about selling for six months but needed EOFY to pass before they could assess their position clearly. Now they know what the property earned, what it cost, and what their accountant recommends. The decision to sell, or not to sell, becomes much cleaner once those numbers are on the table.

For investment property owners in Bulimba, Hawthorne, and Norman Park, the July reassessment often confirms what they already suspected: the yield is not what it was relative to the equity they have accumulated, and a sale makes financial sense. For upgraders, EOFY simply removes the distraction. Their borrowing capacity has not changed. Their desire to move has not changed. The financial year settling just frees up the mental bandwidth to act.

If you have been putting off a conversation about selling until you felt ready, the end of the financial year is often the trigger that clears the path. That conversation is worth having now, not when the spring rush begins.
