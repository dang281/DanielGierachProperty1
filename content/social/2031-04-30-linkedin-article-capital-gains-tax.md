## Caption

The main residence exemption from capital gains tax is not automatic if the property has ever been rented. Most vendors assume full exemption. Partial CGT liability surprises more inner east sellers than it should.

The exemption applies in full when a property has been the owner's principal place of residence for the entire period of ownership, and has never been used to produce income. The moment a property has been rented for any part of the ownership period, the exemption becomes proportional. The CGT liability is calculated based on the fraction of time the property was not the principal place of residence, applied to the capital gain made over the full ownership period. On an inner east property that has appreciated significantly, that proportional liability can be a substantial figure.

The six-year absence rule allows owners to treat a property as their principal place of residence for up to six years while it is rented, but the conditions attached to that rule are specific. It applies from the time the owner last lived in the property. It does not stack with another property being treated as the principal place of residence at the same time. Investors who have moved into a second property, claimed that as their residence, and assumed the six-year rule still protects the original property may find the interaction between the two rules removes the exemption entirely.

EOFY is approaching and investors considering selling before 30 June need to understand their CGT position well before settlement, not at tax time the following year.

The article covers how the main residence exemption works, the proportional calculation, and when to seek advice from a tax professional before listing.
