## Caption

Off-market sales in Brisbane's inner east are presented to homeowners as an opportunity: a buyer ready to go, no campaign, no weekends of open homes, no marketing cost.

What is not said as clearly is what the vendor gives up.

A well-run property campaign creates competition. Competition is what produces prices above what any single buyer, acting alone, is willing to pay without pressure. When you accept an off-market offer, you are replacing the competitive process with a bilateral negotiation. The buyer making the off-market approach knows there is no competition. Their behaviour reflects that knowledge.

The "buyer ready to go" framing is often accurate. The buyer is ready. What is unclear is whether they are ready to pay what the market would deliver if the property were properly exposed. In the inner east, the gap between an off-market sale and a well-run campaign result regularly runs to $50,000 or more on a $1.2 million property. Sometimes considerably more.

Off-market sales do serve a legitimate purpose in specific circumstances: privacy is a genuine requirement, the vendor has a strong relationship with the buyer, or the timeline constraints are real. But those circumstances are less common than the frequency of off-market approaches suggests.

If an agent contacts you with an off-market buyer, the question to ask is: what evidence do you have that this price reflects current market value? If the answer is the buyer's offer itself, that is not evidence. That is the buyer's floor, not the market ceiling.
