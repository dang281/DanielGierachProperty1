## Caption

The summary page of a building and pest report is the wrong place to start reading.

Most reports open with a summary that rates the property as acceptable, satisfactory, or requiring attention. That summary is a general opinion. What matters is what is underneath it.

The section to look at first is active defects: anything currently causing damage, or currently at risk of causing damage if left unaddressed. Active timber pest activity, rising damp, structural movement in the subfloor or roof frame, faulty drainage that is already causing issues. These are different from general maintenance items, which every older property will have and which most buyers can manage over time.

The distinction is important because sellers and buyers often negotiate based on the existence of a report finding rather than the actual significance of it. A general maintenance note about aging gutters is not the same as active termite workings in the subfloor. Reading the report carefully, and ideally calling the inspector to talk through any items that are unclear, is worth the 20 minutes it takes.

For buyers in Brisbane's inner east where most properties are 30 to 70 years old, a building and pest report is not a reason to pull out of a contract. It is a tool to make sure you are buying the property you thought you were buying.
