## Caption

Queensland land tax is calculated on the unimproved value of your taxable land holdings, aggregated across all properties you own in Queensland. Your primary residence is exempt. Everything else is potentially taxable.

The threshold applies to the combined unimproved value across your portfolio, not to individual properties. An investor with two inner east properties, each valued at an amount below the individual threshold, may find that combined they are well above it.

What has changed for inner east investors in recent years is the underlying land value growth. Properties in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Morningside, and Norman Park have seen significant land value increases. An investor who bought a decade ago and calculated their land tax position at that time may now be paying substantially more without having changed anything about their portfolio.

The land valuation used for land tax purposes comes from the Queensland Valuer-General and is updated annually. The assessed value is not the market value. The two can diverge significantly in a rising market.

Land tax is assessed on 30 June each year. The liability is based on your holding on that date. If you are considering selling an investment property and want to understand whether doing so before or after 30 June affects your land tax position for the following year, that is worth confirming with your accountant before you act.

The full article explains how the calculation works, who is liable, and what to check if you hold property in the inner east.
