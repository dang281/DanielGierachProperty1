## Caption

Off-market sales are common in Brisbane's inner east. An agent brings a buyer to a homeowner before the property is listed. No open homes, no marketing campaign, no advertising costs. The appeal is obvious: speed, privacy, and the absence of process. The cost is less visible.

What off-market sales almost always limit is buyer competition. A property sold to one buyer before any marketing has not tested what the market would have paid. The buyer who is presented off-market has a significant advantage: they know there is no competing offer, no auction pressure, and no timeline pressure from a competing buyer in the wings. The vendor is negotiating from a weaker position than a well-run campaign would create.

The argument for off-market, that it saves marketing costs and disruption, rarely survives the arithmetic. Marketing costs on a well-run inner east campaign typically represent a small fraction of the price gap between an off-market result and a competitive campaign result. The disruption of a campaign, four to five weeks of open homes and conditional period, is real but finite.

Off-market makes sense in specific circumstances: estate sales with privacy requirements, vendors who are not ready to campaign, and situations where the buyer pool is genuinely narrow. Outside those circumstances, limiting exposure almost always limits price.
