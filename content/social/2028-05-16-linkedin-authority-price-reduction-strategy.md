## Caption

A price reduction seems like the obvious move when a campaign is slow. It is also frequently the wrong one.

Here is what actually happens when a price is reduced. The buyers who inspected the property in the first two or three weeks and passed on it receive an alert. A meaningful number of them do not come back at the new price and think, great, now it's affordable. They reassess from scratch. Their first question is not: is this property good value at the new price? It is: why has this property been reduced? What has the market seen that I missed?

A price reduction confirms what slow weeks had already suggested: that the market spoke, and it said no. The reduction is evidence of that verdict, not a relaunch of the campaign. Some buyers are re-engaged by it, but many of the buyers who were closest to making an offer have mentally moved on. They have inspected other properties in the weeks since. They may have found something else. They have recalibrated their idea of what this kind of money should buy.

The more effective response to a slow campaign is almost always a different diagnosis. Sometimes it is the presentation at the property itself, where small adjustments to styling or access times can shift buyer perception. Sometimes it is the photography, which in a slower campaign can often be improved. Sometimes it is the honest conversation with the vendor that did not happen in week one, about where the market actually sits.

A price reduction occasionally accelerates a stalled campaign. But it works best when it is the last option considered, not the first. danielgierach.com
