## Caption

With the Brisbane 2032 Olympics approximately one year away, the question I keep getting asked is how much the Games have actually moved inner east property prices.

The honest answer is: the effect is real, but it is almost impossible to isolate.

The infrastructure that came with the Olympic commitment has had a measurable impact. Cross River Rail opened up access across the inner east in ways that have changed the commute calculation for buyers who previously ruled suburbs out on travel time grounds. Stadium precinct upgrades, improved transport connections, and the broader international attention on Brisbane as a city have all contributed to the desirability of the inner east. These are not speculative effects. They have happened.

What is harder to separate out is how much of inner east price growth over the past four or five years is attributable to the Olympics specifically, versus population growth, interstate migration, sustained undersupply of inner east housing stock, and the general maturation of Brisbane as a market. All of those factors have been running simultaneously. A professional valuer could not cleanly isolate the Olympic contribution, and I am not going to pretend otherwise.

The speculative Olympic premium that buyers and media commentators discussed heavily in 2027 and 2028, the idea that you could buy ahead of the wave and ride an Olympics-specific uplift, has been absorbed into broader price movements. If it existed as a distinct layer, it is now part of the substrate.

What I can say honestly is that the inner east in July 2031 is a different market to the inner east in 2027 in ways that are partly attributable to the Olympic commitment and largely attributable to deeper structural factors. Buyers who purchased with a ten-year view have been well rewarded regardless of which factor you credit.
