## Caption

Days on market is one of the first numbers buyers and sellers reach for when assessing a property or a market. It is a useful number. But it is widely misread.

What days on market actually tells you is whether a property's asking price aligned with what the buyer pool was willing to pay, and whether the campaign execution attracted the right buyers at the right time. A high number usually indicates one of three things: the property was overpriced at launch, the marketing did not reach the qualified buyer pool, or the property had a presentation issue that deterred offers. These are all solvable problems, but they are different problems.

What days on market does not tell you is whether the eventual sale was a good result. A property that sits for 60 days and then sells strongly, having found the one buyer who specifically wanted that configuration, may have been a better campaign than one that sold in seven days at below what a more patient process would have achieved. Speed is not the same as outcome.

As a signal rather than a verdict, days on market is most useful when read across a suburb over a period of months. A rising average across similar properties indicates buyer hesitation, often from pricing pressure or broader market softness. A falling average indicates supply tightening and buyer confidence increasing. A single property's number tells you something, but the trend tells you more.

If you are assessing a campaign or researching before listing, ask what the days on market means for your specific property type, not just what the suburb average is sitting at.
