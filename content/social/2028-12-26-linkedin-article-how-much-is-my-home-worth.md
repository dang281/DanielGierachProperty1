## Caption

Two houses on the same street in Hawthorne can be $200,000 apart in price. The street is the same. The land size is similar. But one has a north-facing rear, has been renovated to a quality that photographers want to put in front of buyers, and sits on the higher ground that keeps it out of the overland flow path. The other has a south-facing rear, original kitchen and bathrooms, and a drainage issue that turns up in every building inspection.

Automated valuation tools cannot see any of that. They work from settlement data that is 60 to 120 days old, aggregated across all sales in the suburb. They cannot read aspect. They cannot assess renovation quality. They do not know whether your property is in the school catchment boundary or two streets outside it.

What automated estimates are useful for: getting a general sense of the suburb range, or checking whether a property you are considering buying is sitting within a normal price distribution. What they are not useful for: deciding what price to ask for your own home.

The value of a comparative market analysis done by someone who physically walks the property is in exactly this difference. The comparable sales in your street, adjusted for the specific variables that affect your property's position within the range, give you a number you can actually use in a negotiation.

The full article explains how residential property is actually valued, where automated tools fall short, and what to expect from a proper appraisal.
