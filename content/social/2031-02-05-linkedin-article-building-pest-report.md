## Caption

The summary page of a building and pest report is the wrong place to start reading, and most buyers start there.

The summary classifies findings into minor, moderate, and major risk categories. Reading from that page means making decisions based on a classification system rather than an understanding of what the inspector actually found. Two very different findings can sit in the same risk category. Active subfloor timber pest activity and a note about deteriorating weatherboard paint can both appear as moderate risk items, but they are not the same problem and they do not carry the same weight in a purchase decision.

Reading from the beginning of the report, working through the inspector's findings section by section, is what gives a buyer the information they actually need. The distinction between active timber pest activity and evidence of previous activity that has been treated is significant. The difference between a structural finding and a maintenance finding changes whether you are looking at a deal-breaker or a cost to factor into your post-settlement budget.

Older homes in the inner east almost always produce building reports with findings. A 1960s Queenslander in Bulimba or Hawthorne will have maintenance items. That is not a reason to walk away from the right property at the right price. It is a reason to read carefully, understand what you are buying, and price the work correctly.

The article covers the structure of a building and pest report, what constitutes a significant structural finding versus routine maintenance, and how to assess active pest activity versus historical evidence.
