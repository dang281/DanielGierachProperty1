## Caption

Sellers often accept a lower unconditional offer over a higher conditional one. To buyers, this looks irrational. To vendors who understand what a conditional contract actually means, it makes complete sense.

A conditional offer includes a finance clause, a building and pest clause, or both. Each one is a legal exit for the buyer. During the 2 to 3 week window those conditions are open, the property is effectively off the market. The listing goes from active to under contract, other buyers move on, and if the contract falls over the vendor relaunches into a pool that has already moved past their property once. A $20,000 to $30,000 gap between a conditional and an unconditional offer is often a rational trade for a vendor who wants certainty more than they want to risk the process starting again.

From the buyer's side, an unconditional offer requires genuine preparation. Finance pre-approval needs to be solid, not estimated. A pre-purchase building and pest inspection, done before the offer is submitted, removes the need for the clause entirely. Buyers who are ready to go unconditional are a different proposition to the agent and the vendor, and that changes the negotiation.

There is a right time to use a conditional offer and a right time to go unconditional. Understanding the difference, and what each costs and saves, is the kind of thing that matters when other buyers are also making offers.
