## Caption

The conventional wisdom on winter property is consistent: fewer buyers, lower results, wait for spring. It is not wrong exactly, but it is incomplete, and for a well-prepared seller it can lead to a decision that costs them.

Winter in Brisbane's inner east does produce a smaller pool of active buyers. That part is accurate. The part that gets left out is what kind of buyers remain. A buyer attending an open home on a cool Saturday morning in June is not doing it casually. They have a reason to be there. They have finance arranged, a timeline driving them, and enough genuine intent to get out of bed and go to inspections when most people would not bother. Casual buyers browse in spring. Serious buyers show up in winter.

The properties that perform well in winter are not seasonal flukes. They are, almost without exception, properties that would have sold well in any season: correctly priced against the current market, well-presented, properly prepared, with an agent who managed the campaign rather than simply listed the address. Winter does not rescue a poorly priced or underprepared property, but it does not penalise a good one.

The other variable worth noting is competition. The sellers who heard the conventional wisdom and chose to wait mean the supply side in June and July is thin. A well-positioned property in a quiet winter market is not competing against a dozen similar listings. It may be competing against two or three.

For a seller with a genuine reason to move, winter is a more viable window than its reputation suggests. danielgierach.com
