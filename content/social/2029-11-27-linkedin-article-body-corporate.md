## Caption

Most buyers looking at a unit or townhouse ask about the body corporate levy rate early in their due diligence. It's a reasonable question. What the levy costs each year is a real holding cost and it factors into how the property stacks up financially.

But the levy rate is not the most important number in the body corporate financials. The sinking fund balance is.

The sinking fund is what the body corporate uses to pay for major capital expenditure: roof repairs, external painting, lift servicing, common area upgrades, structural work. If that fund is well-maintained, the scheme can absorb those costs without imposing additional charges on owners. If it's depleted, a special levy or a levy increase is likely the moment anything significant needs attention. That cost arrives after settlement, sometimes within the first year or two of ownership.

A building with a levy of $3,000 per year and a healthy sinking fund is often a better financial proposition than a building with a levy of $2,200 per year and a fund that's been running on minimal contributions for five years.

Annual levy rates for units and townhouses in Brisbane vary considerably by building type. A small townhouse complex might run $2,000 to $4,000 per year in total levies. A mid-rise building with a pool, lift, and common facilities can be $6,000 to $14,000 or more. But the figure that tells you whether those levies are covering what they need to cover is the sinking fund, not the levy headline.
