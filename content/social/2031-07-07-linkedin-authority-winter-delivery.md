## Caption

The idea that winter kills the Brisbane property market is one of those beliefs that sounds reasonable until you actually look at what is happening at open homes in July.

What I observe in the inner east in winter is not a dead market. It is a different market. The buyer pool is smaller, yes. The Saturday open home queues are shorter than they are in October. But the buyers who show up in July have typically been searching for months. They have missed out on properties in autumn. They have made their suburb decision, their price decision, and often their product decision. They are not browsing. They are ready to act.

That changes the dynamic for a vendor considerably. In spring, a vendor might attract 40 groups through an open home, but a meaningful portion of those groups are early in their search. In July, 18 groups might walk through, and a higher proportion of them are genuine candidates.

Properties that launch well prepared in winter also benefit from something spring campaigns cannot replicate: the absence of competing listings. When a well-presented home hits the market in July on a quiet street in Norman Park or Hawthorne and there are two or three comparable listings in the suburb rather than twelve, the buyer's attention is concentrated in a way that does not happen when spring supply builds.

The vendors who achieve strong winter results in the inner east are not lucky. They are prepared, realistically priced, and they are meeting a buyer pool that has been waiting for them.

The myth that winter is dead does not survive contact with the actual pattern I see every year through July.
