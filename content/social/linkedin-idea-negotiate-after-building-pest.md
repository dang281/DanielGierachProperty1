## Caption

A building and pest report is not a price renegotiation document by default. Whether it gives a buyer genuine grounds to negotiate depends on what the report actually says.

Building and pest reports have two categories of findings that matter. Active defects are things that are broken, failing, or actively deteriorating now. General maintenance items are things that are worn, aged, or due for attention in the normal course of ownership. These two categories require completely different responses.

Active defects, particularly active timber pest activity, significant structural movement, or a failing roof, are material findings that affect the value of the property. A buyer who discovers an active termite issue after signing has found something that was not apparent at inspection and that affects the price they agreed to pay. That is a reasonable basis for discussion.

General maintenance items, aged paint, minor weathering, small cracks in render, worn gutters, are features of every property of a certain age. Using them to renegotiate price on a 1950s Queenslander in Hawthorne is not a negotiation strategy, it is a reason for the vendor to reopen the campaign to the next buyer.

The buyers who negotiate well after a report read it carefully, identify the genuine active issues, quantify what remediation would cost from a licensed tradesperson, and present a specific, reasonable adjustment to the vendor. The buyers who lose properties use the report as a generic lever and get told no.
