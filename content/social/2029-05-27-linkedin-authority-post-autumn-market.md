## Caption

Once the main autumn campaign window closes, the inner east market looks different. Volume drops, buyer competition eases, and the properties that remain on market tend to fall into two categories.

The first is overpriced homes from the autumn campaign that did not sell because the vendor held a number the market would not meet. These properties often come back to market in winter with a price adjustment and a more motivated vendor. For buyers who inspect systematically, they occasionally represent the best value of the year.

The second category is genuine winter listings. Vendors who list in June and July have usually done so for a specific reason: relocation, a changed personal situation, or a property that has become available at a time outside the peak campaign seasons. These vendors are typically realistic about pricing and timeline.

The conventional wisdom about winter being a slow market is partially true for buyer volumes. It is less true for buyer seriousness. The buyers who are out inspecting in June are not browsers. They have been in the market for months and are ready to act when the right property appears.

For buyers who found autumn too competitive, winter offers a different dynamic. Less competition, more motivated vendors, and more room for a measured inspection and due diligence process. Not every winter campaign produces a bargain, but the environment is almost always less frenzied than April or May.
