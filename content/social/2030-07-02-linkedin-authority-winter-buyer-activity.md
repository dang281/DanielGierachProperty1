## Caption

There is a version of the winter slow season that is real, and a version that is mostly commentary.

The real part: fewer listings go to market in July. Open home visitor numbers drop. The auction calendar thins out. If you measure activity by volume alone, winter looks quiet.

What that framing misses is the composition of the buyers who do show up.

The buyer who attends three open homes on a Saturday in July has already done the research. They have a pre-approval in place, they know the suburb, they have probably missed out on something earlier in the year and are not interested in missing out again. They are not browsers. The buyer who needs to see eighteen properties before they can form a view does not tend to do that across the winter school holidays.

What this means practically is that the open home to offer conversion rate in winter is often higher than the raw attendance numbers suggest. Fewer people through the door, but a higher proportion of those people are in a position to act.

Vendors who are holding off for spring are making a calculation about competition. More buyers in October is real. But more buyers also means more casual inspectors, more first-timers who are still learning what they want, and more weekends before an offer lands. Spring is not guaranteed to produce better results, just louder ones.

The right time to sell is when your property is prepared, your pricing is accurate, and your agent understands who is actually buying right now. In the inner east this July, the buyers at open homes are not wasting anyone's time.
