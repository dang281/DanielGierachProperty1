## Caption

On a $1 million owner-occupier purchase in Queensland, transfer duty adds approximately $38,025 to the cost of buying. That number is not in most buyers' heads when they are calculating their deposit and working out their LVR.

The practical problem is that transfer duty is not equity. It does not sit on the asset side of a personal balance sheet. It goes out the door on settlement day and does not come back. A buyer who has saved $200,000 and assumes they have a 20 per cent deposit on a $1 million property actually has approximately $162,000 available for deposit once duty, legal fees, and other acquisition costs are subtracted. That changes the LVR calculation, the lender's assessment, and potentially whether lenders mortgage insurance becomes a factor.

The figure shifts depending on buyer type. Owner-occupiers pay at a different rate to investors. First home buyers have access to concessions that reduce or eliminate the liability depending on the purchase price. The threshold for full first home buyer concession in Queensland has moved over the years and buyers who rely on advice from a friend who bought two years ago may be working from an outdated number.

There is also a timing issue. Transfer duty is assessed at settlement. Buyers who have not factored it into their savings plan sometimes find themselves bridging a gap in the weeks before settlement, which creates pressure at the worst possible moment in the transaction.
