## Caption

Auction clearance rates are reported every week, cited in every market commentary, and rarely explained in a way that is actually useful.

The headline number counts properties sold under the hammer, or unconditionally sold prior, divided by the total number of properties that went to auction. What it does not capture is properties that were passed in and then sold privately in the days following the auction. Some of these post-auction sales happen at or above reserve. They are genuine sales that simply did not close on auction day. Depending on how the data is collected, a significant portion of passed-in properties that subsequently transact within a week are excluded from the clearance rate entirely, which means the headline understates actual selling activity.

It also does not tell you the quality of the result. A property that clears at auction could have sold for a strong premium with four registered bidders, or it could have sold to the only person in the room at the vendor bid price. Both count the same in the clearance rate.

What clearance rates are genuinely useful for is trend reading, not point-in-time verdicts. A rate that has been rising over four to six weeks tells you buyer confidence is increasing and sellers are pricing realistically. A rate that has been falling for the same period indicates buyer hesitation or vendor pricing that is ahead of the market. The trend is the signal. The individual weekly number is mostly noise.

In the inner east, clearance rates need to be read alongside the volume of auctions scheduled, which fluctuates significantly through the year and affects how the rate is calculated.
