## Caption

Heritage overlays in Brisbane's inner east are one of the most misunderstood elements of a property title, particularly for buyers new to the character home market.

The key thing to understand: a heritage overlay does not prevent renovation. It requires Brisbane City Council approval for changes to the external character of the property, particularly street-facing changes. Internal renovations typically proceed without any heritage approval requirement.

In practice, this means a Queenslander in Norman Park or a character home in Hawthorne with a heritage overlay can still be extended, updated, and modernised. The constraint is on what the street-facing facade looks like, not on what happens inside. A modern open-plan interior behind an intact Queenslander facade is the most common outcome, and it is what the overlay is designed to produce.

For sellers, the overlay is rarely a negative. Inner east buyers who are specifically looking for character homes typically respond well to properties with heritage designation. It signals that the streetscape has protection, which contributes to the character of the neighbourhood they are buying into.

The situations where it creates complications: buyers who plan significant structural changes, demolition of part of the structure, or changes that would alter the street-facing character. These require detailed assessment before purchase.
