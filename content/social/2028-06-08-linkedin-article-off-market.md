## Caption

When an agent contacts a homeowner on behalf of a buyer and says "we have someone ready to move quickly," that is a convenience offer. And convenience, in a property transaction, benefits the buyer, not the vendor.

An off-market sale limits buyer competition. Limiting competition almost always affects the final price. A properly run campaign creates urgency among multiple qualified buyers. That urgency is the mechanism that pushes a sale price above what any single buyer would have offered if they had no competition at all. When a vendor sells off-market, they are typically removing that mechanism before they have ever tested what the market would actually pay.

This is not speculation. It is how price discovery works. The homeowner who accepts an off-market approach may receive a strong offer. They rarely know whether it was the best one available.

That said, there are genuine circumstances where an off-market sale makes sense for a vendor: significant time pressure, a strong desire for privacy, or a specific situation where a campaign is impractical. In those cases the trade-off is real but understood.

The more common scenario is a homeowner who receives an unsolicited approach, finds it flattering or convenient, and sells without knowing what they gave up. Knowing how to respond to an off-market approach, and what questions to ask before you agree to anything, is practical knowledge worth having.
