## Caption

Most sellers think about pre-sale renovation as a return calculation. Spend $40K on a kitchen. Get $60K back in the sale price. A simple equation.

It rarely works that way.

What renovation actually does is keep a property in contention. A dated kitchen does not lower your price by $40K. It narrows your buyer pool, because some buyers price the inconvenience of doing it themselves rather than the actual cost. You lose the buyers who cannot be bothered, not just the ones who cannot afford it.

The properties I see sellers over-invest in are usually the ones where the renovation is visible and expensive but does not address what buyers are actually reacting to. Bathrooms that cost $30K but the first thing buyers notice is the dark hallway and the low ceilings. Floors that cost $20K in a house that needs a roof.

Before spending anything, the useful question is: what is the one thing holding this property back from competing with the best listings in the street?

Sometimes the answer is a $3K repaint and a skip bin. Sometimes it is nothing - the property presents well and the money is better kept in your pocket.

Good preparation and genuine renovation are different things. Knowing which one applies to your property is worth a conversation before you start spending.
