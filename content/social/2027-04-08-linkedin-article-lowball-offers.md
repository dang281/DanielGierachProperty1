## Caption

Receiving an offer well below your expectations is one of the more emotionally loaded moments in a property campaign. The instinct is to feel insulted and reject it immediately.

That reaction is understandable. It is also almost never the right move.

A low offer means a buyer has raised their hand, engaged with your property, and put a number on paper. That is more than most people who walked through your open home will ever do. The useful question is not whether the number is low, it is what the offer is telling you and how to respond without losing the buyer or your negotiating position.

In Brisbane's inner east, offers more than 8 to 10 per cent below asking price are generally considered low. But whether an offer is genuinely low or simply realistic depends on how the property is priced relative to comparable recent sales. An offer that looks low on an overpriced listing may actually reflect fair market value. That is a different problem.

Rejecting a low offer outright removes your ability to negotiate and rarely improves your outcome. Countering clearly, at a price that reflects your position, keeps the conversation open. Most negotiated results in a private treaty campaign go through at least one low offer before reaching agreement. Knowing that in advance changes how you respond in the moment.

The full article covers when to hold firm, when to counter, and what an early low offer is actually signalling about your campaign. danielgierach.com/insights/handling-lowball-offers-selling-brisbane
