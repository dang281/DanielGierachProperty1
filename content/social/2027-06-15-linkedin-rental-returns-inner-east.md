## Caption

There is a version of the inner east investment property conversation that focuses entirely on the weekly rent figure. And then there is the version that accounts for what you actually keep after costs. Those two conversations can arrive at very different conclusions about the same property.

Gross yield in the inner east at mid-2027 looks reasonable on paper for houses and less compelling for townhouses, once you factor in purchase price. The issue is that gross yield is not what lands in your account. The gap between gross and net yield is wider here than in most Brisbane suburbs, and it is wider for a specific set of reasons.

Townhouses in the inner east typically come with body corporate fees. Those fees vary considerably by complex, but anything in a pool or lift serviced building will add to costs in a way that does not show up in a rental appraisal. Council rates on inner east houses are not trivial. Insurance on older character homes in flood-adjacent areas carries a premium that surprises a lot of first-time landlords. Property management fees in the inner east sit in a fairly standard range, but they compound on top of everything else.

The investors I see managing their inner east properties well are the ones who built their cashflow model around net figures from the start. They budgeted for vacancy, for maintenance, and for the periodic capital expenditure that older properties require. They were not surprised in year two because they planned for year two before they bought.

If you are considering an inner east investment property and you want a straight read on what the actual numbers might look like for a specific property type or suburb, that is a conversation I am happy to have.
