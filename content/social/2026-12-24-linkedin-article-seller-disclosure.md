## Caption

Under the Property Law Act 2023 (Qld), Queensland sellers are now required to provide a completed Seller Disclosure Statement to a buyer before any contract is entered into.

This changed the previous regime, under which disclosure obligations were more limited and largely embedded in the contract process itself. The new framework requires the seller to actively disclose known material facts upfront, before the buyer signs.

The practical consequences: a buyer who does not receive the disclosure statement before signing a contract has a right to terminate, at any time before settlement. That is a significant exposure for a vendor. A campaign that moves quickly, with an offer accepted before the disclosure document has been prepared and provided, creates unnecessary legal risk.

The disclosure statement itself requires the seller to confirm a range of matters, including whether there are any adverse entries on title, any pending orders or notices affecting the property, any relevant body corporate information for lots in a community title scheme, and various other matters prescribed under the Act.

For sellers in Brisbane's inner east who are planning to list in early 2027, getting the disclosure statement prepared as part of pre-campaign preparation is not optional. It is a legal requirement that protects both parties.

Your conveyancer or solicitor prepares this document. It should be part of your pre-listing checklist.
