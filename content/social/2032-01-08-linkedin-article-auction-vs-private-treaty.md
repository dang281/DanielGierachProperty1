## Caption

The method of sale should follow the property and the buyer pool, not the seller's preference and not what the neighbours used last spring. Getting this decision wrong is one of the more expensive mistakes a vendor can make.

Auction works when you have a broad pool of competitive buyers who will all value the same property in roughly the same range. It creates urgency, produces an unconditional result on the day, and lets the market set the price rather than the agent. The conditions that make auction work are: strong demand, a relatively liquid comparable sales environment, and a property that photographs and presents well to a general audience.

When those conditions are not present, auction becomes a public price negotiation where you have already shown your timeline. A passed-in auction without a strong bidding floor is harder to recover from than a property that never went to auction in the first place.

Private treaty suits vendor-controlled timelines and properties where buyer motivation is more varied. You can negotiate, you can accept subject-to-finance offers from buyers who would not bid at auction, and you are not committed to a campaign end date.

Expression of Interest sits between the two. It suits genuinely unique properties where the buyer value range is wide and competitive tension is useful, but the bidding format of auction does not fit the buyer pool. Development sites, prestige riverfront properties, and homes with unusual configurations often perform well by EOI.

The article at the link covers how to work through this decision with your agent before you commit to a campaign strategy.
