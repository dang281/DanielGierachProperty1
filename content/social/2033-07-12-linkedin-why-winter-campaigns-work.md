## Caption

The assumption that spring is always the better time to sell does not hold up uniformly across Brisbane's inner east. It holds for some properties in some streets in some suburbs. For others, the inverse is closer to the truth.

The properties that tend to outperform winter expectations share a few characteristics. They have demand drivers that are not seasonal. School catchment properties in Camp Hill, Norman Park, and Coorparoo attract buyers who are making enrolment decisions on a school-year calendar, not a property-market calendar. Those buyers are often in the market in June and July precisely because they need a settled address before the following February. Competing against four other properties of the same type in spring is not necessarily better than selling to one motivated buyer in July.

Unique properties also often benefit from a thinner listing pool. A character home in Hawthorne or Balmoral that would be one of twelve in a spring market becomes one of two or three in a winter market. Scarcity creates its own form of competition.

What the winter market does not forgive is underprepared properties. The buyers active in winter have fewer alternatives, so they scrutinise more carefully. A property that might pass through a busy spring campaign without close inspection meets a more discerning buyer pool in July.
