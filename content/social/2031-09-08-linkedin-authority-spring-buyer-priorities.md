## Caption

A few things come up in nearly every buyer conversation I have at inner east open homes this spring, and they are consistent enough to be worth sharing with vendors preparing to launch.

School catchment remains the most consistent priority for the family buyer segment and has not shifted across several market cycles. A property in a catchment that families want will always attract more competition from that cohort than an equivalent property one street outside it. That affects both price expectation and the buyer profile the campaign is likely to draw.

Floor area and renovation quality run closely together, particularly in the $1.2 million to $2 million range where buyers are often comparing a move-in-ready property against a period home that needs work. A buyer who has done several renovations may see the opportunity clearly; one who has not needs to feel confident about what they are walking into. Presentation that makes the renovation readable rather than uncertain changes that calculation.

Street appeal is the deciding factor at the open home attendance stage, before the buyer has stepped inside. Buyers who have shortlisted three properties in a suburb make a final attendance decision partly on what the front of the property looks like from the car on the way past. Vendors who invest in the garden, the paint at the front, and the entry often see it in their attendance numbers.

What changes across cycles is the flexibility buyers show when supply is tight. When comparable listings are scarce, buyers often accept one item from their list that is not quite right, if the rest is strong. Vendors who know what their property does well and present it clearly have an advantage over those who try to obscure what it does not.
