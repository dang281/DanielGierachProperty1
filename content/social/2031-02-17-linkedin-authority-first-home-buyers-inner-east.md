## Caption

The entry price point for first-home buyers in Brisbane's inner east has shifted considerably over the past decade. That is not a complaint or a prediction, it is a straightforward observation from the sales evidence. What first-home buyers can compete for in the inner east in 2031 is different from what they could buy in the same suburbs ten years ago, and understanding that clearly is more useful than either dismissing the market or catastrophising it.

What first-home buyers are realistically purchasing in the inner east now tends to fall into a few categories. Older units and townhouses in Coorparoo, Balmoral, and Cannon Hill, particularly those in smaller complexes with manageable body corporate fees. Post-war cottages on smaller blocks that require work, where the buyer is buying the land and the location rather than a finished product. Occasionally, a house in the outer edge of the inner east where prices have not moved as sharply as the established streets closer to the river.

What they are largely priced out of is the renovated Queenslander on a full block in Bulimba or Hawthorne, the family home in a catchment suburb, and most of the Camp Hill and Coorparoo detached market at the upper end. That is the reality of where those prices sit now.

This is not unusual in the history of inner Brisbane. First-home buyers have always bought what was accessible and held it as the market moved. The suburbs they are buying into now will likely follow the same pattern. The entry price is higher, but the logic of inner east Brisbane holding its value over time has not fundamentally changed.
