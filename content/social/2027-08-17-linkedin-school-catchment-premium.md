## Caption

School catchment boundaries in Brisbane's inner east are not a soft consideration. They show up consistently in comparable sales, and they show up materially.

Buyers with school-age children frequently narrow their search to one or two specific catchments before they even think about suburb. Whites Hill State College, Bulimba State School, and Coorparoo State School each draw buyers who have already decided the catchment is non-negotiable. For those buyers, a property inside the boundary is a different product from one a street away that is not.

Two properties that are otherwise nearly identical, similar size, similar condition, similar land area, can sit on different sides of a catchment boundary and be priced differently in a way that is consistent and repeatable across comparable sales. That is not a premium created by individual buyer sentiment. It is a structural feature of that segment of the market.

What I notice is that catchment-boundary awareness is often underused by vendors when they are setting price expectations. They are comparing their property to others in the suburb without isolating whether those comparables are inside or outside the relevant catchment. That distinction matters, and getting it right means either capturing a premium you have earned, or not leaving money on the table because the analysis was too broad.

The catchment premium has held through the rate rise cycle. It is not driven by market conditions. It is driven by demographic demand that does not move with monetary policy.

If your property sits inside one of these catchments, that is a positioning conversation worth having before you go to market.
