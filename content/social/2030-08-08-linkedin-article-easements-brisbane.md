## Caption

The title search will tell you an easement exists on the property. What it will not immediately tell you is what kind, where it sits, and what it means for how you use the land.

A drainage easement running along the rear boundary of a standard inner east block is usually a practical non-issue for daily life. Council retains rights to access and maintain the drainage infrastructure, but the land above it is yours to use within those constraints. Many buyers purchase properties with drainage easements and barely notice them. An access easement is a materially different situation. This type of easement may allow an adjoining owner or a utility provider to cross your land. The path it follows, the permitted hours, and the scope of that right all matter significantly.

Both of these appear in a title search as "easement." The distinction is not always obvious at a glance, and this is where buyers get into trouble. They either dismiss all easements as minor, or they panic at the word and walk away from a property that had no practical impact on their intended use.

When your solicitor flags an easement during due diligence, the right questions to ask are: What type of easement is it? Who holds the benefit? What rights does it grant, and over which part of the land? Does it affect any existing or planned structures? Your solicitor can answer all of these from the registered easement document, which goes further than the title search summary.

The full article covers the most common easement types in Brisbane, how to read them, and what to watch for in the inner east specifically.
