## Caption

Land tax is assessed on the combined unimproved land value across all Queensland holdings. Rising inner east land values are pushing investors over the threshold without them realising it until the assessment arrives.

Here is how it happens. An investor bought in Morningside or Camp Hill five years ago when the unimproved land value was well below the land tax threshold. They might also hold a block in a regional Queensland town. Neither holding individually triggered the obligation. But the combined value now exceeds the threshold, because inner east land values have moved substantially. The assessment comes. It is not small.

The issue is compounded for investors who hold multiple Queensland properties. Each property doesn't carry its own separate threshold. The threshold applies to the aggregate unimproved value across all holdings. An investor with two inner east properties and one on the Sunshine Coast can find themselves well into assessable territory when they never expected to be.

For investors who are now assessing whether to sell a Queensland property, this calculation needs to be part of the decision. In some cases, realising the capital gain and exiting a holding makes more financial sense than continuing to hold and absorb a growing annual land tax liability. The answer depends on each investor's portfolio position, and their accountant is the right person to model it.

What I've seen is that many investors aren't having that conversation because they haven't yet received the assessment. The article covers how land tax is calculated, what the thresholds are, and the questions worth asking before the next assessment date.
