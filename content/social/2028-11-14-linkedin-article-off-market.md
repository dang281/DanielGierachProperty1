## Caption

Off-market property sales in Brisbane's inner east happen more often than they appear in the public data, precisely because they do not appear in the public data.

The pitch to vendors is usually framed as a benefit: no inconvenience of open homes, no marketing costs, a fast result. What it is not framed as is the cost of limiting buyer competition.

Competition between buyers is what drives price above the floor. When two buyers want the same property and neither is willing to lose it, the negotiation tends to find a level that neither would have reached on their own. Off-market removes that dynamic entirely. The vendor is negotiating with one buyer who knows they are the only buyer.

There are genuine reasons to sell off-market. A tenanted property with a difficult tenant. A vendor who genuinely needs speed and certainty above price. A property type with a very small buyer pool where a targeted approach makes sense. These are real exceptions.

What the article addresses is the assumption that "we have a buyer ready to go" is automatically a good outcome for the vendor. The buyer who is ready to go off-market is often the buyer who is most informed about the property's value, most confident that they can acquire it at below-market price, and least likely to be tested by competition.
