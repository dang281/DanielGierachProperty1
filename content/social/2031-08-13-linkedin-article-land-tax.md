## Caption

Land tax in Queensland is assessed each year on 30 June. The tax is calculated on the total unimproved value of all taxable land across Queensland holdings, not on the value of each property individually. As inner east Brisbane land values have risen over the past several years, investors who crossed the applicable threshold without actively tracking their land value position are now receiving assessments they did not budget for.

This catches investors in two ways. The first is the cumulative threshold effect: an investor with two properties whose individual land values sit below the threshold but whose combined value exceeds it faces a liability that would not exist if only one property were held. The second is the reassessment effect: investors who purchased five or more years ago and have not revisited their land value since purchase may find the unimproved value has increased substantially, moving them into a higher rate band or over the threshold for the first time.

August is when these assessments arrive and when investors begin reassessing their portfolio positions in response. For some, the assessment prompts a genuine portfolio review. For others, it is the first moment they understand that land tax is calculated across their entire Queensland holding, not property by property.

Understanding the structure of the tax is the starting point for any sensible response to an assessment. The full article explains how Queensland land tax is calculated, what is and is not taxable, and the main exemptions that apply.
