## Caption

When vendors hear that average days on market in their suburb is 21 days, most of them hear something it does not actually say.

What low days on market tells you is that properties priced accurately at launch are finding buyers quickly. That is a pricing accuracy signal, not a market strength signal. These are different things, and conflating them is the reason some vendors walk into a campaign with unrealistic expectations.

A property priced 15 percent above where comparable sales evidence sits will not sell in 21 days. It will sit. Buyers will inspect it, use it to calibrate value on other properties, and eventually stop coming. After six or eight weeks the vendor either reduces the price or withdraws. Either way, the final result is almost always below where the property would have landed if it had been priced accurately from the beginning. The extended time on market itself becomes part of the story.

The more useful question to ask before a campaign is not "how fast does this suburb sell?" It is "does the comparable sales evidence actually support what you want?" If it does, a fast sale is a reasonable expectation. If it does not, a fast sale is not on offer regardless of what the suburb average says.

I say this to vendors at the start of every appraisal conversation because the alternative is finding out six weeks into a campaign that the number did not mean what they thought it meant. That conversation is harder to have later than it is to have now.
