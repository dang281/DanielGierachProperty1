## Caption

A lowball offer lands and the instinct is to reject it outright. In most cases, that instinct is the wrong move.

Rejecting an offer without a counter closes the negotiation entirely. The buyer has told you what they are willing to start at, and you have told them you are not interested in talking. They may walk away and not return. The gap between where they opened and where you need to land may have been closeable, but you will not find that out if the door shuts before negotiation begins.

In Brisbane's inner east, a first offer that comes in eight to ten per cent below the asking price is not unusual, particularly from buyers who attend multiple campaigns and understand that opening low is a standard negotiating position. That offer is not necessarily a reflection of what the buyer is prepared to pay. It is a test of how motivated the vendor is and whether there is room to move.

The correct response to a low but genuine offer is a counter. Make it clear that the offer is well below where you need to be, name the gap honestly, and keep the conversation alive. What you learn from the buyer's response to your counter is more valuable than what their opening offer told you.
