## Caption

One of the most common misunderstandings I encounter with vendors is the assumption that the cooling off period is something they can negotiate away. It is not. In Queensland, the five-business-day cooling off period is a statutory right that belongs to the buyer. The seller cannot waive it. The seller cannot exclude it from the contract. It exists regardless of what the contract says.

What sellers can understand is what it costs a buyer to use it. Exercising the cooling off right means forfeiting 0.25 per cent of the purchase price. On a $1.2 million sale, that is $3,000. That is the buyer's penalty for changing their mind, and it is the only recourse available to the seller if the buyer exits within the cooling off window.

The important exception is auction. Contracts signed on the day of auction, or within three business days after, do not carry a cooling off period. Unconditional bidders at auction cannot change their mind the following morning. That distinction is one of the structural advantages of the auction method that sellers sometimes overlook when they are weighing up how to run their campaign.
