## Caption

Most building and pest reports in Queensland run to 30, 40, or 50 pages. Most buyers open them at the summary page. That is the wrong place to start.

The summary tells you there are major defects and minor defects. What it does not tell you is that "active timber pest activity" and "peeling paint on the back fence" can appear on the same report under similar language, and they require completely different responses. One has real cost and urgency. The other does not.

The ranking system between major and minor defects is where most buyers lose their bearings. A major defect is defined by structural significance, not by how expensive it sounds in the report. Some items listed as major are routine maintenance that any building will accumulate over time. Others are genuinely material and warrant further investigation before you proceed. The label alone does not tell you which is which.

The other thing that catches buyers out is treating general maintenance items and active pest issues as part of the same conversation with the vendor. They are not. One is expected in any property of age. The other is a specific problem with a specific remedy that should be assessed separately.

Reading a B&P report well is a skill. It is learnable, and knowing what to focus on before you go back to the vendor or your solicitor makes the rest of the process considerably cleaner.

The full article walks through what to look for, in what order, and how to think about what actually matters.
