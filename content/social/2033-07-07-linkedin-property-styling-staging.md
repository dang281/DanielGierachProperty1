## Caption

Property styling is primarily a photography investment. The goal is not to make a house look like a different house. The goal is to produce photographs that generate enquiry, and enquiry is what drives competition. Competition is what moves price.

That reframe matters because vendors who resist styling often do so on the grounds that buyers will see through it. They will. No one is trying to hide the property's actual condition or dimensions. Styling works because it controls where a viewer's eye goes in a photograph and what feeling that photograph produces. A well-styled room photographs as spacious and light, even when it is a modest size. An unstyled room often photographs with the wrong things in focus.

The practical question for a vendor is not whether to style but what level of styling the property and the buyer pool warrant. A heritage cottage in Balmoral targeting owner-occupiers at the upper end of the price range has a different brief from a two-bedroom unit in Morningside. Both can benefit from some version of presentation investment. The question is where that investment produces the most measurable return.

The article covers the cost range for full styling versus partial preparation, what a styling brief should address, and how to assess whether the likely price uplift justifies the cost for your specific property.
