**Caption:**

Under the REIQ contract, buyers are entitled to one pre-settlement inspection. The property must be in the same condition as it was at the time the contract was signed. That is not a loose standard; it is a contractual obligation.

The pre-settlement inspection is where sellers most commonly create their own problems in the final days before settlement. Not through deliberate action, but through oversight.

Fixtures and fittings included in the sale must still be in the property. If the contract listed the dishwasher or the garden shed, they need to be there. Items the seller is removing need to be gone without causing damage. Rubbish left in the house or on the property after the final clean-out gives the buyer grounds to delay settlement and seek rectification. At that point, with keys due to be handed over and removalists booked, delay is costly for everyone.

The article I have linked covers exactly what the pre-settlement inspection covers, what sellers are obligated to leave in place, and what the buyer can do if the property is not in the required condition. The scenarios that create the most friction are the ones sellers did not anticipate, and most of them are avoidable with straightforward planning in the final week before settlement.

Read it before you get to that week.
