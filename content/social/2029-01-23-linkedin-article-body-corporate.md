## Caption

Most buyers who ask about body corporate levies focus on the annual fee. The levy is the wrong number to focus on.

The number that matters more is the sinking fund balance.

Body corporate levies are split between the administrative fund (day-to-day maintenance, insurance, management fees) and the sinking fund (capital works: roof replacement, common area refurbishment, lift servicing). A complex with a well-funded sinking fund has been consistently collecting from owners to pay for future capital works. A complex with a depleted sinking fund will need a special levy to cover major works when they arise.

That special levy is the cost buyers miss when they only look at the annual fee.

For reference, levies in Brisbane's inner east typically run $2,000 to $4,000 per year for a small townhouse complex and $6,000 to $14,000 or more per year for a mid-rise with a pool, lift, and gym. Neither figure tells you whether the sinking fund is healthy.

Before you make an offer on any unit or townhouse, request the body corporate records and look at the sinking fund balance and the maintenance schedule. That review, done before signing, is far easier than negotiating around it afterward.
