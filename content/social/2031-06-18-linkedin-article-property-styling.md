## Caption

Property styling is primarily a photography investment. Better photographs drive enquiry. More enquiry produces competition. Competition is what moves price. The logic works backwards from the sale result, not forwards from the cost of the furniture hire.

When vendors evaluate the cost of styling, the question they typically ask is whether the furniture is worth the fee. That is not the right question. The furniture is not the product. The photographs are the product, and the photographs are what almost every buyer in the inner east market sees before deciding whether to attend an open home. A property that photographs well generates more enquiries, more attendance, and more active buyers at inspection. That is the mechanism through which styling affects the sale result, regardless of whether the buyer notices the furniture.

Vacant properties benefit most from this. An unfurnished inner east home photographs as smaller than it is, because the rooms have no reference scale. A styled vacant property solves that problem and also presents each room with a clear function, which matters particularly for buyers who are trying to assess liveability rather than floor area. The cost for a three-bedroom inner east home typically runs $3,000 to $6,000 for a four-week campaign. On a property priced above $1 million, the enquiry volume that a well-photographed campaign generates relative to a poorly photographed one is the relevant comparison, not the absolute cost of the styling fee.

Vendors targeting a spring campaign who make styling decisions in June, with time to do them properly, produce better results than those who make those decisions the week before the first open home.

The article covers what styling involves, what it typically costs, and how to evaluate whether it is appropriate for your property.
