## Caption

Off-market sales are common in Brisbane's inner east. They are also almost always a trade-off that costs the seller money.

The logic of an off-market sale is appealing on the surface. No open homes. No campaign period. No photography, no advertising spend, no weekends of strangers walking through your house. An agent calls to say there is a buyer ready to go. You accept an offer. You move on. The convenience is real.

The problem is that limiting competition almost always affects the final price. Property values are not fixed. They are discovered through a process in which buyers compete against each other. When that competition is absent, the price discovery mechanism is absent with it. The buyer who purchases off-market may pay a fair price by their assessment. But fair by whose benchmark? Without comparable competing offers, there is no way for the seller to know whether a better outcome was available.

The agent who approaches a seller with "I have a buyer ready to go, no need to list" is offering convenience. That is a legitimate thing to offer. But the seller should understand clearly what they are trading for that convenience: the opportunity for a competitive campaign to establish true market value.

In a market where well-presented, correctly priced inner east properties are attracting multiple serious buyers within the first two weeks of a campaign, skipping the campaign to accept an off-market offer is a decision worth examining closely before making.
