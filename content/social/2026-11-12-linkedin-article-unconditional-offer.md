## Caption

In a competitive offer situation, a seller will often accept a lower unconditional offer over a higher conditional one.

That sounds counterintuitive. Here is why it happens.

A conditional offer, even at a higher price, introduces risk and delay. If the buyer's finance falls through at day 14, or the building and pest comes back with a note the buyer decides to walk on, the vendor is back to day one. The campaign has continued running while the contract was in place. The buyer pool has moved on.

An unconditional offer eliminates that window. Once the contract is unconditional, both parties are committed. The vendor can prepare for settlement, discharge the mortgage, arrange removalists, and plan the next step without holding their breath on a finance clause.

In Brisbane's inner east right now, buyers who can offer unconditional terms, typically because their finance is pre-approved and the inspection was done before the offer, have a genuine structural advantage in a multiple-offer situation. That advantage is worth more than most buyers realise, and it is often the reason a vendor chooses the second-highest offer over the first.

Understanding this changes how you approach an offer in a competitive campaign.
