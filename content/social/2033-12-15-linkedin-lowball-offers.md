## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright closes the negotiation completely. A counter-offer, even one significantly above the opening position, keeps it alive. This distinction sounds simple but it is the one that most determines whether a deal gets done from a low opening bid.

In Brisbane's inner east, 8 to 10% below asking price is a standard opening position from experienced buyers. It is not necessarily an insult, a sign of disrespect, or a signal that the buyer is not serious or not motivated. It is an opening negotiating position from someone who understands how negotiations work and who expects a counter-response. Vendors who interpret a low first offer as a statement about the property's value, rather than as an opening move in a negotiation, often make the mistake of dismissing it rather than responding to it.

The response to the first offer determines the outcome more often than the price of the first offer does. A vendor who counters, even at a number well above the opening position, gives the buyer a target to work toward. The negotiation has a floor and a ceiling. Movement becomes possible. A vendor who rejects with no counter has ended the conversation, and in many cases the buyer moves on permanently rather than coming back.

Knowing the acceptable range before the first offer arrives is the preparation that makes this easier. When a vendor has thought through the price they are genuinely willing to accept, the initial response is strategic rather than reactive. It is measured rather than emotional. The first offer, whatever number it starts at, becomes the beginning of a process rather than a referendum on the property.
