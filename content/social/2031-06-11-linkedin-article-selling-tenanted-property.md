## Caption

Selling a tenanted property in Queensland requires 24 hours written notice before every inspection. A fixed-term lease that extends past the settlement date means the buyer takes over that lease, not vacant possession.

Both of those facts regularly catch inner east investors off guard when they begin the sale process without first reviewing their tenancy obligations. The notice requirement is not a formality. It is a legislative requirement under the Residential Tenancies and Rooming Accommodation Act 2008 (Qld) and inspections must occur at a reasonable time and with reasonable frequency. A tenant who does not consent to more frequent access cannot be compelled to allow it, regardless of campaign requirements. A campaign that assumes weekly open homes without engaging with the tenant's rights in advance can run into problems that affect both the timeline and the relationship with the tenant.

The fixed-term lease issue is the more consequential one for most investors. If a lease expires after the proposed settlement date, the buyer does not receive vacant possession at settlement. They receive the property with the tenant and the lease in place. That changes who the likely buyers are, typically narrowing the pool to investors and removing owner-occupiers, which affects both competition and price. Aligning the campaign launch and settlement date with the lease expiry date avoids this entirely, but it requires planning several months ahead.

EOFY triggers portfolio reviews and investors considering selling their inner east rental should understand these obligations before they begin a campaign, not partway through it.

The article covers the notice requirements, the lease alignment question, and how to plan a tenanted sale from the beginning.
