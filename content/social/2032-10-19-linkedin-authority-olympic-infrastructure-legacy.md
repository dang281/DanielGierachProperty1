## Caption

The Games ended in August. The city has had two months to breathe, and it is worth taking stock of what actually changed in the inner east.

The Woolloongabba precinct is the clearest example. The Cross River Rail station was already under construction before the Games, but the Olympics accelerated upgrades to the surrounding streetscape, bus interchange, and pedestrian connections. The precinct around Gabba is more walkable now than it was three years ago. That is not a prediction, it is something you can observe on foot.

Cycling infrastructure across the inner east was extended and improved in the lead-up to 2032. Separated lanes along key corridors and improved connections to the bikeway network mean more residents are moving through the suburb without a car. That has a practical effect on how people use the area daily.

Brisbane's international profile is now different. That does not automatically translate into property demand, but it has brought the city's name into conversations it was previously absent from. Some of that will filter through to buyer inquiry from interstate and international purchasers over the next few years.

What did not change is the character of the inner east: the mix of post-war houses, elevated street trees, access to the river, and proximity to the CBD. The fundamentals that made these suburbs attractive before the Games are intact. The infrastructure additions sit on top of that foundation, not in place of it.
