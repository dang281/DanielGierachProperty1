## Caption

Yesterday's poll asked how an auction reserve should be set. The votes are spread across all four options, which I think reflects something true about the field: there is no single correct method, but there is a useful framework for combining them.

A reserve worth defending on auction day is built bottom-up from three inputs.

The first is comparable sales: what genuinely similar properties have sold for in the past 60 to 90 days, not headline numbers from years ago. The second is the registered bidder pool: how many buyers have signed up to bid, and what feedback they have given through inspections. The third is the seller's walk-away figure: the price below which the seller would rather hold the property than sell it.

The art is in combining them. A reserve set purely on comparable sales ignores who is actually in the room on the day. One set purely on the seller's bottom line risks passing in to a strong room. One set purely on bidder feedback can leave money on the table if the room turns up better than expected.

There is a calculator that walks through the three inputs and shows where they intersect. Useful for stress-testing a reserve against the campaign feedback in the final week. Link in the comments.
