## Caption

At almost every open home I run in the inner east, the conversation eventually turns to character homes versus modern builds. Buyers have opinions, vendors have opinions, and people standing in a 1920s Queenslander on Wellington Street in Hawthorne or a pre-war cottage on Lytton Road in Balmoral will ask me some version of the same question: why does this still cost so much more?

The answer has three parts.

The first is land. Character homes in the inner east tend to sit on original allotments, which are often wider and deeper than what new infill development has produced. That land cannot be replaced, and in suburbs like Norman Park, Camp Hill, and Bulimba, where flat, usable sites within walking distance of amenities are scarce, the block itself carries significant value independent of what's on it.

The second is aspect and setting. Many of the best character homes in Hawthorne and Balmoral occupy elevated positions with river or city outlooks that a townhouse three streets away simply cannot replicate. Buyers pay for that view and for the privacy that comes with a proper boundary.

The third is irreplaceability. You cannot build a new Queenslander and have it be the same thing. Buyers who want high ceilings, timber floors, wide verandahs, and the particular feel of a century-old house understand this. That demographic is not small, and it is not going away. They will not substitute a modern build and feel equally satisfied.

The premium for character homes in the inner east reflects all three of these things, and in my observation it has remained remarkably stable even as the broader market has moved through cycles.
