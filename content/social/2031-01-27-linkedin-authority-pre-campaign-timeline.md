## Caption

In Brisbane's inner east, a well-prepared property and an underprepared property in the same street will not achieve the same result at auction, even if they are structurally similar. The difference is usually built in the four to eight weeks before the first open home, not on the day.

What happens in that window: the vendor and agent work through decluttering and storage, minor repairs that would show up on a building and pest report or in photography, any paintwork that needs refreshing, garden presentation, and the brief for the styling team if styling is being used. Photography is booked only when the property is ready, not before. Rushing photography because the vendor wants to go live quickly is one of the more common ways inner east campaigns lose money.

Alongside the physical preparation, the disclosure statement needs to be prepared with the vendor's solicitor. In Queensland, a seller's disclosure statement is a legal requirement, and getting it completed early means it can be handed to an interested buyer's solicitor on the day of a serious inquiry. Delays at that stage lose buyers.

The other conversation that needs to happen in this window is the sale method and pricing conversation. Choosing between auction, private treaty, and expression of interest affects how the campaign is structured, the marketing schedule, and the price expectations that guide negotiations. That decision should not happen under pressure in the week before launch.

Missing the pre-campaign window does not just mean launching late. It means launching underprepared, and underprepared properties show it.
