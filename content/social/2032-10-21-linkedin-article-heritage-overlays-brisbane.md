## Caption

A heritage overlay on a Brisbane inner east property does not prevent renovation. What it does is require Brisbane City Council approval for external changes that are visible from the street. Most homeowners in character homes do not know which type of overlay applies to their property, what it restricts, or how it affects buyers considering the purchase.

That knowledge gap has real consequences. A buyer who discovers a heritage overlay during due diligence and does not understand it may walk away from a property they could have renovated. A seller who does not disclose it, or cannot explain it clearly, creates uncertainty in the campaign that costs money.

There are two main overlay types in Brisbane. The Traditional Building Character overlay applies to a large number of pre-1947 homes across the inner suburbs and primarily protects the street-facing character. The Local Heritage Place designation is more specific and applies to buildings with recognised historical or architectural significance. The controls differ, and the approval pathway differs with them.

For buyers, the practical question is whether the renovation work you have in mind requires a development application or sits within accepted development. For sellers, it is whether your marketing material and contract documents give buyers enough information to make a confident decision without putting the campaign on pause to chase answers.

The article covers both overlay types, what BCC approval actually involves, and what buyers and sellers should confirm before a campaign starts.
