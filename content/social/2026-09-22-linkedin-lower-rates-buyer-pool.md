## Caption

The standard observation about rate cuts is that they add more buyers to the market. That's true. But the more useful insight for vendors in Brisbane's inner east is that rate cuts change the composition of the buyer pool, not just its size.

The inner east has a high proportion of upgraders, owner-occupiers who already own property and are looking to move up. These buyers are different from first-time purchasers or pure investors in one important way: they're sensitive to rate movements on both sides of the transaction. They're selling and buying, often at the same time or in close sequence. When rates are higher and borrowing capacity is compressed, the upgrader who can see the property they want in the inner east is often stuck. The gap between what their current property will sell for and what they need to borrow to buy the next one doesn't work at the higher rate.

When rates ease, that calculation shifts. The borrowing capacity that was marginal becomes viable. The upgrader who has been waiting now has a path, and they move. That buyer's entry into the market at the price point they're buying creates competition that wasn't there before, and competition at any price point applies upward pressure on that segment.

What I observe in the inner east when borrowing conditions ease is that the volume of genuine upgrader buyers increases more noticeably than in outer suburban markets. These buyers are motivated, they've been watching for a while, and they move with conviction when the conditions allow it. For vendors selling at the price points these buyers target, that shift is meaningful.
