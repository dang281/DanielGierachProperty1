## Caption

March auctions in Brisbane's inner east consistently show strong buyer engagement. The conditions that make auction the right choice are present right now.

This is not a universal statement. Auction is the right method for some properties and sellers in some conditions. But in the inner east in autumn, when buyer pools are deep, when well-presented family homes are attracting multiple genuinely interested parties, and when the urgency to transact before the mid-year softening is real, auction creates the outcome that private treaty often struggles to match.

When does auction work well in this precinct?

Norman Park is a suburb where auction performs consistently. The buyer pool for a four-bedroom character home in the right street tends to be broad, with families, upgraders, and investors all competing for a limited pool of properties. When three or four of those buyers are in the room on auction day, the result regularly exceeds private treaty expectations.

Camp Hill has a similar profile. The Whites Hill catchment premium means buyers who are motivated by school access will compete, often past their stated limit, when they find the right property. Auction captures that motivation in a way private treaty cannot reliably replicate.

Hawthorne, where prestige and character home buyers overlap, is another suburb where auction has performed well consistently. The buyer at this end of the inner east market is often experienced and knows the process. They respond to the competitive dynamic.

My view on when auction is the right choice: when you have evidence of genuine competition in the buyer pool, when the property has broad appeal, and when an unconditional result serves the seller's plans. Not every property, but the right ones.
