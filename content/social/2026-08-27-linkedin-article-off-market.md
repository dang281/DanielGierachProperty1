## Caption

Off-market is one of the most misused terms in real estate marketing, and the honest version is not what most agents lead with. So here it is plainly: off-market usually means limiting buyer competition, and limiting competition almost always affects price. Not always dramatically, but the direction is consistent.

The logic is straightforward. Competition between buyers is what drives price beyond the floor. When a property is shown to a small, selected group instead of the full market, the depth of that competition shrinks. If the right buyer happens to be in that group, you might get a good outcome. But "might" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

There are situations where off-market genuinely makes sense. Genuine privacy requirements, where the owner's identity being associated with a public listing is a real problem. Tenanted properties where the notice obligations and tenant cooperation make a full campaign operationally difficult. Estate sales where the executor needs speed over price optimisation. These are real use cases and I've managed them.

For most other situations, particularly in the inner east where demand is active and buyer competition is real, off-market is a convenience for the agent or a misplaced desire for discretion on the part of the vendor. It's worth asking clearly: who benefits from this being off-market, and is it you?

The article at danielgierach.com/insights/off-market-property-sales-brisbane covers the full picture, including when the trade-off is worth making and when it isn't.
