## Caption

Selling a tenanted property in Queensland is manageable. It requires understanding two things: your obligations to the tenant during the campaign, and how the tenancy affects the buyer pool you are selling into.

For open homes, you need to give the tenant 24 hours written notice for every inspection. Not a verbal confirmation. Written notice, each time. The number of inspections is not capped, but the notice requirement applies to every one of them. A vendor who schedules open homes without proper notice creates a legitimate complaint and a risk to the campaign.

The tenancy type matters for the settlement outcome. A fixed-term lease that does not expire before the settlement date means the buyer takes over that lease as the new landlord. They cannot force vacant possession on settlement day. That significantly changes the buyer pool. Owner-occupier buyers almost always require vacant possession as a contract condition. Investors do not, but investor buyers often price the tenancy differently, particularly if the rent is below market.

The practical solution for most vendors is to align the settlement date with the lease expiry before launching the campaign. If that is not possible, price and market the property as an investment from the start rather than positioning it for owner-occupiers who cannot buy under those terms.
