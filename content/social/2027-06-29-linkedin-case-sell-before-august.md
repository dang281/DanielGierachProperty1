## Caption

Every year in Brisbane's inner east, vendors who planned to sell in spring reach June and start wondering whether they should move earlier. In many cases, the honest answer is yes.

The assumption behind waiting for spring is that more buyers are active in September and October, which is broadly true. What is also true is that more properties are competing for those buyers at exactly the same time. The spring decision is a trade-off: more buyer activity against a more crowded field.

The properties best suited to a winter campaign are those whose selling points live in the structure rather than the garden. A well-built Queenslander with a generous floorplan, good ceiling height, and a north-facing aspect does not need the garden in full bloom to present well. A property on a quiet tree-lined street photographs well in winter. A home with a recently renovated interior looks just as compelling in June as it does in October.

The properties that benefit most from spring are the ones where the outdoor appeal is central to the selling argument: a pool that looks best in summer light, a garden that is the feature of the back yard, a deck that needs warm weather to communicate its value. Those properties should wait.

For everything else, the winter window deserves an honest look. Brisbane's inner east buyers active in June and July are serious. They are not browsing open homes for entertainment. They have financing ready and they move when the right property appears. Competing against three listings instead of twelve is worth more than most vendors realise.

If you are still deciding whether to list before August or hold for spring, I am happy to give you a straight read on which approach suits your property.
