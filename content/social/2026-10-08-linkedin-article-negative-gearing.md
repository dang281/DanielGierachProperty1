## Caption

Negative gearing makes financial sense under a specific set of conditions: the annual losses you absorb are more than offset over time by capital growth. When that calculation is genuinely positive, holding a negatively geared property is a rational strategy. The problem is that conditions change and the calculation is rarely reviewed as deliberately as it should be.

As holding costs have risen, whether through borrowing costs, land tax as property values have increased, insurance, or ongoing maintenance on older inner-east stock, the annual loss component of the equation grows. At the same time, the capital growth assumption that underpinned the original decision is not guaranteed to continue at the same rate. The strategy that made clear financial sense at purchase needs to be tested against current numbers, not assumed to remain sound because it was sound at the start.

Settlement timing relative to 30 June also matters for investors considering a sale. The interaction between settlement date, the capital gains discount for assets held longer than 12 months, and the financial year in which the gain is realised can have a material effect on the tax outcome. This is firmly in accountant territory, but understanding that the timing is a variable worth optimising is useful to know before you commit to a settlement date.

The article at danielgierach.com/insights/negative-gearing-selling-investment-property-brisbane covers the holding-cost review, the triggers that should prompt a reassessment, and the CGT timing considerations in more detail.
