## Caption

When an agent says they have a buyer ready to go, they are describing a convenience offer. Off-market sales almost always limit buyer competition, and limiting competition almost always affects price.

The buyer-ready offer is a common conversation at the end of a quiet market period, and it feels attractive because it removes the effort and uncertainty of running a campaign. But the buyer the agent is presenting is one buyer. One buyer cannot compete with themselves. The mechanism that moves a property price above the first offer received is competition between buyers who both want the property and who know other buyers want it too. An off-market transaction removes that mechanism entirely and replaces it with a negotiation between the vendor and a single party, where the vendor has no information about where other buyers in the market would have landed.

In the inner east, the difference between an off-market result and a well-run campaign result on a property in the $1.2 million to $1.8 million range regularly sits at $50,000 or more. That figure is not precise but it is grounded in the observable difference between what a single-buyer negotiation produces and what a competitive campaign produces on comparable properties. The convenience of avoiding a campaign has a cost, and that cost is usually borne entirely by the vendor.

There are circumstances where an off-market approach makes sense. A vendor who needs to transact quickly, who has a specific buyer they know and trust, or whose property has a genuinely limited buyer pool may find the trade-off acceptable. Most vendors in the inner east are not in those circumstances.

The article covers how off-market transactions work, when they make sense, and what vendors should ask before agreeing to one.
