## Caption

Most buyers receive a building and pest report and immediately flip to the summary page. That is the wrong place to start, and misreading the summary is one of the most common reasons buyers either walk away from sound properties or proceed with ones that have serious issues.

The summary in a standard Queensland building and pest report uses a traffic light classification: major defects, minor defects, and items requiring further investigation. The problem is that a long list of yellow items (minor defects) on an older timber home does not mean the property is compromised. It often means it is a 1960s house. Some things wear. That is expected and manageable.

What the summary does not adequately distinguish is the difference between active timber pest activity and general maintenance items. Both can appear in the same report. They require completely different responses. Active termite activity in a load-bearing structure is a negotiation point and potentially a deal-breaker. A cracked downpipe is a weekend of work.

Before you read the summary, go to the timber pest section of the report. Find out whether the inspector identified live activity, active damage, or conducive conditions. That distinction shapes how you should read everything else.

Inner east buyers paying at or above median should get their own independent report rather than relying on a vendor-supplied one. The cost is modest. The information asymmetry it removes is significant.
