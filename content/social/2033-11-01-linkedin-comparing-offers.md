## Caption

When I sit down with a vendor to compare two offers, price is always the first number they look at. But it is rarely the only number that matters.

Here is a scenario I walked through with a vendor recently. They had two offers on the table. One was $1.42M, unconditional, with a 30-day settlement. The other was $1.46M, subject to finance and building and pest, with a 45-day settlement. On paper, the second offer looked like an extra $40,000. In practice, it was an extra $40,000 attached to a 2 to 3 week window in which that offer could fall over completely.

Finance conditions lapse if the buyer cannot get approval. Building and pest conditions can be used to renegotiate if the inspector finds anything the buyer did not expect. And they almost always find something. So that $40,000 premium was not guaranteed. It was a possibility, contingent on two separate events that the vendor had no control over.

The unconditional offer at $1.42M was certain. The conditional offer at $1.46M was a number with a risk window attached to it. Most vendors, when they understand the distinction, recalibrate what the gap actually represents.

Settlement period matters too. A vendor who needs to be out by a specific date may value a 30-day settlement more than an extra $10,000 and a 60-day one. Deposit size signals buyer commitment. Access arrangements can protect the vendor's ability to stay in the property longer if they need it.

The highest offer and the best offer are not always the same thing. My job is to make sure vendors understand the difference before they decide.
