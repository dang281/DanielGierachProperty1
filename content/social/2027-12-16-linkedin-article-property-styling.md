## Caption

The most useful way to understand property styling is not as interior design. It is as a photography investment.

The photography is what gets buyers through the door. A styled property photographs significantly better than an occupied, everyday-lived-in home. Better photography produces more clicks on the listing. More clicks produce more open home attendance. More open home attendance produces more competition. Competition produces price.

The chain runs from styling to price, but the most critical link is the photograph.

This matters because it explains which styling investments are worth making and which are not. Heavy curtains in a dark room photograph poorly. Light, neutral tones photograph well. Beds made with hotel-quality linen photograph well. Family photos on the wall do not photograph badly, but they break the buyer's ability to imagine themselves living there.

Professional styling typically costs between $2,500 and $6,000 for a three-bedroom house in Brisbane's inner east, depending on how much of the vendor's own furniture is usable. Virtual staging, where furniture is added digitally to empty rooms, is a lower-cost alternative that produces good results for properties that are already vacant.

The return on styling is not guaranteed, but in a market where buyer decisions are made on a phone screen before anyone attends an open home, presentation has become more important, not less.
