## Caption

A clearance rate is a percentage. It tells you how many properties sold at or through auction as a proportion of those scheduled. What it does not tell you is which properties, at what price points, in which suburbs, and against what reserve.

In the Brisbane inner east, where auction is the standard method for houses in established suburbs but less common for apartments and smaller townhouses, the weekly clearance figure needs context to mean anything. A strong clearance rate in a week dominated by sub-$1m auctions is a different signal from the same rate in a week where multiple $2m-plus properties went under the hammer.

What I look at alongside the headline figure: the number of properties scheduled in the inner east specifically, which price range is performing best at auction in the current conditions, and the post-auction sale rate for properties that were passed in. In many campaigns, a property passed in at auction and sold within forty-eight hours is a strong result. The headline clearance rate counts it as a miss.

For vendors weighing up whether to use auction as their sale method in March 2034, the clearance rate is one input. The more relevant questions are: is there a buyer pool for this specific property type, is there more than one buyer, and is the reserve calibrated to where that buyer pool is operating? Those questions have answers that the weekly percentage alone cannot provide.
