## Caption

Two houses on the same street in Coorparoo or Bulimba can sell six months apart and be $200,000 different in price. That gap is not a market anomaly. It is the result of differences that do not appear in a listing portal search: land size, orientation, the condition of the structure, whether the property had dual access, whether the lounge room faced north or south, whether the kitchen had been updated or was still on the original 1990s fit-out.

When an experienced agent prices a property, the starting point is comparable sales evidence, but that evidence requires interpretation. A sold price means nothing on its own without knowing what the property actually offered a buyer at the time of purchase. A house that sold for $1.45 million with a 600 square metre north-facing block and a renovated interior is not a comparable for a 430 square metre south-facing block that has not been touched since 2005, even if they are on the same street.

What vendors get wrong when they look up sold prices on listing portals is that they are looking at headline numbers without the context that made those numbers achievable. They might pick the top comparable on the street and assume their property can match it, without accounting for the features that drove that result.

Good comparable sales analysis includes a conversation about which features of your property align with recent results and which do not. That is what separates a well-grounded appraisal from a figure plucked from a portal.
