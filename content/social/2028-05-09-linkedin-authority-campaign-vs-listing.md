## Caption

A lot of sellers believe they are running a campaign when what they actually have is a listing. The distinction matters.

A listing means a property is on realestate.com.au with photos and a price guide. That is the minimum viable presence in the market. It is necessary but it is not sufficient, and treating it as a campaign is one of the more common reasons a property underperforms its potential.

A campaign starts before the listing goes live. It involves preparing the property so that the photography captures something buyers will respond to, not just document what is there. It means timing the launch against what else is available in the area, so the property is not competing directly with something very similar in the same week. It means identifying the buyers who have been active in this price range and suburb who have not yet found what they need, and making sure they know this property exists before the first open home, not after.

During a campaign, inspection feedback is collected and acted on. If buyers are consistently raising the same objection, the question is whether that objection can be addressed or whether the pricing needs to reflect it. Between week one and week two, the agent and the vendor should be having a genuine strategic conversation, not a polite check-in.

When momentum slows, the response is a diagnosis, not a reflex. The answer might be the price, but it might equally be the way the property is being presented at open homes, the quality of the photography, or the timing of when it launched.

That active management from start to finish is the campaign. The listing is just the address. danielgierach.com
