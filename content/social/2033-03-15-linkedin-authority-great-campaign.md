## Caption

In Brisbane's inner east autumn 2033, the gap between a good campaign and a great one comes down to three variables that vendors control: preparation, pricing strategy, and method of sale.

Preparation is the one most underestimated. A property that arrives on market with maintenance items addressed, professional styling in place, and photography that reflects how the home actually feels in good light starts with an advantage that does not close during the campaign. Buyers shortlist emotionally and justify rationally. The emotional shortlisting happens in the first six seconds of a listing photo. What happens in those six seconds is entirely within a vendor's control.

Pricing strategy determines how many buyers show up and how quickly they act. A well-priced property in Coorparoo or Camp Hill attracts a broader buyer pool than a price that has been set to leave room to negotiate. More buyers in the room creates competition. Competition is what drives results above expectation, not negotiation from a single motivated buyer.

Method of sale matters because it should follow the property and the buyer pool, not default. Auction works for a character home in a suburb with strong clearance data and a pool of competitive buyers. Private treaty works better when the buyer profile is narrower or the purchase is more finance-dependent. Getting this wrong does not sink a campaign but it does add friction.

Three variables. All within the vendor's sphere. That distinction matters.
