## Caption

The pre-settlement inspection is not a formality. It is a contractual right the buyer has to confirm the property is in the same condition as at contract date, with all inclusions present and all agreed repairs completed. Most vendors treat it like a courtesy walkthrough. The buyers who find problems at pre-settlement are the ones who understand it is not.

Removalist damage and missing inclusions are the two most common sources of last-minute disputes. Removalists are not always careful. A wall gets scuffed, a door frame gets clipped, a fixed shelf gets taken because the removalist assumed it was personal property. Missing inclusions happen when the vendor takes something that is in the contract as an inclusion, because they've had it for years and genuinely forgot it was listed. Light fittings, dishwashers, and blinds are the most frequent examples.

Vendors who do their own walkthrough two to three days before settlement catch these issues before the buyer's representative does. A scuff discovered three days before settlement is a minor repair. A scuff discovered during the pre-settlement inspection with ninety minutes before the settlement appointment is a potential dispute that can delay settlement and expose the vendor to breach claims.

The article covers exactly what buyers can and cannot require at a pre-settlement inspection, what the vendor's obligations are when defects are found, and how to structure the final days before settlement to avoid the common problems.
