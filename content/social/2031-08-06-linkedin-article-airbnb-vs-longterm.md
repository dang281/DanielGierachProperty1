## Caption

The occupancy rate required for a short-term rental to net more than a long-term tenancy is higher than most investors estimate before they start. Once cleaning fees, platform commissions, short-term rental insurance, higher maintenance frequency, and utility costs are accounted for, many inner east short-term rental properties are running at a similar net return to a quality long-term tenancy with significantly more management effort involved.

The headline weekly rate on a short-term platform looks appealing compared to a long-term rental figure. The problem is that the headline rate is not the net figure, and the occupancy assumption built into most investors' back-of-envelope calculations is optimistic. A property that earns $800 per night but sits vacant three nights per week and requires a professional clean after every booking is a different financial proposition to the $550 per week long-term tenancy the investor was comparing it against.

August is when property investors reassess strategies. EOFY figures have come through, tax returns are being prepared, and the question of whether the current strategy is actually working gets asked with real numbers rather than projections. For investors who chose short-term rental in the inner east, this is the moment of honest reckoning. Some will find it has worked well. Others will find the management effort has not been matched by the return.

The full article sets out the honest comparison between both models, including the costs that are most frequently underestimated in the short-term rental calculation.
