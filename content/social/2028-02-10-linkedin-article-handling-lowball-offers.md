## Caption

A low offer is not always a bad offer.

Most sellers' instinct when a significantly below-asking offer comes in is to reject it outright. That instinct is usually wrong.

Rejecting a low offer closes the negotiation entirely. The buyer who opened low now has no path forward and moves to the next property on their list. The seller is back to waiting. The opportunity to find out where that buyer's ceiling actually sits is gone.

The better response is to counter at a number close to your floor, not your asking price. That counter does two things: it signals you are willing to engage, and it opens a conversation about where the negotiation can go. The buyer who opened 10% below asking may have the capacity to come to within 3%. You will not know unless you respond.

In the inner east, a low opening offer on a well-priced property is often a negotiating tactic rather than a genuine valuation. The buyer is testing. How the seller responds sets the tone for the rest of the negotiation.

I have written a full guide to handling offers when selling, including how to evaluate competing offers and when to counter versus when to hold firm.
