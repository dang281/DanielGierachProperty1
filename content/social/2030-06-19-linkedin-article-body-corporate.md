## Caption

Most buyers ask about body corporate levies when assessing a unit or townhouse. Few ask about the sinking fund balance. That is the wrong priority.

The annual levy tells you what you are paying right now. The sinking fund balance tells you what capacity the body corporate has to cover major capital works without a special levy. A body corporate with a well-funded sinking fund can replace the roof, repaint the building, or repair the lifts without asking owners for a sudden cash contribution. A body corporate with a depleted sinking fund cannot.

The levy ranges are broad but give a rough frame. For a small townhouse complex, levies typically run between $2,000 and $4,000 per year. For a mid-rise building with a lift, pool, and gym, $6,000 to $14,000 or more is common. But a $3,000-per-year levy at a complex with a near-empty sinking fund is a riskier position than a $10,000-per-year levy at a complex that is properly funded for future works.

The practical steps before buying: request the body corporate records from the selling agent, look at the sinking fund balance, check the minutes from the last two AGMs for any planned or disputed works, and review whether any special levies have been issued or proposed.

This is information you are entitled to request and review before exchanging contracts. Use it.
