## Caption

Most buyers look at the quarterly levy and stop there. That is the wrong number to focus on.

The levy tells you what the building currently costs to run. It does not tell you whether the building has been maintained properly, or whether a large special levy is quietly forming in the background. That information lives in the sinking fund balance.

A sinking fund that is underfunded relative to the age and condition of the building is a warning sign, not a minor detail. It means maintenance has been deferred, and someone will eventually pay for it. On a well-managed building, the sinking fund grows steadily and reflects a long-term maintenance plan. On a poorly managed one, it sits low year after year until the body corporate has no option but to pass a special levy to fund a repair that should have been anticipated a decade earlier.

Levy ranges also vary significantly by building type. Boutique complexes in the inner east often carry lower administrative levies but require more owner-funded maintenance individually. Larger complexes with lifts, pools, and gym facilities carry higher levies for a reason.

Before signing a contract on a unit, there are specific questions worth asking, and the article covers exactly what those are and where to find the answers in the body corporate records.
