## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright closes the negotiation entirely. The right response to a lowball offer is almost never a flat rejection.

This is one of the most consistent mistakes sellers make, and it is understandable. A low offer feels like an insult. The instinct is to reject it and move on. But that instinct costs money more often than it saves dignity.

A lowball offer is almost always the beginning of a negotiation, not the final position of the buyer. The buyer is testing. They want to know whether the seller is motivated and what the actual floor is. A counter-offer at your genuine minimum tells them far more than a rejection, and it keeps the conversation open.

A few things worth understanding about handling offers well:

Your response time matters. A slow response signals either disinterest or internal disagreement between parties. Buyers read these signals. A prompt, professional counter-offer signals a seller who is engaged and serious.

Conditions are negotiable. A buyer who offers $1.05 million with a long finance condition might be willing to accept $1.08 million with a shorter one. The headline number is not the only variable on the table.

Multiple offers are best managed by creating a structured process rather than running a race between buyers without boundaries. An experienced agent will set up a process that maximises competition without alienating any party.

Your agent's role in a negotiation is to protect your interests while keeping the buyer engaged. The moment a buyer feels manipulated or disrespected, the negotiation typically ends.
