## Caption

Most property transactions in Queensland settle without incident. But the disputes that do arise at settlement day almost always trace back to the same source: the pre-settlement inspection.

Buyers are entitled to one pre-settlement inspection in Queensland. Its purpose is to confirm the property is in the same condition as it was when the contract was signed. Not better. Not worse. The same.

Two things most commonly go wrong at this stage.

The first is removals damage. Moving a large fridge, a dining table, or a heavy wardrobe through a narrow hallway can scratch floors, mark walls, and chip architraves. Any damage that did not exist at contract signing is the vendor's responsibility to make good before settlement.

The second is inclusions. Items that were present at the open homes and during buyer due diligence, but were not explicitly excluded in the contract, are assumed to remain with the property. Light fittings, dishwashers, curtains, and built-in shelving are common points of dispute. If a vendor removes an item that was not formally excluded in the contract, the buyer has grounds to delay settlement until it is reinstated or compensated for.

The practical advice for vendors is to walk through their own property two or three days before the scheduled settlement. Check every room against the contract inclusions list. Look at floors, walls, and any feature the removalists moved. Raise any issue with your solicitor before settlement day, not on the day itself.
