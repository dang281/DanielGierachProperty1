## Caption

An easement on a title search tells you a legal right exists over part of the land. It does not tell you whether that right will affect your life in the property. That distinction requires reading the detail, not just noting the presence of the instrument.

A drainage easement along the rear boundary of an inner east property is common. In most cases it sits behind a fence line, council maintains it, and an owner rarely thinks about it after settlement. That easement almost never affects value or the practical enjoyment of the land in any meaningful way.

An access easement running through the yard is a completely different matter. It preserves the right of another party to cross that land, potentially at any time, and it does not disappear at settlement. Building over it without consent is not permitted. Fencing it off or blocking access could create a legal dispute. The presence of that easement changes what you can do with the property.

Most buyers receive a title search as part of their contract review, note the word "easement" in the documents, and move on. Without knowing which type of easement is registered, and where it runs on the land, the review is incomplete.

The article I have published covers the types of easements commonly found on inner east Brisbane properties, how to locate them on the lot plan, what each type means for the owner, and the questions to ask your solicitor before signing.
