## Caption

Off-market property transactions are often presented as an advantage for vendors. The honest picture is more specific than that.

Off-market works when there is a genuine pre-existing buyer, someone who has specifically registered interest in the property type and suburb, and the agent has a direct and credible connection to that buyer. In those circumstances, the off-market transaction can be clean, fast, and achieve a good outcome for both parties.

Off-market does not work when it is simply a campaign without the public element. Limiting the property to the agent's database of registered buyers means limiting the buyer pool, and limiting the buyer pool means limiting competition. Limiting competition almost always affects the final price.

The claim that occasionally accompanies off-market proposals, that "we have a buyer ready to go," is worth examining. An agent who has a pre-approved buyer actively searching for a property matching your description is genuinely useful. An agent who uses the phrase as an approach to avoid going to market with a proper campaign is offering something that benefits the agent's efficiency more than the vendor's outcome.

The question to ask before agreeing to an off-market sale is simple: who is the buyer, have they seen comparable properties, and what is their pre-approval? If those questions produce clear answers, the conversation is worth having. If they produce generalities, a proper campaign will almost certainly produce a better result.
