## Caption

The gross rental yield on a short-term rental is almost always higher than a long-term tenancy. The net return, after platform fees, cleaning, insurance, and maintenance, is usually much closer than the top-line figures suggest.

The comparison that most investors make when evaluating short-term versus long-term rental is between the advertised nightly rate multiplied by an assumed occupancy level, and the weekly rent on a comparable long-term tenancy. That comparison is not complete. Platform fees on Airbnb and similar services run 15 to 20 per cent of gross revenue before anything else is deducted. That single line item already narrows the gap substantially, and the operating costs beyond the platform fee are where the real difference emerges.

Cleaning between every guest stay, consumables, the higher insurance premium for short-term accommodation, and a maintenance rate that is materially higher than a long-term tenancy due to more frequent turnover of occupants typically add $15,000 to $25,000 per year for an average inner east property. When those costs are factored into the net return calculation, short-term and long-term often land within a few thousand dollars of each other annually. The short-term option then requires year-round active management, guest communication, scheduling, and the variability of occupancy that comes with seasonal demand.

Neither structure is inherently wrong. The question is whether the additional effort and operational risk of short-term rental is worth the net return premium, which is frequently much smaller than the gross figures imply.

The article works through both models with realistic numbers for the inner east market.
