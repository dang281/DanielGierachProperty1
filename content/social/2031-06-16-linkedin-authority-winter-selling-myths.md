## Caption

The advice to wait for spring is given to Brisbane vendors so consistently that it has stopped being advice and started being assumption. In Brisbane's inner east, the data does not consistently support it.

Start with the climate. Brisbane's winter is mild. An open home in June or July is not cold in any way that deters buyers from attending. The argument that buyers stay home in July because the weather is unpleasant does not apply here the way it might in Melbourne or Sydney. Buyers in the inner east are not wearing coats to open homes in June. They are wearing what they wear in March, because the temperature difference is not dramatic enough to change behaviour.

The presentation argument also runs the other way from what is usually assumed. A property photographed in winter Brisbane does not suffer from the harsh midday light that creates blown-out exteriors in January photographs. The garden is typically well-managed rather than overgrown, which is a genuine presentation advantage for properties with outdoor space. A property that has been properly prepared looks good in June, and in some cases it looks better than it would in February.

The supply side of the equation is the most important factor that the spring-is-better advice ignores. Spring brings more listings as well as more buyers. A vendor who launches in September is competing with a higher volume of comparable properties than one who launches in June. The buyers who are active in winter have fewer options to consider, which concentrates attention on the properties that are available.

Winter is not always the right choice. A vendor who is not yet ready should not rush to beat the season. But for a prepared vendor with a well-presented property, the case for launching in June or July is stronger than the seasonal narrative typically suggests.
