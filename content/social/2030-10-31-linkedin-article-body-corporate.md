## Caption

When buyers evaluate a unit or townhouse, the body corporate levy is usually the first number they look at. It is not the most important one.

The sinking fund balance is what matters.

The levy is the annual fee you pay to cover shared maintenance and administration costs. The sinking fund is the reserve account the body corporate accumulates for major capital works: roof replacement, lift upgrades, facade repairs, common area renovations. A body corporate with a strong sinking fund has money set aside for the work the building will eventually need. A body corporate with a depleted sinking fund has two options when major work arrives: levy owners for a special levy, or borrow.

Special levies are the bill that arrives without warning. They can be $5,000, $15,000, or $50,000 depending on the building and the work required. They are not optional. If you own a lot in the scheme, you pay your share.

Before buying in any body corporate scheme, request the most recent body corporate financials and review the sinking fund balance relative to the 10-year capital works plan. A scheme with a levy of $4,000 per year and a fully funded sinking fund is a better proposition than one with a $2,500 levy and a sinking fund running at 20 percent of required capital.

Levies for small townhouse complexes typically run $2,000 to $4,000 per year. Mid-rise buildings with lifts and pools commonly run $6,000 to $14,000 per year or more. The building age and complexity drive the number.
