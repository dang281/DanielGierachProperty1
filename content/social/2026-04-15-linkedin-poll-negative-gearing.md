## Caption

Negative gearing gets thrown around in almost every property conversation in Australia. It comes up at barbecues, in federal budgets, and in almost every investment property discussion.

But ask ten people to define it and you will get a range of answers.

The basics: if the costs of owning an investment property (mortgage interest, rates, insurance, management fees, maintenance) are higher than the rental income it produces, the property is negatively geared. That shortfall is a genuine cash loss each year. The offset is that the loss is deductible against your other income, which reduces your tax bill.

Whether that trade-off makes sense depends on what the property does in capital growth terms over time. Strong growth can more than compensate for years of negative cashflow. In a flat or falling market, you carry the losses without the upside.

Brisbane's inner east has delivered strong capital growth over recent years, which is one reason why negative gearing remains a popular strategy here despite rates being materially higher than they were in 2021.

Which of these best describes negative gearing?
