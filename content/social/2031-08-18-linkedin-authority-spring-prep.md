## Caption

The most consistent mistake I see from vendors in August is underestimating how long proper spring preparation takes. By the time they realise the timeline is tight, it often is.

A vendor who wants to launch in late September needs to have made their key decisions by mid-August at the latest. That is not an arbitrary deadline. It is the result of what preparation actually requires when you map it out in sequence.

Decluttering comes first. You cannot properly assess what needs repairing or painting until you can see the property clearly, and it takes longer than most families expect. Minor repairs and maintenance come next, which means engaging trades that may not be immediately available in August. Garden work follows its own timeline. A neglected garden does not transform in a week.

Once the physical preparation is done, styling decisions need to be made. A stylist needs to assess the property, prepare a plan, and book the furniture placement. Photography cannot happen until the property looks the way it will at open homes. Photographs need to be processed and approved. The listing needs to be prepared, reviewed, and scheduled.

All of this is sequential. Each step depends on the one before it. Shortcuts show up in the finished product in ways buyers notice even if they cannot articulate exactly what looks rushed.

The gap between a well-prepared spring launch and a rushed one in the inner east regularly sits between $50,000 and $100,000 in sale result. Sometimes more. That is not a figure I have invented. It is the difference between a campaign that buyers trust from the first open and a campaign that asks buyers to do the work of imagining what the property could be.

Mid-August is the right moment to make these decisions, not late August.
