## Caption

One of the most common misconceptions I encounter from vendors in Queensland is the belief that they can waive the buyer's cooling-off period. They cannot. The five-business-day cooling-off period is a buyer's right under Queensland law. It is not a negotiable contract term. The vendor has no capacity to remove it from a private treaty sale.

What vendors can do is provide the correct pre-contractual documentation, which starts the cooling-off clock accurately. What buyers can do is waive the cooling-off period themselves, in writing, once they have received independent legal advice. That is the buyer's choice, not the vendor's.

The second thing most sellers have backwards is the auction exception. Contracts signed at auction in Queensland have no cooling-off period at all. The contract becomes unconditional at the fall of the hammer. This is one of the genuine advantages of auction for a vendor who wants certainty: the buyer who wins under the hammer cannot change their mind on the Tuesday after.

Understanding the cooling-off framework is not just legal trivia. It has direct implications for how a vendor structures their negotiation with a buyer, what they agree to in terms of settlement timing, and how they manage the period between exchange and unconditional status.

Most sellers discover the detail of this when they are already in a transaction. The better time to understand it is before you sign an agency agreement.
