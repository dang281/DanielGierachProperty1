## Caption

Easements appear on title searches for a large proportion of properties in Brisbane's inner east. The instinctive buyer reaction, on seeing the word "easement" in the contract, is often alarm. That reaction is sometimes warranted and often not. The problem is that most buyers cannot tell which type of easement they are looking at from the title alone.

A drainage easement along the rear boundary of a residential block is one of the most common easements in the inner east. What it means in practice: council has the right to access and maintain the drainage infrastructure that runs through that strip. For most properties, this affects a two-to-three metre strip at the rear. You cannot build a permanent structure within it. You can garden, pave, and use it for most purposes. Its effect on resale value in the inner east is negligible, because informed buyers understand what it is.

An access easement is a fundamentally different matter. This type of easement grants another party, usually an adjoining property owner, the right to cross part of your land. Depending on where it falls, it can constrain how you use your yard, restrict fencing options, and create a potential ongoing relationship with a neighbour that not every buyer wants to take on.

Both appear as "easement" on a title search. Only one of them is usually a concern.
