## Caption

The building and pest report arrives, often as a PDF with forty or fifty pages, and most buyers go straight to the summary page. That is usually the wrong place to start.

The summary page on a building and pest report organises findings into categories: major defects, minor defects, and maintenance items. What it does not do is tell you which items require urgent attention and which are the normal background noise of any established property. A pre-war Queenslander in Hawthorne that has been maintained well will still generate a list of items in a building and pest report. The question is whether those items are expected for the age and type of building, or whether they indicate a problem that affects the purchase decision.

Active timber pest activity is categorically different from a maintenance item. Active termites require treatment, remediation, and an understanding of what damage has been done to the structure. A building inspector's note that gutters need cleaning does not. The two should not be processed with the same level of concern, but the summary page on a standard report presents them with similar formatting.

Reading the full report with specific attention to the inspector's comments around each finding is the only way to understand what you are buying. The report is the inspector's professional opinion. The summary is an index, not a conclusion.
