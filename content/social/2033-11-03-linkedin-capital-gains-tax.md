## Caption

The main residence CGT exemption is not automatic if your property was ever rented. Most vendors find out only when the campaign has already started.

This is one of the more expensive surprises in a property sale, and it is almost entirely avoidable with the right advice before you commit to a campaign. The exemption that most owner-occupiers assume applies fully does not always do so, and the situations where it falls short are more common than vendors expect.

If you held a property for ten years and rented it for three of those years, three-tenths of the capital gain is potentially assessable. Not the full gain, but a meaningful portion of it. The maths depends on the exact period, the cost base, and your individual tax position, but the principle is clear: time spent as a rental reduces the exemption on a pro-rata basis.

The six-year absence rule is another provision that vendors regularly misunderstand. It allows a former principal residence to retain its CGT-exempt status while rented, but it cannot operate alongside another nominated principal residence. If you bought a second home and treated that as your main residence during the rental period, the absence rule generally does not apply to the original property. The conditions are specific, and the consequences of getting them wrong are significant.

Settlement date is the third variable worth knowing about. The capital gain is assessed in the financial year settlement occurs, not exchange. Moving settlement by a few days across 30 June can shift a substantial tax liability into a different year. Whether that is beneficial depends on your overall income position in each year.
