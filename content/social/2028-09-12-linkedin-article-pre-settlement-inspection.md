## Caption

The buyer is entitled to one pre-settlement inspection, usually in the twenty-four hours before settlement. The purpose is to confirm the property is in the same condition as at the date the contract was signed.

That sounds simple until you consider what sellers routinely do in the weeks between contract and settlement. The furniture that was staging the property gets moved out and leaves scuff marks on the walls. The removalists damage a door frame. The dishwasher that was working when the contract was signed develops a fault. The garden, which was immaculate during the campaign, has been left unwatered.

None of these issues are typically deal-breakers, but they all create last-minute negotiations that delay settlement and create stress for both parties. A buyer who walks through on the day before settlement and finds the property in materially different condition from the day they signed has legitimate grounds to raise it.

The practical answer is straightforward. Before the removalists arrive, take a systematic walkthrough with photos. After the removalists leave, take another. Fix any damage before the pre-settlement inspection date. Leave the garden watered. Make sure all inclusions listed in the contract are still present.

The pre-settlement inspection is the last impression the buyer has of the property as a seller. It is worth the hour it takes to get it right.
