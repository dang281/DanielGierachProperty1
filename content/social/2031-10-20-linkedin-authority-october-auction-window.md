## Caption

October is the peak auction month in the inner east. Buyer confidence is at its seasonal high, competition among buyers is deepest, and vendors who have prepared and priced correctly benefit from that environment more than in any other month.

The October auction works when the conditions are right. A property with broad appeal, genuine competition on the buyer side, and a vendor who has set a realistic reserve. When those three things align, auction delivers results that private treaty cannot replicate. The unconditional result, the competition-driven price movement, the certainty of a set date.

But I should be honest about what happens when it does not work. A property that passes in at auction is not a failed property. It is a property that either entered the campaign with a reserve disconnected from the market, or that had a buyer pool smaller than expected. A good agent does not disappear after a pass in. They immediately re-engage the highest bidder or the most qualified registered buyer and move into private treaty negotiation with real information about where the market has set the price.

The vendors who are sometimes better served by private treaty even in October are those with a narrow buyer pool, a home that requires a specific type of buyer, or a vendor who cannot tolerate the public nature of a pass in. Those are legitimate situations. Method should always serve the property and the vendor's circumstances, not the agency's auction clearance rate.
