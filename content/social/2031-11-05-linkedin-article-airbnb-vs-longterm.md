## Caption

The gross yield from short-term rental can look compelling on paper. The nightly rate, multiplied out across a year at a reasonable occupancy assumption, produces a number that often looks significantly better than the long-term weekly rent for the same property.

The problem is that gross yield is not the number that matters.

After platform fees, which typically run at 15 to 20 per cent of gross revenue, ongoing cleaning costs between every stay, elevated insurance premiums for short-term rental properties, higher maintenance turnover from more frequent guest use, and the cost of furnishing and restocking consumables, the difference in net return compared to a well-managed long-term tenancy is often within a few thousand dollars per year. For some properties in average locations, the long-term tenancy actually delivers a better net position with significantly less management complexity.

The properties where short-term rental genuinely outperforms are those with a specific location or lifestyle advantage, close to the CBD, near the river, or with a setting that commands a consistent premium above the average nightly rate. In those cases the occupancy rates and nightly pricing are high enough to push the net return meaningfully above the long-term equivalent.

For most standard investment properties in the inner east, the analysis is tighter than the headline yield figures suggest. Worth running the real numbers before committing to a short-term strategy.
