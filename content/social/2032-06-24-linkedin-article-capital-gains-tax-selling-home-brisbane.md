## Caption

The assumption most Brisbane homeowners make is that selling their home means no capital gains tax. In many cases that assumption is correct. In a meaningful number of cases, it is not, and the cost of discovering that late is significant.

The main residence exemption is real and broad, but it has edges. If you have rented out your home at any point during your ownership, the exemption is proportional, not absolute. If you have used part of the property for income-producing purposes, a home office counted as a dedicated business space for example, that can affect your position. If you bought the property and it was not immediately your main residence, the clock starts from when you moved in, not when you settled. And if you are selling an investment property that you also lived in at some stage, the calculation becomes a matter of record-keeping and accountant judgment.

None of this is Daniel-Gierach-the-agent's area of legal or financial advice. What I can tell you is that every seller in Brisbane should be having this conversation with their accountant before they sign an agency agreement, not after. The timing of a sale can affect the CGT outcome, and that is a decision worth making with the right information in front of you.

I have written a plain-language guide to how CGT applies when selling a home in Brisbane, covering the main residence exemption, the partial exemption scenarios, and the questions to ask your accountant.
