## Caption

Most buyers sign the contract first and call their solicitor second. That sequence is backwards.

A solicitor's job is to review the contract before you sign it, not explain what you agreed to after the fact. By the time you are sitting down with your solicitor reading through a signed contract, you have already accepted the conditions, the inclusions, and any special conditions buried in the schedule. There is nothing to negotiate. The review is purely informational at that point.

What a solicitor looks for before you sign is materially different from what they can do after. Before signing, they can identify unusual special conditions, flag whether the deposit structure creates risk, question inclusions that are not clearly described, and recommend amendments. After signing, they are administering a deal you have already committed to.

The other question buyers wrestle with is whether to use a solicitor or a conveyancer. In Queensland, both are licensed to handle property conveyancing. The distinction matters most when the transaction is complex: if there is a related party arrangement, a deceased estate, a trust structure, or unusual conditions, a solicitor's broader legal training is the relevant factor. For a standard residential purchase with clean conditions, a licensed conveyancer handles the process competently.

The article covers how to choose between the two, what to ask before you appoint anyone, and what to send your legal representative before you make an offer, not after you accept one.
