## Caption

Norman Park's position between Hawthorne and Camp Hill is not incidental. It is exactly the reason the suburb commands the prices it does.

The Whites Hill State College catchment runs across Norman Park, and its influence on buyer behaviour is consistent and measurable. Families who have children approaching secondary school, or who are buying ahead of that point, make the catchment boundary a non-negotiable part of their property search. The streets that fall inside it attract a buyer who has narrowed their search specifically to that zone and is prepared to compete for what comes up.

Whites Hill Reserve gives the suburb a physical lung that borders do not always provide. The reserve trails connect into the surrounding green space and give families a recreational infrastructure that sits outside the property boundary. The elevated character streets, many of them lined with Queenslanders and pre-war timber homes, carry the kind of architectural detail that the suburb's buyer profile specifically seeks. These are buyers who are not looking for new construction. They want original character, well-maintained, in a street that has held its standard.

With Easter approaching at the end of March, the window for vendors who want the full autumn market is narrowing. The buyers who are actively looking now are the buyers who will make decisions before the break. Well-prepared properties in Norman Park do not sit on the market for long.
