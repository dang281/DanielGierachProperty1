## Caption

Net equity is not sale price minus loan balance. Most vendors who haven't sold before are surprised by how much the actual number differs from what they calculated on the back of an envelope.

The costs that reduce net proceeds are: agent commission, marketing costs, conveyancing fees, lender discharge fees (these vary by lender and are not always disclosed upfront), and if you are on a fixed-rate loan, break costs that can run into tens of thousands of dollars depending on how far through the fixed term you are and where rates are now relative to when you fixed.

Stamp duty on your next purchase also needs to sit in the same calculation if you are selling to buy. Vendors in Brisbane's inner east who are upgrading within the same suburb often underestimate the stamp duty bill on a $2 to $3 million purchase, which can be significant. Running the full in-and-out calculation before setting a sale price expectation is not optional. It is how you avoid getting to settlement and being short.

The fixed-rate break cost is the one that catches people most consistently right now. If you fixed your rate in 2021 or 2022 at a low rate and you are selling before that term expires, the break cost is calculated by the lender, not you, and the number can be significant.

I've laid out the full calculation, the order in which to gather the numbers, and the questions to ask your lender before you commit to a sale timeline.
