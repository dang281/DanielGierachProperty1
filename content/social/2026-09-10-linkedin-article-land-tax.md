## Caption

Land tax in Queensland is calculated on unimproved land value, not the market sale price of your property. That distinction matters because as inner-east Brisbane land values have risen substantially over recent years, more investors have found themselves crossing the taxable threshold without necessarily expecting to.

The Queensland Revenue Office sets the land tax threshold for individual investors, and as land values appreciate the number of investors who cross it continues to grow. If you bought in Hawthorne or Norman Park several years ago, the unimproved land value component of your holding may have moved materially, even if you haven't thought about it in those terms. Worth checking with your accountant if you haven't reviewed this recently. Note: the specific threshold figures should be verified against current Queensland Revenue Office published rates before relying on them.

There are two additional points the article covers that often get missed. The first is the settlement adjustment: land tax is apportioned between buyer and seller at settlement, so the timing of your sale within the financial year affects how it lands. The second is the difference between market value and unimproved land value. These are different figures and the tax calculation uses the latter.

When land tax is combined with rising holding costs, higher maintenance on older inner-east stock, and changes in borrowing costs, the calculation about whether to continue holding a negatively geared investment has shifted for some owners. Not for all of them, but it's worth running the numbers deliberately rather than assuming the strategy that made sense at purchase still makes sense now.
