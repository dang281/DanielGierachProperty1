## Caption

Queensland land tax catches a lot of investors off guard. Not because the rules are obscure, but because the threshold at which it kicks in is lower than most people expect, and the way land values have moved across the inner east over the past decade means properties that were well under the threshold a few years ago are no longer.

The part that trips people up most often is that land tax is assessed on unimproved land value, not purchase price or market value. Your house might be worth $1.8 million, but the land component the OSR uses to calculate your liability is a different number assessed separately by the Valuer-General. And if you own multiple properties in Queensland, all those land values are aggregated. That's where investors with two or three properties suddenly find themselves with a significant annual bill they hadn't planned for.

There's also the question of how land tax interacts with your investment decision-making. If you're considering selling one of multiple Queensland properties, understanding how your remaining holdings will be assessed afterward is worth knowing before you settle on a price expectation.

It's a dry topic, but it's one that affects cash flow in ways that go unaccounted for until the assessment notice arrives.

I've written through the main points in the article below. Well worth reading before end of financial year.
