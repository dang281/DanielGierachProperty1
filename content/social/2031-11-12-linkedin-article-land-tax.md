## Caption

Queensland land tax is assessed on the combined unimproved land value across all Queensland holdings, not per property. That distinction matters a great deal for inner east investors who built a portfolio over the past decade.

An investor who purchased a property in Norman Park in 2018 and added a second in Coorparoo in 2022 might not have been above the land tax threshold when either property was bought. But after several years of strong land value growth across the inner east, the combined unimproved land value of those two holdings may now sit well above the threshold. And they are receiving unexpected assessments after each EOFY without having planned for the liability.

The threshold for land tax in Queensland applies to the total value of all Queensland land you hold, not each individual parcel. The rate then applies progressively above that threshold. Investors who have not revisited this calculation recently, particularly those who purchased pre-2025 and have seen significant value growth, should be running this check with their accountant before the next EOFY cycle.

The other detail that catches investors off guard is that the unimproved land value used for the assessment is the Queensland Government's own valuation figure, which may differ from recent comparable sales data and is updated regularly.

The article below explains how Queensland land tax is calculated, what the current thresholds are, and what steps investors with multiple holdings should take to understand their current position.
