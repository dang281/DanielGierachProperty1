## Caption

Some of the most valuable homes in Brisbane's inner east sit under a heritage overlay. Many of their owners have quietly assumed that the overlay means significant renovation is off the table. That assumption costs them both money and opportunity.

A heritage overlay in Brisbane constrains the method and materials of renovation work, particularly to the street-facing facade and structural elements of the original dwelling. It does not prevent significant value from being added to the property. Extensions to the rear, internal reconfiguration, new kitchens and bathrooms, decking, landscaping, and secondary structures are all possible within a heritage overlay, subject to council approval.

The properties where renovation stalls are typically the ones where the owner or their builder approached the project without understanding the overlay constraints in advance, sought approval for something council was never going to grant, and then treated the rejection as proof that nothing was possible. Starting from the right position, with a designer who understands the heritage framework in the relevant suburb, produces a very different outcome.

Character Queenslanders in Morningside, Norman Park, Hawthorne, and Balmoral with heritage overlays are not renovation-proof assets. They are assets where the renovation approach requires more planning than a standard knock-and-rebuild site, and where that planning investment is almost always justified by the premium the finished result commands.

The article at the link below steps through how heritage overlays work in the Brisbane context and what inner east owners with character homes should know before they start a planning conversation.
