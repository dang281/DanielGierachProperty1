## Caption

Off-market approaches happen most often in winter, when buyer demand is concentrated and specific buyers are willing to pay to avoid competition. It is also when some homeowners are most tempted to accept, because the timing feels right and the process feels simple.

Here is what most vendors do not hear clearly enough: an off-market sale almost always means selling for less than a well-run campaign would achieve. The mechanism is straightforward. Price in property is primarily a function of competition between buyers. If only one buyer is in the room, there is no competition. The price reflects that buyer's offer, not the market's ceiling.

"We have a buyer ready to go" is a phrase agents use when approaching vendors before a campaign. That buyer is real. But the buyer is there because they have identified an opportunity to buy before the market gets to see the property. That is good for them. It is not necessarily good for you.

The situations where off-market makes sense for the vendor are limited but real. Where the vendor genuinely values privacy above price, where there are tenants in place and minimising inspections is a priority, where a quick unconditional result matters more than an optimised price. These are legitimate reasons, but they should be the vendor's choice, not an outcome they drifted into.

If you receive an off-market approach and want a second opinion on whether it represents fair value for your property, that conversation costs nothing.
