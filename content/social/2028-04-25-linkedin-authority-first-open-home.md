## Caption

The first open home is the only one that happens in a vacuum. Every subsequent inspection is coloured by what buyers already know or think they know about the property. That first Saturday is the one moment when the market forms its initial impression with no prior opinion to anchor against.

That impression is durable. Buyers talk to each other at open homes, in Facebook groups, in conversations with their buyers' agents. A property that draws a strong crowd in week one creates the perception of competition. Buyers who were on the fence become more decisive. Buyers who had mentally placed the property in the too-hard basket reconsider. The energy of a well-attended first open home is something that gets reported back to vendors not just through numbers but through the tone of agent feedback.

A quiet first open home creates the opposite effect. The buyers who attended tell their network. A second or third week with low numbers confirms the initial read. Even if week two is genuinely better, it is fighting against an established perception. A price reduction can follow, but by then the market has already filed the property.

The work that determines how week one goes happens before the property is listed. The presentation, the preparation, the timing of the launch relative to comparable competition in the area, the briefing of buyers who have been waiting for something like this. That preparation is not a luxury. It is the campaign.

If the first open home does not go well, recovering is possible but it is harder than getting it right from the start. danielgierach.com
