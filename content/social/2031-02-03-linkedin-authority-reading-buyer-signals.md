## Caption

After enough open homes in the inner east, the difference between a curiosity inspector and a genuine buyer becomes fairly readable. Not perfectly, but clearly enough to change how you follow up and how you report to the vendor.

The curiosity inspector tends to move quickly through the property, spending most of their time in the living areas and kitchen. They ask general questions: how old is the roof, what are the council rates, when was the last sale. Their questions are about the property as a concept rather than as a purchase. They often come in groups, spend time discussing the neighbourhood, and leave without asking anything specific about the contract process.

The genuine buyer behaves differently. They slow down in the rooms that matter most to them, often returning to the same room twice. They look at storage. They measure doorways. They check the aspect of the back yard and they look at the neighbouring properties with purpose. Their questions shift toward the transactional: what is the vendor's preferred timeline, has there been any prior contract activity, are there any special conditions on the contract.

None of this is a guarantee. Buyers who look casual can surprise you, and buyers who look ready sometimes walk. But after years of inner east open homes, the read is accurate often enough to shape the follow-up strategy. Knowing which attendees are worth calling on Monday morning, and with what information, is part of what moves a campaign toward an offer.

Vendors do not always see this work happening. But it is happening at every open home.
