## Caption

A buyer makes an offer of $1.52 million, subject to finance and building and pest. Another buyer offers $1.48 million, unconditional. The vendor takes the $1.48 million.

From the outside, this looks irrational. From the vendor's position, it is straightforward.

A conditional offer comes with a two to three week window during which the entire sale can unravel. The finance condition can lapse if the bank does not approve. The building and pest condition can be exercised if the inspector finds something the buyer decides they do not want to accept. In both cases the vendor is back at the start, having lost weeks of their campaign window and often their best-positioned buyer, who has moved on to something else in the meantime.

An unconditional offer eliminates that window entirely. The moment it is signed by both parties, the transaction proceeds to settlement. There is no exit clause for the buyer based on finance or inspection outcomes.

For a vendor in Autumn 2033 who wants certainty, the price difference between conditional and unconditional is a premium for peace of mind. And for inner east buyers who have the financial position to go unconditional, it is a genuine competitive advantage in a market where multiple offers are common.

The mechanics of structuring an unconditional offer correctly, and the risk management that needs to happen before you do, are worth understanding properly.
