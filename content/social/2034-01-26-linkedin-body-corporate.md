## Caption

Two strata properties. Both with a $3,000 annual levy. One has $500,000 in the sinking fund. The other has $50,000. These are not the same purchase.

The annual levy tells you what you will pay each quarter to run the building today. The sinking fund balance tells you whether the building can absorb the capital works that are coming in the next three, five, or ten years. A building with a thin sinking fund will eventually need to fund major repairs through special levies, which fall directly on owners at the time, regardless of when they bought in.

This distinction matters enormously, and it is often missed at inspection. The levy figure is on the marketing material. The sinking fund balance is in the body corporate records, which typically arrive during the finance period, well after most buyers have already decided they want the property.

By the time the records land, the emotional commitment is made and the practical leverage for reconsidering or renegotiating has mostly passed. Buyers who understand what to look for in body corporate records before they make an offer are in a considerably better position than those who read them for the first time during finance.

The article covers what to check in a body corporate search, what the sinking fund balance actually tells you, and how to read the records before you are emotionally committed to the deal.
