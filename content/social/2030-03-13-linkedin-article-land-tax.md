## Caption

Land tax in Queensland is calculated on the combined unimproved value of all your Queensland holdings. As inner east land values have risen over recent years, more investors are crossing the threshold without realising it.

This is one of those costs that arrives as a surprise bill rather than a planned expense. Investors who bought in the inner east four or five years ago may have been comfortably below the threshold at purchase. The same portfolio today, valued at current land rates, may sit above it.

A few things worth understanding:

Land tax applies to the unimproved land value, not the total property value. The unimproved land value is set by the Queensland Valuer-General and is typically lower than market value, but in high-demand suburbs like Bulimba, Norman Park, and Camp Hill, these values have moved considerably.

The tax-free threshold applies only to your primary place of residence. Investment properties, including holiday rentals and properties in a company or trust structure, are assessed separately and at different rates.

Aggregation across multiple properties is where many investors are caught. Two properties each sitting below the threshold can combine to push the portfolio above it.

The Queensland Office of State Revenue assesses and issues land tax annually. If you receive a notice for the first time, it is worth understanding whether the assessed value is correct before paying. Reviews and objections are available.

The practical step: ask your accountant to check your current land tax exposure before you buy the next property, not after.
