## Caption

Land tax in Queensland is assessed on the combined unimproved value of all land holdings in the state, not the value of each property individually. For investors who own a single property, that distinction is sometimes academic. For investors who have built a portfolio across multiple inner east suburbs over a decade of capital growth, it has become a materially more significant cost than it was when they bought.

The inner east has seen substantial land value growth over the past several years. Unimproved land values in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Morningside, and Camp Hill have moved considerably. For investors who bought when those values were lower, the combination of land value growth and the stacking of multiple holdings has pushed them into higher land tax brackets without a corresponding review of whether the returns still justify the position.

The threshold question for most investors is not whether they owe land tax. It is whether they know what they owe and have factored it correctly into their net return calculation. Investors who hold multiple properties in the inner east and have not revisited their land tax position in the past two years are worth flagging this for.

The article covers how Queensland land tax is calculated, the thresholds, how multiple properties stack, and the questions to ask before adding another property to an existing portfolio.
