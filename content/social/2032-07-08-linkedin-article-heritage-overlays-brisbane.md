## Caption

A heritage overlay in Brisbane's inner east does not prevent renovation. That is the single most important thing most buyers get wrong about these properties.

What it actually requires is Brisbane City Council approval for external changes to street-facing character elements. Roof form, front facades, verandahs, and streetscape features are covered. Internal renovations, additions to the rear, and changes not visible from the street typically proceed without heritage approval at all.

The problem is that most buyers cannot tell which properties are affected without checking the BCC planning maps. A character-style home and a heritage-overlay home can look identical from the footpath. The planning status only becomes visible when you check the property report.

There are two implications worth understanding before you buy or sell.

First, a heritage overlay is not a barrier to a modern kitchen, a bathroom renovation, or a rear extension. The scope for internal improvement is often much broader than buyers assume.

Second, character homes in the inner east frequently sell at a premium to non-character homes of similar size on the same street. The streetscape appeal and perceived quality of the older build tend to hold their own in the market.

The article covers how to check overlay status on the BCC planning maps, what triggers an approval process, and what sellers should disclose.
