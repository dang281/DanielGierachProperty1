## Caption

Heritage overlay is one of the most misunderstood designations in Brisbane property. Most buyers assume it means they cannot touch the property. Most sellers assume it will reduce their buyer pool. Neither is accurate.

What heritage overlay actually does is require Council approval for changes to the external character of the property. Internal renovations typically proceed without heritage approval. The weatherboards, the verandah, the streetscape appearance: that is what Council is protecting. What you do inside is largely your own business.

For buyers, this matters because it affects what the property can become. A character home on a 600-square-metre block in Norman Park with a heritage overlay is different from an identical-looking property on the same block without the overlay, even if they look the same from the street. The overlay constrains what a developer or significant renovator can do, which is why those properties sometimes sell for less than the land value would otherwise suggest.

For sellers, an overlay is worth understanding before you set a price guide. In some inner east suburbs, the buyer pool for character homes responds positively to the designation, because it signals the character of the street will be protected. In other suburbs and price points, it narrows the buyer pool by ruling out buyers who want to build new.

The full breakdown of how overlays work, what they restrict, and how to research the status of a specific property is in the article below.
