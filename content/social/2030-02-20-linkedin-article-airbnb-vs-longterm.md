## Caption

Short-term rental adds $15,000 to $25,000 per year in extra costs that most investors do not factor in before comparing gross figures.

The comparison most investors make is: short-term rental income at $X per night, long-term rental at $Y per week. At face value, short-term often looks more attractive. But that comparison only holds if you are comparing net figures, and most people aren't.

The costs that close the gap on short-term rental:

Cleaning between every stay. In the inner east where short-term properties typically target the premium leisure and corporate market, professional cleaning after each booking is not optional. At $200 to $400 per clean and multiple stays per week, this alone is a significant line item.

Management fees for short-term platforms and local property managers. These typically run higher than long-term management fees as a percentage and involve more active engagement.

Linen, consumables, and restocking. Guests expect hotel-standard presentation. The cost of maintaining that standard across a full year is consistently underestimated.

Insurance. Standard landlord insurance does not cover short-term rental. A specific short-term rental policy costs more, and for good reason.

Vacancy. Short-term rental income is not linear. Low seasons, weeks without bookings, and the time between the last guest checking out and the next checking in all create income gaps that don't exist on a signed twelve-month lease.

Beyond the numbers, there is a resale consideration. A history of short-term rental reduces your cost base through depreciation adjustments and may affect how lenders value the property for a future buyer.
