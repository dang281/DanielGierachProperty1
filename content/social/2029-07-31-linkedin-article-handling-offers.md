## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright closes the conversation. There is almost always a better response, and most sellers do not know what it is.

A flat rejection sends the buyer elsewhere, often permanently. A counter, even one that holds firm on price, keeps the conversation alive and signals that the seller is serious without being dismissive. The difference in outcome between those two responses can be material.

Price is also only one variable in offer comparison. An offer $30,000 below asking on unconditional terms often represents better value than one at full asking with a finance condition, a building and pest condition, and a 90-day settlement. The conditional offer carries real risk: finance can fall through, pest reports can produce renegotiations, and extended settlements create uncertainty for the seller's own plans. When you strip out the conditions, the two offers often look very different.

Countering without losing ground or goodwill is a skill. It involves understanding what the buyer actually needs, which is not always a lower price. Sometimes it is a longer settlement, an earlier possession date, or certainty that the deal will proceed. Knowing that creates room to move in one direction while holding firm in another.

The article covers the full framework for evaluating and responding to offers, including the scenarios that most sellers handle poorly under time pressure.
