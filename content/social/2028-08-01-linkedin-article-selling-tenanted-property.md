## Caption

Twenty-four hours written notice is required for every single open home inspection when a property is tenanted. Not a blanket permission at the start of the campaign. Twenty-four hours, every time.

That changes how a campaign runs. Coordinating inspection access around a tenant's schedule, ensuring notice is properly served in writing for each inspection date, managing the relationship between the tenant's right to peaceful enjoyment and the seller's need to present the property to buyers. These are real constraints, and sellers who underestimate them find their campaign schedule running behind within the first week.

The settlement timing question is more serious. If the tenant is on a fixed-term lease that runs past the proposed settlement date, the buyer takes over that lease. Not vacant possession. The new owner inherits the tenancy, the rent, and the tenant. Many owner-occupier buyers will not accept this, which means a fixed-term lease past settlement can remove a significant portion of the buyer pool before the first inspection.

The answer is not to wait for the lease to expire in every case. But it means the settlement date and the lease expiry need to be aligned before the campaign launches, not discovered mid-negotiation.

The full article walks through notice requirements, lease timing, tenant cooperation, and how to structure the campaign around a tenanted property.
