## Caption

The vendor cannot waive the cooling-off period for a residential property sale in Queensland. It is a statutory right and it is not a negotiating chip.

This comes up in competitive sale situations, sometimes presented as a show of good faith or as a condition of the vendor accepting the offer. A clause purporting to waive the cooling-off period on a residential sale is likely unenforceable. If a vendor or their agent presents this as a requirement, the right response is to seek legal advice, not to sign.

What the cooling-off period actually provides is five business days from the date the contract is received by the buyer or the buyer's solicitor. During that window, a buyer can exit the contract by serving a notice on the vendor, at a cost of 0.25 per cent of the purchase price. On a $900,000 purchase, that is $2,250 to walk away if circumstances change or if something material comes to light in those first days.

Auction contracts are different. There is no cooling-off period on a property purchased at auction. A buyer who signs a contract after an auction has no statutory right to exit. The distinction matters because some buyers assume the cooling-off protection applies universally, which it does not.

The article covers how the cooling-off period is calculated, what triggers the start of the window, how to exercise the right correctly, and how the auction exception operates in practice.
