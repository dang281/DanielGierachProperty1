## Caption

Most buyers call their solicitor after they have already signed the contract. That is the standard sequence, and it is the wrong one.

By the time a solicitor sees the contract, you are already bound. The cooling-off period is running. The finance clause deadline has been set. Any special conditions that should have been included, or removed, are now part of a signed legal document. Changing them requires the vendor's agreement.

A solicitor or conveyancer who reviews the contract before you sign catches the things that cost money after you sign. Missing inclusions, a finance period that does not give your broker enough time, a building and pest clause that is worded too loosely to protect you. These are not rare edge cases. They come up regularly, and they are straightforward to address before signing, and complicated to address after.

In Queensland, the distinction between a solicitor and a conveyancer also matters. They are not interchangeable. One can provide legal advice; the other cannot. Knowing which you need, and when to bring them in, is the kind of detail that does not make headlines but absolutely affects outcomes.

I have written a full breakdown of the difference between solicitors and conveyancers in Queensland, when to use each, and why the sequence matters. Worth reading before you make an offer, not after.
