## Caption

Queensland land tax is calculated on the combined unimproved land value of all taxable Queensland holdings, not on each property individually. Rising inner east land values are pushing more investors over the threshold without them realising it until the assessment arrives.

That distinction in how the calculation works is the one most commonly misunderstood. An investor with two Queensland investment properties does not have two separate land tax assessments, one for each property. The Queensland Revenue Office aggregates the unimproved land values of all taxable holdings and applies the land tax rate to the combined figure. An investor whose individual properties each sit below the threshold can still have a land tax liability when those values are combined. That is the situation an increasing number of inner east investors are finding themselves in as land values have risen.

The primary residence is exempt from land tax and does not form part of the combined assessment. All Queensland investment properties, including those in regional areas, are included in the combined calculation. The threshold that applied to a portfolio in 2027 may now be exceeded by the same portfolio in 2031 without the investor having purchased anything additional, simply because the underlying land values have increased through annual valuations.

Mid-May is when annual financial reviews typically surface this. An assessment that arrives after EOFY is not the first point at which an investor should be aware of their land tax position. Understanding the exposure before the assessment allows for planning rather than reaction.

The article covers how the Queensland land tax calculation works, what is exempt, and how to check current land valuations before the assessment period.
