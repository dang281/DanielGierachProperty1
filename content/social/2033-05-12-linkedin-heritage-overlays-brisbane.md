## Caption

A heritage overlay does not prevent renovation. This surprises a lot of buyers and puts off some who would otherwise be well-suited to these properties.

What a heritage overlay actually does is require Brisbane City Council approval for changes to the street-facing character of the building. The front facade, the roof line, the verandah, the elements that define the home's contribution to the streetscape. Internal renovations, kitchen rebuilds, bathroom updates, extensions to the rear, and additions at the back of the block typically proceed without heritage approval at all. You are not renovating in a museum.

This is relevant in suburbs like Bulimba, Hawthorne, and Norman Park where pre-war and early post-war homes carry heritage listings. These are some of the most desirable streets in the inner east, and buyers who understand the overlay rules can see past the perception of restriction to what is actually a workable and often excellent purchase.

The flip side matters for vendors too. If you have a heritage-listed home and you've made changes to the street-facing elements without approval, that is something a solicitor needs to identify and a vendor needs to disclose. Council records, old approvals, and unapproved works are all discoverable, and they become leverage for a buyer in a contract negotiation.

Understanding the overlay rules, on both sides, is part of making good decisions about these properties.
