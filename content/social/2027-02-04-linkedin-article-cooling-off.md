## Caption

In Queensland, a buyer who purchases a residential property through private treaty has a statutory five-business-day cooling-off period. During this time, they can withdraw from the contract. The seller cannot remove this right.

That last point is the one that surprises people. A seller cannot ask a buyer to waive cooling-off as a condition of accepting their offer. It is a statutory right that belongs to the buyer, and it exists regardless of what the contract says.

If a buyer exercises the cooling-off right and withdraws, they pay a penalty of 0.25% of the purchase price. On a $1.2 million purchase, that is $3,000. The property then returns to market.

Two important exceptions. First, auctions: contracts signed at auction have no cooling-off period. The buyer is unconditional from the moment the hammer falls. This is one of the reasons auctions are attractive to sellers who want certainty. Second, the cooling-off period applies to residential property, not commercial.

For buyers, the cooling-off period provides a window to confirm finance, complete due diligence, and review the contract with your solicitor. It is not a substitute for those steps, but it is a backstop.

For sellers, understanding that a signed private treaty contract still has a five-business-day cooling-off window helps set realistic expectations about when a deal is truly done.
