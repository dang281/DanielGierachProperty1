## Caption

Buyers often think offers disappear into a black box and the agent returns with an arbitrary counter. Sellers often think every offer below their number is an insult. Both interpretations miss how the process actually works.

When a buyer submits an offer, the agent's first job is to understand it, not just relay it. What is the buyer's actual ceiling? Is this their opening position or is it close to the top of their range? Is the conditional structure something the seller can accept, or does a three-week finance period represent real risk given where rates are? The answers to those questions shape how the conversation with the seller goes.

When the agent presents to the seller, the job is to give context, not opinion. Here is what the market is doing. Here is what comparable sales tell us. Here is what this buyer's position signals about their motivation. The decision is the seller's. The agent's role is to make sure that decision is made with accurate information rather than emotion or hope.

Where negotiations fail most often is when both sides are anchored to a number that was formed without reference to the current comparable evidence. The buyer is anchored to what they paid elsewhere. The seller is anchored to what they need to fund their next purchase. Neither is the market.

The market is what comparable, informed buyers in this suburb, in this condition, with this land size, are actually paying right now.
