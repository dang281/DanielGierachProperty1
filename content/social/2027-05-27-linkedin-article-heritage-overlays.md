## Caption

A heritage overlay on a Brisbane property does not mean you cannot renovate. What it means is that you need Council approval for changes to the external character of the building, and that some alterations are restricted or subject to more involved assessment. That distinction matters significantly for buyers considering a purchase.

In the inner east, heritage overlays appear frequently on Queenslanders and character cottages in suburbs like Hawthorne, Balmoral, Bulimba, and Norman Park. An overlay on a well-maintained Queenslander in Hawthorne is typically manageable. Many buyers have renovated these properties successfully, adding contemporary extensions to the rear while retaining the street-facing facade as required. Council's overlay provisions are not designed to freeze a house in time. They are designed to preserve the external character that defines the streetscape.

An overlay on a smaller character cottage in a more tightly controlled precinct requires more careful due diligence before you sign. The scope of what is permissible varies depending on the precinct category, the existing condition of the building, and the nature of the works you are planning. A buyer who intends to add a second storey, alter the roofline, or remove existing architectural elements should get specific advice from a town planner before they commit.

For buyers who are purchasing an overlaid property with renovation intentions, the article below walks through what the overlay actually restricts, what remains permissible, and what questions to ask before the building and pest inspection.
