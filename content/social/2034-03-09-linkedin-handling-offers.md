## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright closes the negotiation. Countering it keeps the deal alive.

This is one of the most consequential moments in a property campaign, and it happens more often in a way that costs vendors money than most people realise. A buyer makes a first offer at a number below the vendor's expectation. The vendor, feeling insulted, instructs the agent to decline without a counter. The buyer, having received no signal of what the vendor will accept, moves on to the next property. The deal dies.

The alternative is a counter that signals the vendor's genuine position without giving everything away. It tells the buyer that negotiation is possible, which keeps them engaged. It gives the agent information about how motivated the buyer is and how far they will move. And it preserves the relationship between buyer and vendor through a process that can get uncomfortable when it is handled without care.

The difference between a vendor achieving their target and missing it by $20,000 often comes down to one response to one early offer. The agent's role in that moment is to advise the vendor clearly on what the counter should look like and why.

The full article steps through how offer negotiations actually work: what the agent does, how counters are structured, what the negotiation looks like when there are multiple buyers, and the mistakes that most commonly cost vendors money.
