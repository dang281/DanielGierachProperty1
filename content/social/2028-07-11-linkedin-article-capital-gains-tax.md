## Caption

The main residence exemption for capital gains tax is not automatic. That is the part most sellers find out too late.

If a property was ever rented, used as an investment, or claimed as a home office for business purposes, the full exemption may not apply. The proportion of time it was used for non-residential purposes can attract a partial CGT liability, calculated on the original purchase price and the sale proceeds. On an inner east property that has grown significantly in value, a partial CGT liability is not a small number.

The six-year absence rule adds another layer. If you rented the property while living elsewhere, CGT exemption can apply for up to six years, but only under specific conditions and only if no other property was nominated as your main residence during that period.

None of this is unusual. Many inner-east sellers have owned their property for fifteen to twenty years and had periods where it was rented. What is unusual is discovering the liability after you have already accepted an offer and agreed to a settlement date.

The time to model your CGT position is before the campaign, not after the contract is signed.

The full article covers the main residence exemption, the absence rule, and when to engage an accountant before you list.
