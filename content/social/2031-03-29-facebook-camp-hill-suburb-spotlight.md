## Caption

Camp Hill has demonstrated the same property behaviour across multiple market cycles, and the pattern is consistent enough to be instructive: when the broader market softens, Camp Hill holds. When the broader market strengthens, Camp Hill leads.

The reasons are structural. The Whites Hill State College catchment provides a floor of demand that is not driven by sentiment or interest rate cycles. Families who need to be within the catchment boundary will pay to be there regardless of what the Reserve Bank has done in the preceding six months. That demand floor means Camp Hill vendors are not exposed to the buyer hesitancy that affects suburbs without comparable structural demand drivers.

The hill streets, the elevated ridge running above Old Cleveland Road with views north and west, represent the suburb's clearest premium. Properties on those streets have consistently outperformed the suburb median because the combination of views, character architecture, and the Old Cleveland Road strip within walking distance is not replicated in adjacent suburbs at the same price. Norman Park buyers who narrow their search to the catchment boundary, and find they can access the Camp Hill ridge streets instead, frequently make that substitution willingly.

At the end of Q1, the autumn market has delivered its most active weeks. Vendors who listed in February and March have had the full benefit of peak buyer attention. For those who are still preparing, the weeks immediately after Easter remain productive, though the buyer pool has thinned relative to what March provided.
