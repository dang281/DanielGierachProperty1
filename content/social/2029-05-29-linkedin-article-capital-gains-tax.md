## Caption

The main residence exemption protects most homeowners from capital gains tax when they sell. But it is not automatic and it does not always apply in full.

If a property was ever rented, even partially, the exemption is proportional. The rental years count toward the taxable portion of the capital gain. A home that was rented for three of the twelve years it was owned carries a partial CGT liability, even if the vendor moved back in before selling.

The six-year absence rule allows a property to retain its main residence status while the owner lives elsewhere, but it has conditions. If another property was nominated as the main residence during the absence period, the six-year exemption does not apply. This catches investors who own multiple properties and assumed the rule covered both.

Settlement date is the other variable worth raising with an accountant before setting the campaign timeline. A settlement on 1 July rather than 28 June means the capital gain falls into the following financial year, which can shift a significant tax liability forward by twelve months. The property value does not change. The cash flow timing does.

None of this is financial advice. It is a reason to have the CGT conversation with your accountant before the campaign launches, not after the contract is signed.
