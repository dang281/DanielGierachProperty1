## Caption

On a $1.1 million owner-occupier purchase in Queensland, transfer duty adds approximately $43,350 to your cost of purchase. Most buyers underestimate this by $10,000 or more.

That gap is not trivial. It affects borrowing capacity calculations, cash reserve requirements, and the realistic upper limit of what a buyer can actually afford. Lenders do not include transfer duty in the loan. It comes out of your available funds on settlement day, alongside legal fees, building and pest inspection costs, and any immediate post-settlement expenses.

The duty rate in Queensland is calculated on a sliding scale. Owner-occupier concessions apply if you intend to live in the property and you are not also claiming a first home concession. The rate increases as the purchase price rises, which means the jump from $900,000 to $1.2 million is not just $300,000 in purchase price. It is also a meaningful step up in the duty calculation.

Investors pay more than owner-occupiers at the same price point, because the owner-occupier concession does not apply. On a $1.1 million investment purchase, the duty figure rises to approximately $47,775. If you are buying in joint names and one buyer is owner-occupier and one is not, the calculation becomes more complex. A solicitor should confirm the applicable rate before you sign.

The article at the link has a full breakdown of the Queensland transfer duty rates, the concession thresholds, and a worked example at several price points common in the inner east Brisbane market.
