## Caption

Two weeks on from the Closing Ceremony, and the Games discussion in property circles has largely been about what happened to prices. The more useful conversation is about what happened to the city.

The infrastructure delivered in the lead-up to 2032 changed daily life in Brisbane's inner east in ways that are already visible. The Cross River Rail project improved access between the inner east and the CBD meaningfully. The Gabba precinct works added public space and transport connections that were not there before. The city-wide transport investment that came with the Games preparation made a number of inner east suburbs more accessible to people who commute or work across multiple locations.

None of that translates into a specific price number. Anyone claiming otherwise is guessing. But the relationship between liveability and saleability is real and it is not complicated: suburbs that are easier to live in tend to attract more buyers, and more buyers tend to produce better outcomes for sellers over time.

The legacy effect on Brisbane's inner east is more likely to be felt through that mechanism than through any single dramatic market shift. The city is easier to live in than it was five years ago. The suburbs in the inner east that benefited most directly from the transport and precinct investment are carrying that improvement into every future campaign.

That is not a prediction. It is an observation about what good infrastructure actually does to a suburb over time.
