## Caption

Take two houses on the same Hawthorne street. Same block size on paper, similar build era, within 200 metres of each other. One has a north-facing aspect with a renovated kitchen and bathroom, a covered outdoor area, and no flood overlay. The other faces south, has a partly updated interior, and sits within a flood zone. The difference in current market value between those two properties could be $200,000 or more.

Automated estimates from the major property portals won't capture that gap. They're working from historical sale data that typically lags 60 to 120 days behind the current market, and they cannot read aspect, renovation quality, flood overlay, land shape, or street position. They produce a number with a range so wide it's often not useful for decision-making.

For a vendor trying to set a realistic price expectation or a buyer trying to work out what a property is actually worth, those estimates are a starting point at best. At worst, they create a reference point that leads to either overpricing a campaign or overpaying in a negotiation.

A current market assessment done properly, with recent comparable sales, an in-person inspection, and knowledge of what buyers are actually paying for in a specific street, produces a number that's meaningfully more accurate.

I've written about how that assessment process works, what it includes, and why the gap between a portal estimate and a real appraisal matters.
