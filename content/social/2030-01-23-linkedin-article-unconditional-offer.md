## Caption

Sellers in the inner east will often accept a lower unconditional offer over a higher conditional one. Understanding why helps both sides.

An unconditional offer in Queensland means the buyer is committing to purchase without any conditions attached. No finance condition. No building and pest condition. No further due diligence clauses. If the buyer cannot complete, they forfeit their deposit, which is typically 10% of the purchase price.

From a seller's perspective, the risk has been transferred. The contract is as close to certain as a property transaction can be. That certainty has value, and most sellers will price it accordingly.

From a buyer's perspective, making an unconditional offer requires confidence in three things: that your finance is approved and reliable, that you have done sufficient due diligence on the property before signing, and that you are comfortable with the deposit exposure if circumstances change.

In the inner east, unconditional offers are most common at auction, where contracts are always unconditional. But they also appear in private treaty negotiations, particularly when there is competition and a buyer wants to differentiate their offer.

A few things worth understanding:

Finance pre-approval is not the same as unconditional finance. Lenders can still find reasons to decline a loan that was pre-approved, particularly if the property valuation comes in below purchase price or if the buyer's financial circumstances change between pre-approval and formal application.

Building and pest inspections ordered before signing are what make an unconditional offer responsible rather than reckless. In the inner east, where older timber homes are common, this is not optional due diligence.

The gap between a conditional offer and an unconditional one is often smaller in price terms than buyers assume.
