## Caption

When buyers assess a body corporate property in Brisbane's inner east, the first number they look at is usually the quarterly levy. That is understandable. It is a direct ongoing cost. But it is the wrong number to prioritise.

The sinking fund balance tells you whether the building has been adequately maintained and whether a special levy is coming. The sinking fund is the pool of money set aside by the body corporate for major capital expenditure: roof replacements, structural repairs, lift maintenance, external painting. When it is well funded, the building can absorb major costs without imposing unexpected charges on owners. When it is underfunded, and many Queensland bodies corporate are, any large maintenance item creates a special levy that falls proportionally across all lot owners.

A body corporate with a low quarterly levy and an underfunded sinking fund is not a bargain. It is a deferred liability. The levy looks attractive because the body corporate has not been collecting enough to maintain the building adequately. At some point that catches up.

The information is accessible. Body corporate disclosure documents are provided as part of the contract process in Queensland. The sinking fund balance, the administrative fund balance, any outstanding special levies, and recent meeting minutes are all in there. What most buyers lack is not access to the information but the framework to interpret it.
