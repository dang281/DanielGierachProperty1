## Caption

Body corporate fees are one of the most commonly underestimated costs in a unit or townhouse purchase. And the levy amount shown in the listing is rarely the full picture.

The body corporate levy covers the building's insurance, common area maintenance, and contributions to two funds: the administrative fund for day-to-day running costs, and the sinking fund for capital works, things like roof replacement, painting, lift servicing. A body corporate with a healthy sinking fund balance has money set aside for major works. One with an underfunded sinking fund is either planning a special levy or deferring maintenance.

Levy amounts vary significantly. A small townhouse complex with no lift and limited common property might run $2,000 to $4,000 per year. A mid-rise complex with a lift, pool, and gym can run $6,000 to $14,000 or more per year, plus any special levies that arise.

The critical document to check before buying into a body corporate is the body corporate records search, sometimes called the BCR or strata report. It shows the sinking fund balance, recent meeting minutes, any outstanding disputes, and any planned works. This is where the story is: not the levy amount quoted in the listing.

An underfunded sinking fund at the time of purchase is a liability you are about to inherit.
