## Caption

The main residence exemption is one of the most valuable tax concessions in the Australian system. It is also one of the most misunderstood.

Most homeowners assume that selling their principal place of residence is CGT-free. For the straightforward case, they are right. But the exemption is not automatic, and a significant number of Brisbane inner east vendors find out at campaign time that their situation is more complicated than they assumed.

The most common situation: the property was rented at some point. Any period of rental creates a proportional CGT liability on the capital gain for those years. If you moved out for two years and rented the property, then moved back and lived in it for six years before selling, you do not have full exemption. You have partial exemption, based on the proportion of the ownership period the property was your principal residence.

The six-year absence rule exists and does help. But it applies only if you did not nominate another property as your principal residence during that period, and it requires the property to have been your principal residence at some point before the absence.

The settlement date, not the signing date, determines which financial year your capital gain falls into. Getting this wrong by a few days can shift significant tax liability.
