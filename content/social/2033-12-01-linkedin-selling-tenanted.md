## Caption

Every open home on a tenanted property requires a separate 24-hour written entry notice to the tenant. Most vendors underestimate how many notices that means across a four-to-five-week campaign. Run open homes twice a week and you are looking at eight to ten individual notices, each needing to be served correctly and within the required timeframe. Get any one of them wrong and the tenant has the right to refuse entry. It is a detail that sounds administrative until it disrupts your campaign schedule.

The bigger issue that catches vendors out is the lease itself. A fixed-term lease that runs past the proposed settlement date means the buyer takes over that lease in full. Vacant possession is not automatic. If a buyer wants to move into the property at settlement and there is a fixed-term lease running three months past that date, the deal either does not proceed or the settlement date needs to change. This is one of the most common misunderstandings in tenanted property sales, and the time to clarify it is before the campaign launches, not when a buyer asks the question during due diligence.

The first practical step is to match the proposed settlement date to the lease expiry before any marketing begins. If the dates do not align, work out the options with the property manager before the first open home, not during the campaign when options narrow.

The second step is to notify the property manager that the property is being sold before the first open home, not after the first offer arrives. Property managers who are informed and prepared make the campaign process significantly smoother. Ones who find out mid-campaign can create unnecessary friction, even without meaning to.
