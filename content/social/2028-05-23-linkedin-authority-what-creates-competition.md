## Caption

People watching an auction tend to think the competition is created on the day. The fast bidding, the auctioneer calling urgency, the crowd gathered on the footpath. That is not where competition is made. It is where it is revealed.

Real competition at auction requires two or more buyers who cannot afford to miss this property at this price. Not buyers who are mildly interested. Not buyers who are happy to see how the bidding goes and step in if it seems reasonable. Buyers who have decided, before they arrive, that they are going home with this property if they can.

That condition is not created on auction day. It is created in the three or four weeks before, through the campaign. It comes from understanding which buyers in the active pool have a problem that this specific property solves. A family who has been searching for twelve months for a house in this school catchment within their budget. An investor who has been looking for a corner block they can develop. A couple who have been outbid twice on properties with a north-facing master bedroom and room for a pool.

For those buyers, this property is not one of several options. It is the answer to a specific search. That is the buyer who bids past where they planned to stop. Two of those buyers in the room means competition. One means a result. Neither means a pass-in.

Finding those buyers, understanding what they need and making sure they have every reason to show up ready to bid, is the work of the campaign. The auctioneer handles the last fifteen minutes. The outcome was largely decided before they arrived. danielgierach.com
