## Caption

Most buyers open a building and pest report, flip to the summary page, and read a verdict. That is the wrong place to start.

The summary is a risk classification. It is not a list of what needs doing. Two properties can receive the same summary rating with completely different underlying conditions, and a buyer who only reads the rating will miss that entirely.

The section that matters is active defects. Not historical defects, not maintenance items noted in passing, but active conditions that are present right now and require a response. Active timber pest activity sits in a different category from general maintenance observations, and the appropriate buyer response to each is completely different. One is a negotiation point or a reason to pause. The other is a to-do list.

What most buyers also miss is that inspectors write to a standard that protects them from liability. That means conservative language and cautious coverage. Learning to read that language correctly, rather than at face value, changes how useful the report becomes.

There is also the question of what to do once you understand what you are looking at. Buyers who have never dealt with a serious defect finding often do not know whether to walk away, renegotiate, or proceed with independent trades quotes. The answer depends on the defect, the property, and the market conditions.

I have put together a full guide to reading a building and pest report as a buyer. It covers where to start, what language to watch for, and how to respond to the findings that matter.
