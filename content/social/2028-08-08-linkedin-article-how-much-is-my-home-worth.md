## Caption

Two houses on the same street in Hawthorne can be $200,000 apart in sale price. Same suburb, same block size, same approximate age. The difference is aspect, renovation quality, the rear boundary situation, and whether one side of the street has a city view.

Automated valuation tools average all of that away. They apply a median to a street and return a number that is right for neither property. The lag makes it worse: most of these tools are working from settlement data that is sixty to one hundred and twenty days old, which in a moving market means they are telling you what the property would have been worth several months ago.

This is not a minor problem. I see sellers enter campaigns with price expectations anchored to an online estimate and then discover, when the first two weeks of open homes produce offers well below that number, that the estimate was doing the anchoring, not the market.

The real answer to what a home is worth comes from comparable sales, reviewed by someone who has actually walked through them, knows the street, and understands which ones are genuinely comparable and which ones only look that way from a data screen.

The article unpacks how comparable sales actually work, what makes two properties comparable or not, and why the methodology matters.
