## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright ends the negotiation entirely. Countering it, even at asking price, keeps the conversation open and often surfaces the buyer's real ceiling.

That distinction matters more than most vendors realise in the moment. A flat rejection signals that the vendor is not engaged and gives the buyer no path back into the conversation without losing face. A counter, even one that does not move from the asking price, signals that the vendor is willing to transact and invites a response. In most cases, a buyer who has made a first offer has a second number in mind that they did not lead with. A counter is what surfaces it.

In the inner east, an opening offer that sits 8 to 10 per cent below asking price is a common starting position from experienced buyers. It is not necessarily a signal of disinterest or an inability to pay more. It is a negotiating position and it should be treated as one. The vendor who understands that the first offer is rarely the best offer, and responds with a counter rather than a rejection, is in a better position than one who closes the conversation before it has had a chance to develop.

The nature of the conditions attached to an offer also changes the calculation. An unconditional offer at a lower price removes the vendor's exposure to finance, building and pest, or other conditions falling over during the conditional period. That risk transfer has real value and the price comparison between a conditional and an unconditional offer is not straightforward. The two figures should not be evaluated as like-for-like.

The article covers how to respond to offers in a way that maximises the outcome rather than simply expressing a preference.
