## Caption

The question I hear most from inner east vendors in early 2033 is a version of: "We heard prices would go up after the Olympics. Why hasn't mine?"

The honest answer is that the relationship between major events and residential property values is more complicated than headlines suggest, and it plays out differently across suburb types and price points. ⚠️ VERIFY Some parts of Brisbane's inner east did see meaningful price movement during the lead-up to 2032. Others tracked more closely to underlying fundamentals: land scarcity, school catchments, proximity to the river, and the quality of the housing stock.

Pricing a property correctly in 2033 means understanding where your suburb actually sits, not where the optimistic narrative places it. That requires looking at comparable sales from the past three to six months, adjusting for condition and land size, and having a direct conversation about what happens if the price is set above where buyers are willing to go.

Overpriced properties in the inner east do not sell quietly. They sit. They accumulate days on market. They attract price reduction speculation from other buyers. And they often sell below what a correctly-priced campaign would have achieved.

The post-Olympic market in Brisbane's inner east is not a bad one. It is a market where preparation and realistic pricing are doing more work than the calendar. That is not a problem. It is a straightforward brief for anyone who takes the time to understand it.
