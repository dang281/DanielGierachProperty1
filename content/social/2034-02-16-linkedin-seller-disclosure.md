## Caption

Most Queensland vendors sign the disclosure section of a contract without giving it serious thought. Their solicitor handles the paperwork, the agent handles the campaign, and disclosure feels like a box-ticking exercise.

It is not. A failure to disclose a known material fact can unwind a contract after settlement. That is not a hypothetical risk. It is a legal outcome that Queensland courts have applied.

The three categories most commonly missed by vendors who do not have a disclosure checklist in front of them: pool compliance certificates, where the timing and currency of the certificate matters, not just whether one exists; body corporate records for units and townhouses, where defect budgets, special levies, and unresolved disputes are material facts; and neighbourhood disputes, where a history of complaint, noise issues, or encroachment creates an obligation to disclose even when the situation feels resolved.

The article at the link below steps through the full disclosure framework for Queensland vendors. It is not legal advice, but it gives you enough context to ask the right questions of your solicitor before the contract goes out, not after it has been signed.
