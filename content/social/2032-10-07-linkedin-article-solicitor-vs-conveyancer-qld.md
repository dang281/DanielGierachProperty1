## Caption

Most buyers in Queensland sign the contract first and call their solicitor second. That sequence is backwards.

The moment to involve a solicitor is before you sign, when the conditions are still negotiable and the inclusions can still be corrected. Once the contract is executed, the framework is fixed. The finance condition deadline is set. The settlement date is locked in. The inclusions and exclusions are what they are on the page. Any errors or oversights from that point forward require agreement from both parties to fix.

In Brisbane's inner east, contracts on character homes often have complications that a buyer does not immediately recognise. Heritage overlays, pool compliance certificates, old form building approvals for extensions, and inclusions lists that do not accurately reflect what is in the home. None of these are necessarily deal-breakers, but all of them are negotiating points before signing that disappear after signing.

The distinction between a solicitor and a licensed conveyancer also matters in Queensland. A solicitor can provide legal advice and act on your behalf in a dispute. A conveyancer handles the transfer process but cannot advise you on the legal implications of what you are signing. For a standard purchase with a clear title and no complications, a conveyancer may be sufficient. For anything more involved, a solicitor is the right choice.

The article on my website covers the full distinction: when each is appropriate, what questions to ask, and what to look for in a Queensland contract before you put your signature on it.
