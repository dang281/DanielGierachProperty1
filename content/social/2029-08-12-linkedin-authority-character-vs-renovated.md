## Caption

There is a consistent pattern in the inner east that sellers who are planning pre-campaign works often get wrong. They spend money neutralising the features that buyers came to compete for.

Original VJ walls, high ceilings, wide timber floors, and federation-era joinery are not defects. They are the product. Buyers in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Balmoral, and Norman Park make a deliberate choice to be in this market because they want these features. When a seller patches VJ walls with gyproc, paints hardwood floors white, or replaces original windows with aluminium double-glaze, they are frequently removing the premium, not adding to it.

The renovations that do translate to price are ones that address genuine functional problems: an outdated kitchen in a property that is otherwise market-ready, a bathroom that is structurally tired rather than just dated, a floor plan that has a fixable awkward transition. These are targeted, purposeful works with a clear buyer-facing justification.

What does not translate is cosmetic renovation done to a seller's personal taste in a market where the buyer pool has very specific preferences. The average renovation in the inner east recovers less than it costs. The properties that attract the most competitive offers in this market are either fully renovated to a high standard by a builder who understood the buyer, or they are original and presented honestly, with buyers factoring in their own vision.

Before you spend anything on a property ahead of a campaign, the most valuable conversation is one about what buyers in your specific street are actually paying for. The answer sometimes saves significant money.
