## Caption

Days on market is one of the cleaner signals in real estate data, but it is often misread. A short figure tells you that buyers found the property compelling at the price it was offered. A long figure almost always tells you the same thing in reverse: something about the price, the presentation, or the campaign put buyers off, and the vendor had to wait for a buyer willing to accept those issues or for a price correction.

In Brisbane's inner east, a well-priced property in good condition in an active campaign will typically find a buyer within the first two to four weeks. That timeline varies by suburb and by the current volume of competing stock, but as a general guide, it holds. When a property is sitting at thirty, forty, or sixty days, the question to ask is not what is wrong with the market but what is wrong with the price.

Extended campaigns in the inner east are almost always a pricing problem. Not always the agent's fault: vendors who come to market with a number that is ahead of the evidence will sometimes need time and market feedback to accept a correction. But the effect on buyers is real. A property that has been on the market for six weeks starts to carry a stigma. Buyers begin to wonder what is wrong with it, even when the answer is simply that it was overpriced from day one.

The antidote is a pricing conversation grounded in current comparable sales evidence, held before the campaign launches rather than four weeks into it. That conversation is less comfortable upfront, but it produces a cleaner campaign and a better result.
