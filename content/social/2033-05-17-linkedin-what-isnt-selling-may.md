## Caption

It is worth being honest about the properties that are not moving in the inner east right now, because the reasons are instructive and most of them are avoidable.

High days on market in May tends to cluster around a few common patterns. The first is overpricing relative to recent comparable sales. When a vendor has anchored their expectations to something that sold six or nine months ago and the market has since recalibrated, the gap between expectation and buyer reality creates a standoff that gets harder to resolve the longer it runs. Price reductions late in a campaign erode buyer confidence rather than restoring it.

The second pattern is presentation. Properties that came to market without professional photography, without styling, or without addressing the obvious cosmetic issues that any buyer walking through will notice are struggling to generate the enquiry needed to create competition. Buyers in the $1.5 to $2 million bracket are not making decisions from duty alone.

The third is compliance. Unapproved structures, patios built without permits, and pools without current safety certificates are slowing settlements and sometimes killing deals at contract stage. These are fixable, but they need to be fixed before the campaign, not after the contract is signed.

None of this is a market problem in the general sense. It is a preparation problem. The buyers are there. The process is what's getting in the way.
