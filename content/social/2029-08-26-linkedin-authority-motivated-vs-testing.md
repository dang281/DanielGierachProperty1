## Caption

Buyers who attend more than a handful of open homes develop an instinct for which vendors genuinely want to sell and which ones are testing the market. It does not take long. The signals are visible in the campaign itself.

A vendor who is genuinely ready to sell has set a price that reflects the market rather than a personal number with no comparable basis. They respond to offers within a reasonable timeframe and do not sit on feedback for a week. When a buyer asks about their timeline, there is a real answer: a purchase they are trying to fund, a settlement they are working toward, a relocation with a confirmed date. The campaign feels like a transaction in progress, not a referendum on value.

A vendor testing the market looks different. The price is set at a margin above where comparable properties have sold, justified by reasoning that does not hold up to scrutiny. Offer responses take longer than they should. Conditional offers that are genuinely strong get rejected on minor terms rather than negotiated. The timeline, when asked, is always flexible in a way that suggests there is no real deadline.

Experienced buyers disengage from the second type quickly. They stop attending, stop submitting offers, and wait for either a price reduction or a change in circumstances that creates urgency. That wait can stretch for months, and during it, the market moves, other properties launch, and the campaign loses the momentum it had in the opening weeks.

If you are going to sell, the most valuable thing you can bring to a campaign is a genuine intention to transact. Buyers read it immediately, and it makes every part of the process work better.
