## Caption

In specific inner east suburbs, being inside a high-demand state school catchment adds a consistent and material premium to property prices. This is not a broad generalisation. The effect is concentrated, it is measurable in comparable sales data, and it is most acute in the months approaching school enrolment deadlines.

The catchments that carry a recognised premium in Brisbane's inner east are not uniform across all schools. They attach to a small number of high-demand state primaries and high schools where the alternative, whether private schooling or out-of-catchment applications, involves cost, travel, or uncertainty. Buyers in these catchments are not paying a premium for sentiment. They are paying for a specific outcome: reliable access to a school they have already chosen.

For sellers, the catchment premium has two practical implications. The first is that the campaign should be explicit about the catchment. Many buyers confirm the boundary before they confirm anything else about a property. Making the catchment prominent in the listing, the marketing, and the open home presentation ensures it reaches the buyers who are specifically seeking it.

The second implication is timing. The catchment premium is highest when the enrolment pressure is most acute, typically from September through to the end of November before the January intake. A seller who is inside a high-demand catchment and launches in spring is selling at the point of maximum buyer urgency. That timing is not accidental for the best results achieved in these streets.

If you are selling in a catchment suburb and the school is not the lead feature of your campaign, it is worth a conversation.
