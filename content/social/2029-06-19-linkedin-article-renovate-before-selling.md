## Caption

The renovation question before selling almost always conflates two different things: fixing what is broken and adding value. They are not the same, and the return on investment is very different.

Fixing defects protects existing value. A roof with visible damage, a bathroom with water penetration, or a kitchen where appliances no longer work sends a signal to buyers that the home has not been maintained. That signal, even if the issues are relatively minor, gives buyers a reason to discount and erodes confidence in what else might be wrong. Addressing genuine defects before launching is almost always worth the cost.

Adding value is different. A full kitchen renovation in the inner east typically costs $40,000 to $60,000 or more. In most cases, it does not earn back its full cost at sale. The exception is when the kitchen is genuinely the room holding the price down: dated, impractical, or out of keeping with the buyer profile for that suburb. In those cases, a well-targeted update can move the needle. In others, the buyers who would have competed for the home at $1.1 million will still compete for it at $1.1 million with or without the new kitchen.

The framework I use with vendors: fix what is broken, clean what is dirty, declutter what is cramped. Leave the structural renovation decisions to the buyer.
