## Caption

Selling a tenanted property in Queensland is genuinely more complex than selling a vacant one, and most landlords I speak with are surprised by how many moving parts there are before a campaign can even begin.

The thing most people get wrong is assuming the tenancy is simply disclosed and buyers factor it in. In reality, the rights of the existing tenant affect almost every aspect of the campaign. Entry notice requirements limit when and how you can conduct open homes. If the tenant is on a fixed-term lease, a buyer may be required to honour that lease, which directly affects who will buy and at what price. A periodic tenancy gives more flexibility but still requires correct notice processes that, if mishandled, can create delays at settlement.

There's also the relationship dynamic. A tenant who feels their privacy is being respected and who understands the process is far more likely to present the property cooperatively. A tenant who feels blindsided will make the campaign harder than it needs to be. That relationship is worth managing carefully from day one.

The investor pool is real and active, but it is a different buyer to an owner-occupier, and you need to understand which buyer type your property will attract before you set your price expectations.

Queensland's rental tenancy laws have continued to evolve and the current framework matters. I've written through the practical steps at the link below.
