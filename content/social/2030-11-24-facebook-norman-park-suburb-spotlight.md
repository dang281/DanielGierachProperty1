## Caption

Norman Park operates differently to most inner east suburbs in one important respect: the Whites Hill State College catchment is its single strongest price driver, and the boundary line has a more visible effect on comparable sale prices than in almost any other suburb in the area.

Buyers who understand this do their research before making an offer. The price for a property inside the catchment on a block of 600 square metres can be materially higher than a nearly identical property outside the boundary on the same street if the lots straddle the zone line. That divergence is real and consistent.

Beyond the school catchment, Norman Park has an appeal that is harder to quantify: the suburb's streets are quiet. Whites Hill Reserve provides bush walking access within the suburb boundary. The fig trees on the established residential streets create a canopy that gives Norman Park a greenery and liveability that buyers from the more exposed suburbs notice immediately.

The Mowbray Park ferry terminal provides river access for residents in the lower section of the suburb, adding a commute alternative that buyers who work in New Farm or the inner city find useful. That access is a lifestyle premium that does not always appear in the property description but comes up in conversations at open homes.

For Norman Park homeowners in the school catchment zone, the late November buyer pool is still active and motivated.
