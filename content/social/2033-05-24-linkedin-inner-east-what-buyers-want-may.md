## Caption

Buyer priorities in Brisbane's inner east shift over time, and knowing where they currently sit helps vendors make better decisions about where to spend money before a campaign.

The clearest pattern right now is the premium being placed on functional liveability over cosmetic finish. Buyers at the $1.8 to $2.5 million level in suburbs like Morningside, Hawthorne, and Coorparoo are not chasing magazine kitchens. They are looking for adequate bedroom count, a layout that works for how families actually live, outdoor space that connects naturally to the interior, and a location that puts them inside the catchment they need.

What this means practically is that a vendor who spends $30,000 on a kitchen renovation before selling is often spending money on a decision the buyer will have preferred to make themselves. The same vendor who spends $8,000 on professional styling and photography, addresses the cosmetic defects a buyer's eye catches immediately, and prices on the evidence of the last three months of comparable sales, is likely to get a better result.

There are exceptions. A genuinely deficient kitchen or bathroom in a property at the higher price point may warrant investment. But the question has to be whether the spend returns more than it costs, not whether it makes the vendor feel better about the presentation.

The buyers telling us what they care about are doing it at every open home. The vendors who listen to that feedback before they list are the ones who don't have to hear it during the campaign.
