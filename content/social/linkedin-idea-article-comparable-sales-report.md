## Caption

A comparable sales report is the closest thing property has to an objective measure of value. Most people read them incorrectly, and the mistake costs money.

The most valuable comparables share four things with the property being assessed: the same suburb or immediately adjacent area, the same general configuration (bedrooms, bathrooms, garage), a similar renovation level, and a sale date within the last 90 days. Properties that sold 18 months ago in a different market are not strong comparables regardless of how similar they look on paper.

Position matters as much as configuration. A home on an elevated, quiet street with a city view is not comparable to a home on a main road at ground level, even if the bedroom count and land size match. In the inner east, where the difference between two streets in the same suburb can be $200,000 or more, this distinction is significant.

The most common mistake buyers and vendors make is over-weighting the highest sale in a suburb as a ceiling for what any property can achieve. A record sale is achieved by the most competitive buyer pool encountering the best property at the best time. It is a reference point, not a standard.

The article below covers what goes into a reliable comparable sales analysis, how to ask the right questions when an agent presents one, and how to identify when comparables are being cherry-picked.
