## Caption

The main residence CGT exemption is not automatic if the property was ever rented. Most vendors find this out during the campaign, not before. Finding out before is much better.

Here is the situation that catches people. You buy a home, rent it out for two years while you live elsewhere, then move in and live there for six years. You sell. You assume you're covered by the main residence exemption. You may be partially covered, but the years the property was income-producing will likely attract a proportional capital gains liability. The calculation is not simple.

In Brisbane's inner east, this matters more than in most markets because of how much land values have moved over the past decade. A property in Hawthorne or Camp Hill that was purchased for investment purposes and later converted to a principal residence may have a cost base that looks very different to the vendor's expectations once the accountant works through it.

There is also the six-year rule to understand, which allows you to treat a former residence as your principal place of abode for up to six years while it is rented, under certain conditions. It applies in some scenarios and not others. Getting this wrong is not a small error.

None of this means don't sell. It means talk to your accountant before you set a price and definitely before you set a settlement date relative to June 30.

I've broken down how the exemption works, when it applies in full, and what partial exemptions mean in practical terms.
