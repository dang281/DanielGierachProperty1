## Caption

Rejecting a low offer outright closes the negotiation. A counter-offer keeps it open. The response to the first offer usually determines whether the deal gets done at all.

This is one of the most consistent patterns in private treaty selling, and it is one that vendors get wrong more often than they should. A low first offer is uncomfortable. It can feel like the buyer is being disrespectful or isn't serious. Sometimes that is true. But often a buyer's first offer is a starting position, not a ceiling. They have room to move. The only way to find out is to counter.

A counter-offer communicates several things at once. It tells the buyer you are a willing seller at the right price. It tells them what the right price looks like from your side. It keeps both parties in conversation. A flat rejection tells the buyer nothing except that they should probably look elsewhere, and many will.

The other pattern worth understanding is that vendors who respond emotionally to low offers often make the mistake of counter-offering too high. A counter that is close to the asking price when the offer was significantly below it restarts the gap and achieves very little. The art of the response is in finding a number that is firm and credible but small enough to keep the buyer engaged.

The article covers how to structure your response to various offer types, the difference between private treaty and auction negotiation dynamics, and when a low offer is actually a sign of strong buyer interest rather than weak buyer intent.
