## Caption

The summary page is the wrong place to start reading a building and pest report. It is also where almost every buyer starts, and it is why so many buyers either panic unnecessarily or miss the things that actually matter.

Here is the distinction that changes everything. Active timber pest activity and general maintenance items require completely different responses from a buyer. A report flagging live termite activity or evidence of active borers is a serious finding that needs specialist advice before you proceed. A report noting peeling exterior paint, a cracked tile, or an aging hot water system is describing normal wear on a home that has been lived in. These two categories of finding are not equivalent, but on a summary page they can appear side by side with the same formatting.

Buyers who read only the summary often do one of two things. They withdraw from a contract over defects that are genuinely cosmetic. Or they focus on the cosmetic items and don't ask the right follow-up questions about the findings buried in the structural or pest sections of the actual report.

In the inner east, older Queenslander and post-war homes in Bulimba, Hawthorne, and Morningside are particularly likely to generate long reports with a mix of character-home quirks and legitimate structural notes. Knowing how to read what is in front of you determines whether you get the deal done sensibly or walk away from a perfectly good home.

I've written a guide to working through the report section by section and knowing which questions to ask your inspector when you've finished.
