## Caption

The single biggest lever a seller controls in a property campaign is not the timing, the styling, or the marketing spend. It is the pricing strategy.

A property priced to attract three competing buyers will almost always sell above what a property priced to attract one buyer achieves. That sounds obvious until you are actually in the room deciding on a campaign number, and the temptation to anchor high is very real.

The mechanism is straightforward. When a buyer sees a property and believes other buyers are also looking seriously, they commit to their best number. When they believe they have time and no competition, they start low and negotiate. The campaign strategy has to create the first situation, not the second.

In the inner east, where stock is limited and buyers have often been researching for months, a well-priced property in the right condition gets traction fast. The first two weekends of any campaign are the most important. Buyer interest peaks early, and it does not come back at the same level if the first two opens pass without an offer.

Pricing to attract competition is not the same as underquoting. It means setting an expectation that reflects market value accurately and presents the property as accessible to the right buyer pool. That distinction matters, and it drives campaigns that perform.

If you are weighing up a campaign this year, the pricing conversation is the one to have first.
