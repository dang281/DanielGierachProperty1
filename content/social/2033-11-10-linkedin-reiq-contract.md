## Caption

Most buyers who miss their finance condition deadline assume they are still covered because the bank has not formally declined. They are not.

The REIQ Contract of Sale is the standard residential property contract used in Queensland, and it contains several provisions that regularly surprise buyers and sellers who have not read it carefully. The surprises are not obscure technicalities. They are the kinds of things that come up in ordinary transactions and cause real problems when people are not expecting them.

The finance condition operates on a strict deadline. If the buyer has not formally waived or extended the condition before that deadline expires, the contract can be at risk, regardless of whether the bank has approved or declined. Extension needs to be agreed in writing before the deadline, not after. A buyer who assumes they are still in time because they have not heard bad news from their lender is taking a risk they may not be aware of.

Inclusions are another area where the contract language does the work, not the original conversation. Chattels disputes at settlement are almost always about what the contract specifically listed as excluded versus what the vendor took. Fixtures stay with the property unless the contract says otherwise. Verbal agreements about what the vendor intended to take are not binding unless they are written into the contract.

The cooling-off exit is not free. In Queensland, a buyer who exercises the cooling-off right during the 5-business-day window forfeits 0.25% of the purchase price. On a $1.4M contract, that is $3,500. It is not a zero-cost exit, and buyers who treat it as one are miscalculating.
