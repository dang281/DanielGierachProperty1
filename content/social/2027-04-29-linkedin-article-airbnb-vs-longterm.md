## Caption

A 3-bedroom house in Bulimba or Hawthorne can generate somewhere between $46,000 and $71,000 per year in short-term rental income at 70% occupancy. The equivalent long-term rental sits around $44,000 to $52,000. On paper, Airbnb wins.

The costs tell a different story.

Short-term rental carries ongoing expenses that long-term tenancies do not: professional cleaning between every stay, restocking consumables, Airbnb management fees (typically 15 to 20 per cent of gross income), higher insurance premiums, and a maintenance schedule that reflects continuous guest turnover rather than a single occupant. When you add those up for a well-run inner-east property, you are looking at $15,000 to $25,000 in additional annual costs that do not apply to a standard tenancy.

That gap closes the income difference in most cases, and in some cases reverses it.

There are properties and situations where short-term rental still stacks up. A premium location close to Riverfire and event precincts, a well-styled property with strong reviews and high seasonal occupancy, or an owner who manages the property personally and eliminates the management fee. The numbers work. But they require active management, not passive income.

For most inner-east investors, the honest case for long-term rental comes down to simplicity, vacancy certainty, and the fact that your net return after costs is often within a few thousand dollars of a well-run Airbnb.

The full breakdown with specific numbers is at danielgierach.com/insights/airbnb-vs-longterm.
