## Caption

The main residence exemption is not automatic if your property was ever rented. Most sellers find this out at tax time, not before settlement.

If you rented your home for any period before selling, even briefly, a portion of your capital gain may be taxable. The exemption applies in full only if the property was used solely as your principal place of residence for the entire period of ownership. The moment it generated rental income, the calculation changes. The ATO uses a time-based apportionment method, and the taxable portion can be significant for properties held over many years.

This matters more in the inner east than in many other markets because the profile of sellers here often includes people who bought a property, lived in it, rented it out during a work relocation or extended travel, then moved back in before selling. Each of those transitions affects the tax outcome.

There is also a separate rule for properties that were never used as a main residence: the six-year absence rule. It allows you to treat a former home as your main residence for up to six years after you moved out, provided it was your principal place of residence at some point. But the rule has conditions, and misapplying it is one of the more common mistakes sellers make when calculating their tax position.

The right time to understand your CGT position is before you sign the agency agreement, not after you have accepted an offer. A conversation with your accountant at the pre-campaign stage gives you options. That same conversation post-settlement gives you a tax bill.
