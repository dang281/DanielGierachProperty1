## Caption

Four years from the Brisbane 2032 Olympics opening ceremony. At this point, the infrastructure effect has been priced into some parts of the market and is still working its way through others.

The suburbs closest to committed venues and transport upgrades saw their premium built in early, when the announcement came and buyers moved before the actual delivery. What is less obvious is how the ripple extends. Improved transit connectivity across the inner east has shortened the effective commute from suburbs that previously felt further from the CBD than they were. That changes buyer psychology in ways that show up gradually in prices, not in a single spike.

The event itself will bring short-term disruption and short-term demand, but the properties that benefit most from the 2032 legacy are those positioned for the infrastructure that remains after the Games close. Better train stations, upgraded arterial roads, new pedestrian connections. These are permanent.

The investment case for the inner east in 2028 is not really about the Games. It is about a city that has been building toward a certain density and connectivity for fifteen years, and is now closer to that outcome than it has ever been.

That is a more durable investment thesis than a sporting event, however large.
