## Caption

Most buyers call their solicitor after they have already signed the contract. That is the wrong sequence. The contract review is where the legal advice actually matters.

Once you have signed, your options narrow significantly. You are bound by whatever terms are in that document, including finance deadlines that may be shorter than your lender needs, special conditions that could expose you to costs you did not anticipate, and inclusions or exclusions that were never written in because you assumed they were obvious. Assumptions do not hold up in a property contract.

What gets missed when buyers sign first and ask questions second includes finance deadline risk, which is more common than people realise, missing inclusions that the agent mentioned verbally but were never recorded in writing, and special conditions that favour the seller in ways a buyer without legal advice would not identify.

The question of conveyancer versus solicitor is often framed as a cost decision. That is rarely the right frame. A licensed conveyancer handles standard residential transactions competently. A solicitor becomes relevant when the transaction has complexity: a deceased estate, a development site, unusual special conditions, trust structures, or commercial elements. The cost difference between the two is typically modest relative to the transaction value, but the capability difference matters in edge cases.

The article walks through both sides clearly, including what questions to ask before choosing.
