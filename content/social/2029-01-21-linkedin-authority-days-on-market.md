## Caption

Days on market is one of the most useful numbers in a negotiation, and one of the most misread.

When a property in Morningside or Cannon Hill sells in under 20 days, it almost always means one of two things: the price was right, or the campaign created genuine competition. Usually both. That is a vendor who got what they wanted and a buyer who had to act quickly to secure it.

When a property sits past 45 days without selling, the story gets more complicated. Sometimes the property has a genuine issue that buyers are pricing in. More often, the listing price was set above what the market was willing to pay, and the vendor is now waiting for a buyer willing to close the gap. Buyers who have been watching the inner east for a while know this. They also know that by day 60 most vendors are ready to have a different kind of conversation.

The useful insight for sellers is this: a well-priced campaign that generates competition in the first two weeks produces a better result than a high opening price that sits and accumulates days. Not because buyers are generous, but because competition does the work that price reduction cannot.

For buyers, days on market is not a negotiating weapon in itself. It is a signal about whether a property is correctly positioned, overpriced, or has an issue worth understanding before you make an offer.
