## Caption

The sale method is not the seller's preference decision. It is the market's.

This is the part that gets lost in the conversation between agents and vendors. An agent who runs every campaign the same way regardless of property type or buyer pool is not serving their client, they are serving their own workflow. The sale method should follow the shape of the likely buyer pool, not the agent's comfort zone or the vendor's assumption that one method always produces better results.

A property with a broad, competitive buyer pool is an auction property. Multiple genuine buyers, similar motivation levels, limited comparable options in the area: that is the setup where open competition works in the vendor's favour. The auction process forces buyers to show their hand simultaneously, and that is where prices move above expectation.

A property with a narrow, specific buyer changes the equation. If the likely buyer is someone who will only proceed with full due diligence, or a buyer who would be put off by open competition, running an auction can actively suppress demand. The buyer you need might not register. The buyer who does register might not be the buyer who values the property most. You can end up with a campaign that generates activity without generating the right activity.

Getting the method wrong does not just produce a lower result. In the worst cases, it means running a full campaign, absorbing the marketing spend, and then relaunching under a different method with a property that now carries days-on-market history.
