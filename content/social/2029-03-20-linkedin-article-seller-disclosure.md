## Caption

Queensland vendors have a legal obligation to provide a completed Seller Disclosure Statement before any contract is entered into.

This is not optional, and it is not something that can be sorted out after the buyer signs. The Property Law Act 2023 (Qld) requires the statement to be given to the buyer before the contract is signed. If the disclosure statement is not provided, or if it contains material inaccuracies, the buyer has the right to terminate the contract at any time before settlement.

That right persists all the way to settlement day, which means an improperly prepared disclosure statement is not a problem you can fix by moving quickly to exchange. It is an ongoing risk until settlement is complete.

The practical implication for sellers preparing to go to market is that the disclosure statement should be ready before the campaign launches, not assembled in a rush after an offer is received. Completing it properly requires title search documents, body corporate information if applicable, and accurate information about known encumbrances, easements, and council notices.

A disclosure statement prepared carelessly, or not at all, is one of the easiest ways for a transaction to unravel at a late stage.
