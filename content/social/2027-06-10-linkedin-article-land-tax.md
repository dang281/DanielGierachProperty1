## Caption

Queensland land tax is one of the holding costs that catches investors off guard most often, and it is not because the rules are particularly complicated. It is because most buyers do not think about it until after they have already settled on a second property.

The key thing to understand is that Queensland land tax is calculated on unimproved land value, not on the property's market value. That distinction matters more than it used to. Inner east Brisbane land values have risen considerably over the past several years, and that movement has pushed a meaningful number of investors over the Queensland land tax threshold without them realising it. They bought a property that seemed like a straightforward investment, and then the assessment arrived.

The threshold and rate structure in Queensland means that the jump from one investment property to two can create a liability that was not in anyone's original cashflow calculations. Once you are over the threshold, you are assessed on the combined value of all taxable land you hold in Queensland, not just the new acquisition. That is the part that surprises people.

The conversation worth having is before you buy the second property, not after. Knowing your current land value position and how a new acquisition would affect your total assessable value is a simple piece of due diligence that a lot of buyers skip.
