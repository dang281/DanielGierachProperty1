## Caption

Brisbane's inner east has a significant number of properties on lots with heritage overlays. Hawthorne, Norman Park, Bulimba and East Brisbane all have pockets of character housing that fall under Brisbane City Council's heritage framework. And a substantial number of buyers walk away from these properties unnecessarily because they misunderstand what an overlay actually restricts.

Here is what it does: it requires Brisbane City Council approval for changes to street-facing elements of the property. Facade alterations, front fencing, verandah modifications. That approval process is not automatic but it is also not prohibitive for work that respects the character of the building.

Here is what it does not do: it does not restrict internal renovations, rear and side extensions that do not affect the street-facing character, or any work that does not touch the regulated elements. A full internal renovation of a heritage-overlaid Queenslander in Hawthorne, including kitchen, bathrooms, and structural changes to rear rooms, generally does not require council heritage approval at all.

The practical implication is that buyers who rule out heritage overlay properties on the assumption that renovation is off the table are eliminating a category of stock that may have exactly the bones they are looking for, in the locations they want, with less competition precisely because of the misconception.

Understanding what the overlay actually says is a ten-minute exercise with a town planner or your solicitor.
