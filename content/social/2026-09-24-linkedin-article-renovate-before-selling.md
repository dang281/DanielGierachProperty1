## Caption

The most useful reframe for thinking about pre-sale work is separating two categories that look identical on a to-do list but behave completely differently in a negotiation: fixing defects and adding value.

Fixing defects means removing the things that give buyers ammunition to reduce your price. A cracked tile, a sticking door, a water-stained ceiling, peeling paint on the exterior. None of these individually collapse a deal, but experienced buyers and their building inspectors notice them, and they become line items in a low offer. Addressing defects before you list protects the value you already have. It's not creating new value, it's preventing existing value from being discounted away.

Adding value is a different calculation. A full kitchen renovation before sale sounds like a logical investment, but for most inner-east properties in the mid-to-upper price brackets, it frequently isn't. Buyers at those price points have their own opinions about kitchens. They know what they want, they have the budget to renovate to their taste, and a vendor's pre-sale kitchen renovation may not align with what they'd choose. You've spent $40,000 to $80,000 on a kitchen the buyer was going to replace anyway. The return is rarely there.

The exception is structural or cosmetic work that is universally appealing and creates a clearly better first impression: fresh paint throughout, refinished floors, a well-presented bathroom rather than a full renovation. These tend to earn their cost back.

The article at danielgierach.com/insights/should-i-renovate-before-selling-brisbane covers which pre-sale investments consistently return value and which ones don't, with specific examples from inner-east Brisbane.
