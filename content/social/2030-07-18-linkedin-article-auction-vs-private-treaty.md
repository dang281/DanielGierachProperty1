## Caption

The method of sale should follow the property and the buyer pool. It should not follow the vendor's personal preference, the neighbour's experience, or what was popular last spring.

Auction works when you have genuine buyer competition. A three-bedroom house in Bulimba with good street presence, a renovated kitchen, and a yard will attract multiple buyer types: families, upsizers, investors. Put those buyers in a room and you get competitive bidding that sets the market price without the vendor having to guess what that price is. Auction gives that property its best result because the buyers themselves set the number.

EOI suits a different kind of property. A large home on an oversized block, an architecturally significant property, or a site with development potential will attract buyers who value it very differently from each other. One buyer's ceiling is another buyer's floor. EOI collects those offers without forcing buyers to bid against each other in real time, and it allows the vendor to assess price, conditions, and buyer quality together.

Private treaty suits a well-priced property where the vendor has a clear sense of what they want, the buyer pool is not broadly competitive, and settlement conditions matter as much as price. It is also the right method when market conditions mean that the certainty of a negotiated price outweighs the upside of a competitive auction.

The wrong way to make this decision is to ask which method the vendor is most comfortable with. The right way is to ask which method gives the specific property the best access to the right buyers.
