## Caption

Whites Hill State College is the most talked-about school catchment in Brisbane's inner east, and Norman Park sits squarely within it. The demand that catchment generates is not abstract: it shows up in days on market, in the number of contracts written within the first week of a campaign, and in the premium that properties within the boundary consistently achieve over otherwise identical homes just outside it.

That boundary premium is one of the most clearly observable patterns I see in the inner east. Two streets apart can mean a meaningful difference in the buyer pool you attract. Buyers searching specifically for the Whites Hill catchment know the boundary precisely and will not compromise on it, which creates a consistently deep pool of motivated buyers for properties within it.

The character housing stock in Norman Park is another consistent draw. The Queenslanders and post-war homes that dominate the suburb's streetscapes are exactly what a significant portion of the inner east buyer market is searching for. Buyers who want character and authenticity tend to arrive at Norman Park after looking at Balmoral, Camp Hill, and Hawthorne, and they often stay.

Whites Hill Reserve provides exceptional parkland on the suburb's eastern side. Access to that reserve is a quality-of-life factor buyers regularly mention at open homes, adding depth to the liveability that complements the character housing and school catchment.

If you own in Norman Park and want to understand what that catchment premium means for your property's value today, I am happy to provide an appraisal.
