## Caption

The common assumption about spring is that more buyers means faster sales. That is true in the right conditions. In the inner east, the actual days-on-market pattern is more complicated and worth understanding clearly before assuming spring automatically produces speed.

Well-priced, well-prepared properties in winter often transact as quickly as their spring equivalents. Winter buyers in the inner east are typically high-conviction buyers who have been searching for months. When they find the right property at the right price, they act quickly because they have already done the decision-making work. A winter campaign that is priced correctly and well prepared can move to contract in a timeline that matches or beats what a comparable property achieves in spring.

Spring can produce faster results, and does when the conditions are right. A well-prepared, accurately priced property entering a market in late September with an energised buyer pool and low competing supply is a very strong combination.

The spring scenario that produces extended days on market is the one most vendors do not anticipate: the overpriced or underprepared property launching into a competitive market. Spring gives a buyer more options than winter. A buyer who walks through a spring property and finds it too expensive or not ready enough does not come back in two weeks when nothing better has appeared. They go to the open home next Saturday and find something closer to what they want. In winter, that alternative might not exist yet. In spring, it usually does.

The practical implication is that the conditions that make winter competitive for a vendor, accuracy of pricing and quality of preparation, are exactly the conditions that make spring competitive too. Season provides context. Preparation and pricing are what actually drive how long a property sits.
