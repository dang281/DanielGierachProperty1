## Caption

There is a meaningful difference between fixing defects and adding value. Most pre-sale renovation advice conflates the two, and sellers end up spending money in the wrong places.

Fixing defects is about protecting what is already there. A leaking roof, a subfloor with moisture issues, a deck with structural concerns, a safety hazard in the electrical system. These are not optional. Buyers or their inspectors will find them, and they will either negotiate hard or walk away. Addressing them before the campaign removes the leverage they would otherwise create.

Cosmetic renovations are a different calculation entirely. A new kitchen in a character home in Hawthorne or Bulimba rarely earns back its full cost. The inner east buyer pool for these properties is experienced. Many of them plan to renovate anyway, and they are not paying full price for someone else's interpretation of what the home should look like. A fresh coat of paint, clean landscaping, and a house that presents well is almost always a better investment than a full kitchen or bathroom update.

The rule of thumb is straightforward. Spend what is needed to remove objections. Do not spend to add features that the buyer may not value the same way you do.

The article covers what actually moves the needle in the inner east market, with a clear framework for deciding what to do, what to leave, and what to skip entirely.
