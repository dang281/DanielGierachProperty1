## Caption

Auction clearance rates are quoted constantly during spring. What they actually measure is less often explained.

A clearance rate is the percentage of properties that sold at auction, out of those that went to auction. A 70 per cent clearance rate does not mean 30 per cent of all properties failed to sell. It means 30 per cent of the properties that went to auction on that weekend did not sell on the day. Many of those sell in the days following, either to the highest bidder or through private negotiation.

The more useful number is the suburb-specific clearance rate over a rolling four-week period, not the weekend headline number. A single weekend is too small a sample. Four weeks gives you a more reliable read on whether the buyer pool for your suburb is competitive or soft.

A clearance rate above 70 per cent in your suburb over four weeks means buyers are competing. Properties are meeting their reserves. Vendors who set realistic reserves are transacting. A rate below 55 per cent means the opposite: buyers are not reaching reserves, properties are passing in, and the post-auction negotiation is becoming the norm.

The inner east clearance rates I track tend to move within a consistent range, but they do vary by property type and price point. A three-bedroom family home in Camp Hill and a four-bedroom character home in Hawthorne can be operating in quite different buyer pools even in the same month.
