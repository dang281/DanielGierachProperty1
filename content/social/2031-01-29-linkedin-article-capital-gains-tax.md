## Caption

The main residence CGT exemption is not automatic if you ever rented the property, and the number of vendors who discover this after settlement is significant.

The assumption most people carry is straightforward: if the property is your home, you pay no capital gains tax when you sell. That is broadly correct for a property that has only ever been your primary residence. The situation changes when there is a rental history, even a partial one. A vendor who bought a property, rented it for two years while living elsewhere, then moved in and lived there for the five years before selling may believe they are fully exempt. Many are not. The exemption calculation is proportional to the period of actual owner-occupation versus the total ownership period, and the ATO's position on this has caught vendors off guard for years.

What makes this particularly consequential is the timing. Most vendors find out at tax time, not before settlement. By the point they are sitting with their accountant filing the year's return, the sale has completed, the proceeds have been used, and the options for structuring things differently are gone. A vendor who had known before listing might have made different decisions about timing, or sought advice about whether a short extension of the owner-occupation period changed the calculation meaningfully.

The article covers the main residence exemption rules, how partial rental history is assessed, the 6-year absence rule and what it actually covers, and what questions to ask your accountant before you commit to a sale timeline.
