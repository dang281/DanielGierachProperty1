## Caption

Here is something sellers often get wrong about property styling: the physical staging is primarily a set for the camera.

A styled room that photographs poorly has not used its budget well. The photos are the first thing a buyer sees. Better photos drive enquiry volume. More enquiry means more people competing for the same property. That competition is what moves the price. The styling itself is rarely what a buyer is thinking about when they submit an offer.

There is also a common mix-up between styling and photography. Some vendors spend well on furniture and soft furnishings, then accept whatever the agent's standard photography package includes. That sequence is back to front. The photography brief and the styling brief should be developed together, because the whole point of the physical presentation is to produce images that stop a buyer scrolling.

A few things worth understanding before you brief a stylist:

First, ask your agent which rooms photograph the most heavily in their campaigns. Not every room needs the same attention.

Second, confirm the photographer is experienced with the specific property type. Queenslanders, townhouses, and apartments each have different challenges with light and angles.

Third, decide on the styling budget only after you know what the photography will look like. The two are connected.

The full article covers what to ask a stylist before you commit, and how to assess whether the result is working.
