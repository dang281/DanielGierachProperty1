## Caption

Most buyers sign the contract first and call their solicitor second. That sequence is backwards, and it costs people.

In Queensland, once you sign a residential contract, you are in a cooling-off period, but that period is short, comes with a financial penalty if you exit, and puts the pressure of review on a clock you've already started. The better approach is to have a solicitor nominated and briefed before you make an offer, not after.

The confusion is often about who to use. In Queensland, conveyancers and solicitors both handle property transfers, but they are not the same. A solicitor can advise you on the contract terms and flag risks that go beyond the mechanics of the transfer. A conveyancer handles the process but is not a lawyer and cannot give legal advice. For a standard purchase in a suburb like Camp Hill or Coorparoo, the distinction might not matter. For a purchase with a complex easement, a heritage overlay, or unusual special conditions, it matters a great deal.

The other thing worth understanding is what each option costs relative to what is at stake. The price difference between a conveyancer and a solicitor for a typical inner east transaction is often a few hundred dollars. The property itself is worth several million. The arithmetic on that should be simple.

I've written a full breakdown of the differences, when each is appropriate, and what to ask before you appoint anyone.
