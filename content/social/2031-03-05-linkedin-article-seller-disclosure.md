## Caption

Under the Property Law Act 2023 (Qld), a Seller Disclosure Statement must be provided before a contract is entered into. Not when the offer arrives. Before.

Most vendors who leave disclosure statement preparation until they receive an offer are already behind. In practice, a well-run campaign prepares the disclosure statement before the property goes to market, so that when a buyer is ready to proceed, the statement is ready to be provided and the contract process can move without delay.

The consequence of getting this wrong is not a minor administrative issue. A buyer can terminate the contract at any time before settlement if the Seller Disclosure Statement was not provided correctly or at all. That is not a right limited to the cooling-off period. A buyer who discovers a failure in disclosure after the due diligence window has closed, after finance has been approved, and in the weeks before settlement, can still use a disclosure failure as grounds to exit. For a vendor who has already committed to a purchase, that outcome is a serious problem.

The disclosure statement requires information about the property's title, any encumbrances, body corporate matters for lots in a community titles scheme, and a range of other matters that take time to compile accurately. Waiting until a buyer is ready does not leave enough time to do it properly.

The article covers what must be disclosed, how to prepare the statement correctly, what happens if it contains an error, and what buyers can do if it is not provided.
