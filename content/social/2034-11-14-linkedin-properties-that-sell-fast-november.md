## Caption

Days on market is one of the most misread statistics in residential property. It measures how long a property was listed, but it does not explain why. Two properties in the same suburb can have very different days on market for reasons that have nothing to do with the quality of either property.

The factors that shorten days on market: pricing that generates enquiry immediately, a property type with more than one buyer who has been watching the suburb, a presentation that photographs well and translates clearly in person, and a vendor who understands that the first week of a campaign is typically the strongest.

The factors that extend days on market: an opening price that is above the range buyers in that suburb have already mapped from comparable sales, presentation issues that buyers can see in photos, and a campaign structure that requires the vendor to approve each offer individually rather than creating a deadline.

In the inner east specifically, the properties that sell within two weeks in November are almost always the ones where two buyers have been watching the suburb and see this as the property they want. The auction or private treaty process becomes a mechanism for them to compete rather than a negotiation from a standing start.

The properties that sit for four to six weeks are usually missing one of those elements. Correcting it mid-campaign is possible but costly in terms of momentum.

Understanding why your property will sell quickly or slowly is a conversation worth having before you go to market.
