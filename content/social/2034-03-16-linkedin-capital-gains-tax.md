## Caption

Most homeowners selling their principal residence assume the main residence CGT exemption applies in full. In many cases, it does. In a significant number of cases, it does not, and the assumption costs people money they did not need to spend.

Three situations that catch Brisbane homeowners unexpectedly. First: the property was used to produce income at any point during ownership, either as a rental before you moved in, after you moved out, or as a home office from which you claimed deductions. Partial use periods require apportionment, and the exempt portion can be smaller than expected. Second: settlement date, not contract signing date, determines which financial year the gain falls into. Getting this wrong can create a tax liability in a year with a higher marginal rate. Third: the six-year rule that allows you to treat a property as your main residence while renting it out has conditions that are easy to breach if you have purchased another property during that period.

None of this means CGT is a barrier to selling. In most straightforward ownership situations, the exemption applies fully and the tax outcome is simple. But the situations where it does not are more common than most homeowners expect, and they are almost always identifiable before the campaign launches rather than after settlement.

The article at the link below steps through the main residence exemption and the most common exceptions that Brisbane homeowners should discuss with their accountant before committing to a sale timeline.
