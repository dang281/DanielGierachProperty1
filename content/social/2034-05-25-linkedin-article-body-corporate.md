## Caption

When buyers are assessing a body corporate property, the levy amount is the number that gets the most attention. It is also the less important number.

Body corporate levies typically cover administration and ongoing maintenance. They run from around $2,000 to $4,000 per year for a small townhouse complex to $6,000 to $14,000 or more per year for a mid-rise with a lift, pool, and common facilities. These figures are disclosed at the listing stage and they are relatively predictable.

The sinking fund balance is the number that actually determines the financial health of the body corporate. The sinking fund is the accumulated reserve for capital works: roof replacement, lift maintenance, external painting, structural repairs. A complex with a $500,000 sinking fund against $2 million in known future capital works is in a materially different position from one with a $500,000 sinking fund and no significant capital works due in the next decade.

Buyers who focus only on the levy and fail to obtain and read the body corporate records are missing the variable that can produce a significant special levy call after settlement.

The records to request before making an offer on any body corporate property are the last two AGM minutes, the most recent sinking fund plan, and the insurance certificate. These are obtainable from the body corporate manager and they take about 10 minutes to review for the key risk factors.
