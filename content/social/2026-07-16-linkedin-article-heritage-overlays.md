## Caption

A heritage overlay on your Brisbane property is not a red flag for buyers. But it does affect what you can alter, demolish, and build, and that affects how you present the property when selling.

Heritage overlays appear on a significant number of residential properties in Brisbane's inner east. Older character homes in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Balmoral, Norman Park, and Morningside sit within areas identified by Brisbane City Council as having historical significance. The overlay doesn't mean the building is fully protected from change. It means changes that affect the external form, character, or streetscape typically require a development application and assessment.

The practical impact for sellers: buyers who are planning to significantly alter or extend a heritage-listed property need to know what's involved before they commit. An informed buyer who understands the overlay and has done their homework is a better buyer than one who finds out at due diligence and uses it to renegotiate.

Disclosure is straightforward. A heritage overlay appears in the planning information your conveyancer obtains as part of contract preparation. The overlay details the level of heritage significance and what restrictions apply.

What I tell sellers: a property with a heritage overlay should be presented to its buyers as a home with a story, not a problem with a complication. Most buyers in the inner east understand what character homes represent. The overlay is often part of the appeal, not a deterrent.
