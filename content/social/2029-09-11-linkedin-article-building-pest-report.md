## Caption

The summary page is the wrong place to start reading a building and pest report. Most buyers start there, see a colour-coded rating, and make a decision based on a single word. That is not how these reports are designed to be read.

The distinction that matters is between active defects and maintenance items. An active defect is something that is affecting the structure or function of the building right now. A maintenance item is something that needs attention over time but is not currently causing damage. They both appear in the report, and they are not equivalent. Treating them as the same leads to overcorrecting on minor findings and, more dangerously, undercorrecting on significant ones.

Structural findings require a specific type of response. Some warrant getting a structural engineer's assessment before proceeding. Others are well within normal range for a home of that age and construction type. An inspector who explains the findings verbally alongside the written report is worth more than one who simply delivers the document and moves on.

What is genuinely negotiable and what is not also requires judgment. A $3,000 maintenance item is not the same negotiating position as a subfloor moisture issue that will cost $15,000 to properly address. Knowing the difference before you make a call to your agent or the selling agent changes the conversation entirely.

The article breaks down how to read these reports clearly, including what to escalate and what to accept as standard.
