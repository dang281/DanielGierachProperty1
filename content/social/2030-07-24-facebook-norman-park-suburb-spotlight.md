## Caption

Norman Park's access to Whites Hill Reserve is a genuine lifestyle asset. The reserve covers a significant area of bushland right on the suburb's eastern edge, and for families with children or buyers who want parkland access without driving to find it, that proximity is a real factor in the purchase decision. It is one of the features that gives Norman Park a character distinct from neighbouring inner east suburbs.

The housing stock leans toward elevated Queenslanders and post-war homes on sloping blocks, the kind of properties that reward thoughtful renovation and tend to hold their character over time. Norman Park State School and the Brisbane State High School catchment give the suburb exceptional school access across both primary and secondary levels, a combination that consistently drives family buyer competition.

Stock volumes in Norman Park have historically been lower than in neighbouring suburbs. Owners tend to hold for longer, and that low turnover means the buyer pool is often deep relative to available listings. When a quality home appears in a well-regarded street, the competition tends to be genuine. Long-term capital growth here has reflected that supply constraint over many years.

If you own in Norman Park and have not tested the market recently, the current landscape for your property type may look quite different from what you last saw.
