## Caption

Under the Property Law Act 2023 (Qld), Queensland sellers must provide a completed Seller Disclosure Statement to the buyer before any contract is entered into. This is not optional, and it is not something that can be resolved after an offer is accepted.

The practical consequence of getting this wrong: a buyer who did not receive the disclosure statement before signing has the right to terminate the contract at any time before settlement. For a seller who moves quickly to accept an offer before the disclosure document is prepared, this creates a significant exposure.

The disclosure statement requires the seller to confirm whether there are adverse entries on title, pending orders or notices affecting the property, relevant body corporate information for lots in a community title scheme, and other matters specified under the Act.

For sellers in the inner east who are preparing for an autumn campaign, this document needs to be part of your pre-listing checklist alongside the contract of sale. Your conveyancer or solicitor prepares it. It typically takes one to two weeks to prepare once they have the relevant information from you.

The sellers who run into problems are the ones who treat this as something to organise after they have an offer. The disclosure statement needs to be ready before the campaign launches, not after it closes.
