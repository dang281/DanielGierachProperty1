## Caption

Two houses on the same street in Hawthorne can sell for $200,000 apart. Same street, similar land size, similar bedrooms. The difference is aspect, presentation, internal configuration, renovation quality, and the specific buyer pool each property attracted on the specific day it went to auction. None of those variables appear in an automated estimate.

Automated valuation tools are built on sold price data. That data is typically 60 to 120 days old by the time it feeds into the model. In a market that has moved, that lag produces estimates that can be meaningfully below what a well-run campaign would actually achieve. In a market that has softened, the same lag can produce estimates that are optimistic. Either way, the estimate reflects the past, not the present.

The other thing automated tools cannot see is what your specific property does well. An agent who has walked through comparable properties, observed buyer reactions, and negotiated outcomes in similar campaigns can tell you something an algorithm cannot: which features of your property will drive competitive interest, and which will limit it.

Getting a realistic picture of what your home is worth requires a conversation, not a search result. The article at the link below explains what actually goes into a credible price assessment and why the same information, read differently, can produce very different outcomes.
