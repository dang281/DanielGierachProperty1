## Caption

Most vendors choose their sale method based on what the neighbour used. That is not a strategy.

I wrote about this because the method decision is genuinely consequential and it is made badly more often than it should be. The right sale method follows the property and the expected buyer, not vendor preference, agent habit, or what sold well three streets away.

Auction works best when the buyer pool is broad and those buyers genuinely disagree on value. The transparency of competitive bidding can push the result above what private treaty negotiations would produce, because two or three buyers who each have a different ceiling will find the ceiling. But that condition has to actually exist. An auction with one serious bidder is not the same thing.

Private treaty is better suited to a narrower buyer pool, or where buyers are likely to need conditions. Finance clauses, building and pest, longer settlement windows. Some buyers will not bid at auction because the unconditional requirement excludes them. If those buyers represent a significant portion of your likely purchaser pool, you are excluding competitive tension by choosing auction.

EOI suits unique or complex properties where the value range across potential buyers is genuinely wide. A structured submission process manages the spread and prevents buyers from anchoring low in early negotiations.

The method should serve the sale, not the other way around.
