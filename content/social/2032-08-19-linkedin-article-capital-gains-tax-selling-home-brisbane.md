## Caption

Most vendors assume the main residence CGT exemption covers them fully. For a large number of Brisbane sellers, that assumption is incorrect.

The exemption is not automatic if the property was ever used to produce rental income. Partial CGT liability applies to the period the property was generating rent. This is not a grey area. It is the standard ATO treatment, and it catches a significant number of vendors who bought their home, rented it out for a period, then moved back in and eventually sold.

The six-year absence rule is the provision most people have heard of. It allows a former principal residence to remain CGT-exempt while you are living elsewhere and renting it out, but only under specific conditions. One of those conditions is that you have not nominated another property as your main residence during that period. If you were renting elsewhere while your home was tenanted, you need to get advice on how the two properties interact under the rules.

The practical consequence is that vendors find out about partial CGT exposure at tax time, not before the campaign. By that point the property has settled, the contracts are done, and the liability is fixed. Understanding your CGT position before you sell, not after, gives you time to plan around it.

A buyer who has lived in their home without any rental period will generally have a straightforward exemption claim. But if there is any rental history, the conversation with your accountant should happen before you call your agent.
