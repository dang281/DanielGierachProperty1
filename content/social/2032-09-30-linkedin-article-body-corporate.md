## Caption

When evaluating a strata or community title property in Brisbane, the sinking fund balance is a more important number than the annual levy.

Most buyers focus on the levy amount when they are assessing a body corporate property. The levy tells you what you will pay each year in ongoing contributions. That number matters. But the sinking fund is the number that tells you whether the body corporate is in a position to handle what is coming.

The sinking fund is the accumulated reserve held for major repairs, capital works, and unexpected expenses across the building or complex. Roof replacements, lift refurbishments, facade maintenance, fire safety upgrades, pool equipment, and common area infrastructure are the kinds of items that come out of the sinking fund.

A body corporate with a low annual levy and a depleted sinking fund is a property where a significant special levy is a realistic possibility. When the capital works can no longer be deferred and the sinking fund lacks sufficient reserves, the owners share that cost proportionally. Special levies can run into tens of thousands of dollars per lot for major works in mid-rise buildings.

Most buyers focus on the levy amount in the listing notes. The sinking fund report is the right place to start before making a decision. It is typically included in the body corporate records and available to serious buyers through the selling agent before contracts are exchanged.

Annual levies vary significantly depending on the type of property. Small townhouse complexes with minimal shared infrastructure sit at one end. Mid-rise buildings with lifts, pools, and extensive common areas sit at the other. The levy rate alone does not tell you much without the sinking fund context.
