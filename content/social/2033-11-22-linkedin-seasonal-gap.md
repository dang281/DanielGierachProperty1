## Caption

There is a persistent belief among vendors that the market shuts down at Christmas. Open home numbers do fall after mid-December. But the character of the buyers still attending changes significantly, and not in a bad direction.

The buyers who are still inspecting properties in late December and early January are not casual lookers. They have been searching through the spring campaign season, attended open homes most weekends since September, and in many cases have submitted at least one offer that did not convert. They are experienced and they are motivated. The casual buyer is at the beach. The serious buyer is still reading listings.

For a vendor, this dynamic creates something worth considering. December and January offer lower competition from other sellers. Supply falls more sharply than demand does. The buyers still in the market are not comparing your property to many other active listings. They are comparing it to how long they have been searching and how much they want to stop doing it.

I have seen December listings achieve results that would have held up in any month of the year, because the buyers who showed up were not going to be outwaited. A couple who has spent four months trying to buy in a specific suburb and school catchment, and who knows the January restart means another six to eight weeks before serious supply returns, is not a passive negotiating partner.

None of this means every property should list in December. It means the window is less dead than its reputation suggests, and vendors who dismiss it without analysis are sometimes leaving a workable option on the table.
