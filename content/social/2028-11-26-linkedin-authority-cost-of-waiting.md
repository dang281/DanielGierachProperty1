## Caption

The decision to wait until February rather than list now has a cost that is rarely calculated, but is easy to understand when you lay it out.

If a property could sell for $1.4 million this December or January, the holding cost for the additional two months while the seller waits for February includes mortgage interest, rates, insurance, and the ongoing cost of maintaining the property. For a typical inner east home at current rates, that two-month holding cost is several thousand dollars.

Add to that the opportunity cost on the proceeds. If the February result is the same as the January result, the seller has paid to wait for nothing. If the February result is marginally higher because of slightly stronger buyer demand, the difference needs to exceed the holding cost to justify the delay.

What the February market adds that November and December do not: more buyer volume. What it removes: the buyers who carried momentum through spring and are still actively looking now.

The February buyer pool is fresh, but it has not been warmed up by six months of open homes. The November and December buyer pool has. They know the market. They have clear evidence of what things sell for. And they have strong motivation to be under contract before the summer break ends.

I am not arguing that November and December are always better than February. I am arguing that the assumption that February is automatically better deserves a calculation rather than a reflex.
