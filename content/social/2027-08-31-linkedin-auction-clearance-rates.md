The auction clearance rate is the most quoted number in property market commentary. It is also one of the most misread.

A clearance rate of 70% means that 30% of auctions did not sell under the hammer on the day. What it does not tell you is what happened in the 48 to 72 hours following. A significant portion of those passed-in properties sell in post-auction negotiation, often at or very close to the vendor's reserve. The headline number does not capture that.

Brisbane's inner east has historically run clearance rates above the broader city average. That is not coincidence. The buyer pool in this corridor is deeper, more competitive, and better prepared than in most other parts of Brisbane. More registered bidders per auction means fewer properties are passed in without a bid, and fewer properties sit.

A high clearance rate in a suburb does not automatically mean every property sells easily. What it tends to mean is that agents are calling auction campaigns correctly and reserves are being set to reflect where the market actually is, not where vendors wish it were.

The metrics that matter more than the headline clearance rate: days on market after an auction pass-in, and whether properties are being passed in at reserve or well below it. Those two data points tell you far more about real buyer appetite than any weekly clearance figure.

Sellers who understand this distinction come into their auction campaign with clearer expectations. They are not chasing a number in the news. They are focused on the right preparation, the right reserve, and what happens on the day and immediately after.
