## Caption

Most homeowners assume the main residence exemption covers them entirely when they sell. It doesn't if the property has rental history.

The exemption is not automatic in that situation. If you rented your home at any point during your ownership, whether for a short period between moves, through a relocation, or as an investment before you moved in, a portion of any capital gain can become taxable. The ATO calculates that proportion based on time, not just intent. A year of rental history on a property held for fifteen years doesn't mean only one fifteenth of the gain is exempt. The calculation is more specific than that, and the numbers matter significantly for properties in the inner east where values have moved considerably over the past decade.

There are also timing decisions that interact with the exemption in ways that catch vendors off guard. The six-year absence rule applies in certain circumstances, but it has conditions attached. Joint ownership between partners adds another layer to work through before settlement.

None of this means you have a tax problem. It means you need to know your position before you sign a contract, not after.
