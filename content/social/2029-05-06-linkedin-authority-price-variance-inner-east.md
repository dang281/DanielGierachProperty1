## Caption

People often ask why two houses on the same street in the inner east can have such different results at sale. The gap is real and it is not random.

The factors that drive it are not always visible on a listing. Aspect is one. A north or northeast-facing rear garden in a Brisbane summer is a practical advantage that is hard to explain on paper but immediately felt at an inspection. Buyers respond to it. Flood overlay is another. Two properties on the same street can sit in different flood categories, which affects insurability, lender appetite, and the breadth of the buyer pool.

Renovation quality matters more than renovation presence. A $40,000 kitchen that suits the buyer profile lifts the result. A $40,000 kitchen that clashes with the home's character or the suburb's buyer expectations does not. The number on the invoice is less relevant than whether the work added appeal to the people who were going to buy there anyway.

School catchments draw a hard line through some inner east suburbs. One side of a street can feed into a high-demand state school catchment. The other might not. That single factor can be worth $100,000 to a family buyer pool.

This is why comparable sales data from online portals is a starting point, not a conclusion. The 200-metre rule does not account for any of this. Street-level knowledge does.
