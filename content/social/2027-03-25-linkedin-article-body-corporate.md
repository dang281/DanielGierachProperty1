## Caption

Apartments, townhouses, and units in Brisbane have a body corporate. Understanding what that means before you buy is more important than most buyers realise.

The body corporate is the legal entity that manages common property, maintains shared infrastructure, and collects levies from owners. For a buyer, the two numbers that matter most are the administration fund levy and the sinking fund levy.

The administration fund covers day-to-day operational costs: insurance, garden maintenance, cleaning of common areas, and management fees. The sinking fund covers long-term capital works: roof replacement, lift maintenance, painting, external repairs. Both are ongoing costs that belong in any yield calculation for an investment property.

The range is wide. A small townhouse complex of six to eight units with no lift or pool might have total levies of $2,000 to $4,000 per year. A mid-rise apartment building with a pool, gym, and lift might run $6,000 to $14,000 per year or more.

What buyers miss most often is the sinking fund balance. A body corporate with a healthy sinking fund has money set aside for capital works. One with a depleted sinking fund may hit owners with a special levy, an unexpected one-off charge, when something needs replacing.

Before signing a contract for a lot in a community title scheme, request the body corporate disclosure statement and check the levy schedule and the sinking fund balance.
