## Caption

A drainage easement along the back fence rarely affects resale price. An access easement running through the yard is a different matter entirely.

Easements are one of the most misunderstood items on a title search. They are not all created equal, and the response "there's an easement on the title" without understanding what type and where can cause unnecessary alarm or, in the other direction, false comfort.

Here is what matters in practice:

A drainage or sewerage easement is the most common type you'll find on inner east properties. It typically runs along the rear or side boundary and restricts what you can build over that section of the land. In most cases, this is a minor practical limitation and has little effect on resale value.

An access easement is more significant. It allows another party to cross your land, which can affect privacy, use of the yard, and in some cases development potential. Understanding who holds the benefit, what the access is for, and whether there are maintenance obligations is important before you buy.

A positive covenant imposes an obligation on the owner, such as maintaining a retaining wall or keeping a drainage infrastructure clear. This is an ongoing cost and responsibility.

In the inner east, where older character properties on irregular blocks are common, easements appear frequently. The key question is always: what does this easement actually restrict, and does that restriction affect how I intend to use or eventually sell the property?

A title search ordered before you make an offer costs little and answers this question definitively.
