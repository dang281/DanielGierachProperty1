## Caption

Most sellers instinctively focus on price when an offer arrives. That's understandable. Price is the number that's easy to read and compare. But in most cases it's only one of several variables that determine which offer actually represents the better outcome.

The variable that gets underweighted most consistently is the conditional risk attached to an offer. A finance condition gives the buyer an exit. If the finance is not approved, or if the buyer decides to invoke the clause for any reason within the allowed period, the contract ends. You're back to the beginning: the property goes back on market, buyer momentum has been lost, days on market has ticked up, and the psychology that builds around a property that "fell through" is real. Two to three weeks of campaign time is gone.

An unconditional offer removes that risk entirely. The buyer has either already arranged their finance or is prepared to proceed without the protection of that clause. When you receive an unconditional offer alongside a conditional one, the comparison is not simply the price difference. It is the price difference plus the value of certainty. On a high-value property, three weeks of lost campaign time and a restart has a real cost.

There are situations where a conditional offer at a higher price is clearly the right choice. But they're less common than vendors think, and the instinct to simply take the highest number on the page without reading the rest of the contract carefully has cost sellers before.

The article at danielgierach.com/insights/how-to-handle-offers-when-selling covers this in detail, including how to compare offers that look different on the surface and what to negotiate when conditions are involved.
