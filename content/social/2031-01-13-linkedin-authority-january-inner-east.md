## Caption

January has a reputation as a slow month in real estate. The listing volumes are lower, the open home boards are thinner, and a lot of agents are still on leave. That picture is accurate for supply. It is not accurate for demand.

The buyers attending open homes in January are not casual inspectors. They are not deciding whether to buy this year. They have pre-approval in place, they have been watching the market for months, and they are ready to move when the right property appears. The fact that fewer listings are available does not reduce their motivation. In many cases it concentrates it.

What open home attendance tells you in January is different from what it tells you in October. Smaller numbers through the door can still represent a highly qualified pool. I pay more attention to the questions buyers ask and the amount of time they spend measuring rooms and walking the site than I do to the headcount. A buyer who comes back for a second inspection in the same week, in January, is a buyer who is ready to act.

For vendors, the January dynamic is worth understanding before they dismiss the idea of a January listing. Competition for quality properties in the inner east is lower than it will be in March. The buyers who are active now are committed. That combination does not always produce the highest price, but it produces a reliable one, and sometimes a fast result with fewer open homes.

The February and March autumn window gets more attention, and for good reason. But January has its own logic, and understanding it helps vendors make better timing decisions.
