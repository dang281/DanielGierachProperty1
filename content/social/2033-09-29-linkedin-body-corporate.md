## Caption

The body corporate levy is the number most buyers look at first when assessing a strata property. It is not the most important number. The sinking fund balance is.

The levy is the regular maintenance contribution. It covers the day-to-day costs of running the common property. The sinking fund is the accumulated reserve held for major capital expenditure: roof replacement, lift maintenance, repainting the exterior, resurfacing the car park. A body corporate with a low levy and a depleted sinking fund is in a worse position than one with a higher levy and an adequate reserve. The low levy looks attractive until the special levy notice arrives.

Special levies are issued when the sinking fund does not have the capital to cover a major expense and the body corporate committee needs to fund it from owners. For a buyer who has just moved in, that is an unbudgeted cost on top of the purchase price. In inner east Brisbane, where apartment and townhouse properties have been trading at significant prices, special levies for building works can run into tens of thousands of dollars per lot.

The due diligence question for any strata purchase is not just what the levy is but whether the sinking fund is on track against the capital plan and what major expenditure is anticipated in the next five years. That information is available in the body corporate records. The article covers how to access them, what to look for, and how to assess the financial health of a body corporate before you commit.
