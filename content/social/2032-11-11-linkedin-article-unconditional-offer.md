## Caption

A seller will often accept a lower unconditional offer over a higher conditional one. This surprises buyers who have not been through a competitive purchase before, but the logic is straightforward once you understand what a conditional contract actually means for the vendor.

A finance condition and a building and pest condition both carry a window, typically two to three weeks in Queensland, where the buyer can exit the contract. That window is the seller's risk period. Their property is effectively off the market, they cannot accept other offers, and if the buyer exits they lose weeks and have to restart the campaign. In a market where buyer interest peaks and fades, that lost time has a real cost.

The unconditional buyer removes that risk entirely. The contract is exchanged and the sale is done. From the seller's perspective, a lower certain price is often more valuable than a higher uncertain one.

Understanding this dynamic is how buyers in competitive inner east markets win properties they would otherwise lose to higher offers that carry conditions. It does not mean buying unconditionally is always the right move, there is genuine risk in waiving your due diligence rights, and the article on my site works through when it makes sense and when it does not. But knowing that the option exists and how sellers think about it is a meaningful advantage in a multiple-offer situation.

If you are preparing to buy in the inner east this spring, this is worth reading before you write your first offer.
