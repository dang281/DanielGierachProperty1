## Caption

A seller accepting a lower price for an unconditional offer is not irrationality. In most cases, it is sound judgment.

A conditional contract with a finance clause and a building and pest clause carries a two to three week window where the deal can legally unwind. Finance falls through. The building and pest report comes back with findings the buyer uses as a renegotiation tool. The deal collapses and the property goes back to market. That sequence happens more often than buyers or sellers expect, and when it does, the vendor has lost weeks, momentum, and sometimes the next property they were counting on.

An unconditional offer eliminates that risk window entirely. The contract is binding from the moment it is signed. There is no finance clause to fail. There is no building and pest period. The seller can plan their next move with certainty.

Whether that certainty is worth a price difference depends on the vendor's circumstances. A seller who needs a clean, predictable outcome, perhaps because they are buying simultaneously or have already committed to something else, may genuinely be better served by a lower unconditional offer than by a higher conditional one that carries the risk of falling over.

Understanding the mechanics of unconditional contracts in Queensland, including what rights remain and what risks transfer to the buyer, is essential context before making or accepting this kind of offer.
