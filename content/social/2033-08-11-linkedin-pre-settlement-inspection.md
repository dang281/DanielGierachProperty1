## Caption

The pre-settlement inspection is one of the parts of a property transaction that vendors sometimes treat as a formality. It is not. The buyer's right to inspect the property before settlement is intended to confirm that the property is in the same condition as it was at the time of sale and that all agreed inclusions are still present. Disputes at this stage are more common than most vendors expect.

The most frequent issues are removalist damage, items listed in the contract as inclusions that have been removed, and maintenance problems that have developed between the date of sale and settlement. Any of these can trigger a request for a price adjustment or a delay to settlement. Resolving them at the eleventh hour is considerably more stressful than preventing them.

The practical approach is a vendor walkthrough two to three days before the scheduled pre-settlement inspection. Check that every inclusion listed in the contract is physically present. Check for any damage that has occurred since the sale. Address any maintenance issue that has emerged. Arrive at the pre-settlement inspection confident that the property matches the contract.

Settlement disputes that arise from the pre-settlement inspection are almost always preventable with preparation. The article covers what buyers and sellers should expect, what the common disputes involve, and how vendors can get to settlement day without surprises.
