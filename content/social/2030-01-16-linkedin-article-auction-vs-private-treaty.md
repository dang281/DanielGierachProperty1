## Caption

The choice of sale method should follow the property and the buyer pool, not seller preference or what sold last week in the street.

Auction works well when a property has a broad or unpredictable buyer pool, when competition is likely but buyer motivation is hard to measure, or when a seller needs a clean, unconditional result on a defined timeline. In the inner east, well-presented family homes in suburbs like Norman Park, Camp Hill, and Hawthorne often fit this profile well.

Private treaty works well when the buyer pool is narrower, when conditions are more likely, or when the seller has specific timing requirements that don't suit a four-week auction campaign. It also suits properties where the value proposition is clear enough that price is unlikely to be dramatically moved by competition.

The mistake most sellers make is treating the method as a default. It is a strategy decision.

A few things worth understanding:

Auction creates urgency. Buyers must bid unconditionally on the day. That is powerful when you have genuine competition but it requires genuine preparation, both in marketing and in buyer management.

Private treaty gives buyers more time and more ability to use conditions as negotiating tools. A well-managed private treaty campaign can still create competition, but it requires a different kind of skill from the agent.

Expression of interest sits between the two. It gives structure and deadline without the theatre of auction day. It suits properties where privacy or complexity is a factor.

None of these methods is inherently better. The right one depends on your property.
