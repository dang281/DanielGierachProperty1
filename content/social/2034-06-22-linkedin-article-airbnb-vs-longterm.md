## Caption

The gross income from short-term rental looks attractive. The number that matters is the net return after the costs that do not appear in the headline comparison.

Platform fees from Airbnb and similar services typically run 15 to 20 percent of gross revenue. Cleaning after each stay is a direct cost that accumulates quickly on a frequently booked property. Short-term rental insurance is more expensive than landlord insurance on a long-term tenancy. The wear and maintenance cycle on a short-term rental is compressed: linen, furniture, kitchen equipment, and finishes all degrade faster under continuous turnover. And in Queensland, council approval is required for short-term rental operations in some local government areas.

Adding these costs to the calculation reduces the net return gap between short-term and long-term rental significantly. On most inner east properties, after accounting for platform fees, cleaning, insurance, and maintenance, the net return from Airbnb ends up within a few thousand dollars per year of a well-managed long-term tenancy, with considerably more management time and attention required.

There are properties where the short-term rental model makes sense: high-demand locations with strong occupancy, properties with specific amenity that justifies a higher nightly rate, and owners who are willing to manage the operational complexity. For most standard inner east investment properties, the long-term rental model produces a comparable net return with significantly less effort.
