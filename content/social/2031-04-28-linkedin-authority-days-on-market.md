## Caption

In Brisbane's inner east, a property that has been on the market for more than 45 days is communicating something to every buyer who sees it. The question is whether the vendor understands what that message is.

Buyers read days on market as a signal. A long campaign implies one of three things: the price is ahead of the evidence, the presentation has not connected with the buyer demographic, or the campaign itself has not reached the right buyers effectively. Buyers do not know which of those three is the cause, but they assume one of them is, and they adjust their behaviour accordingly. The adjustment is predictable. They develop leverage expectations. They bid lower, offer lower, or wait, because they believe the vendor will eventually need to accept less than the asking price.

The problem with that dynamic is that it compounds over time. Each additional week on market reinforces the buyer's perception that the property is overpriced or flawed. By the time a vendor is genuinely ready to accept a market price, they are negotiating from a weakened position against buyers who have been watching the listing for six weeks and have formed a view about how low they can go.

For a vendor who finds themselves in a long campaign, the relevant questions are direct. Has the price been adjusted to reflect what the market is actually prepared to pay? Has the presentation been assessed honestly against comparable properties that are selling? Has the campaign been reaching buyers who are active in that price range, or has it been generating inquiry from buyers who cannot act?

The earlier these questions are asked, the more options remain available. A campaign in week five has more room to reset than one in week ten.
