## Caption

Staging is primarily a photography investment. Better photos drive enquiry, enquiry drives competition, and competition is what moves price.

This is the part of the staging conversation that most sellers miss. The instinct is to think about how the property looks on the day of an open home. The more important question is how it looks in the listing photos that are seen by hundreds or thousands of buyers before any of them walk through the door.

A buyer decides whether to inspect a property based on what they see online. In the inner east, where the competition for buyer attention is genuine, a well-styled and professionally photographed property attracts more inspections than an equivalent property presented without that investment. More inspections mean a deeper pool of potential buyers. A deeper pool means a better chance of competition.

A few things worth understanding about styling in practice:

Staging is not about making the property look different from what it is. It is about presenting what the property is at its best. Buyers who feel misled by staging leave the open home without interest.

The investment typically ranges from a few thousand dollars for a light package to more for a full installation. The return, measured against what an additional buyer at the negotiating table adds to the final price, almost always exceeds the cost.

Not every property needs full staging. A well-presented, tidy home may need only minor adjustments and a professional photographer. The decision should be made property by property.

Brief the stylist on the target buyer. A young professional-focused brief produces different choices than a family-oriented one.
