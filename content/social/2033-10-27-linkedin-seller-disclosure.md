## Caption

Under Queensland's Property Law Act 2023, the Seller Disclosure Statement must be provided before any contract is entered into, not when an offer arrives.

This is the most common sequence error I see, and the consequences of getting it wrong are significant. Vendors begin marketing, buyers inspect, offers come in, and the disclosure statement gets prepared alongside the contract. That sequence creates a genuine legal exposure that most vendors are not aware of until they are in the middle of it.

If a buyer terminates because disclosure was not provided correctly, they can do so at any time before settlement. That is not a narrow window. It is the entire period from contract to settlement, which can be six weeks or longer. A buyer who has second thoughts, or who finds something they do not like in the course of due diligence, has a mechanism to exit the contract that a vendor inadvertently handed them by not preparing the disclosure statement before the first open home.

Preparing the statement before campaign launch removes this risk entirely. It is not complicated to do. The disclosure statement covers specific matters about the property, there is a defined list, and it requires a solicitor review before it is given to a buyer. The lead time needed is modest. The cost of not doing it can be very large.

This is one of the things I go through with every vendor before we launch a campaign, because the alternative is discovering the risk after it has already been created.
