## Caption

The assumption that winter is a bad time to sell in Brisbane is repeated so often that many vendors accept it without testing whether it is actually true for their property and their suburb.

Brisbane's inner east is not Sydney. Winter here is mild, open homes in June are not cold, and the argument that buyers hibernate through July does not hold up against what agents who run winter campaigns in this market actually observe. The buyer pool is smaller, but that is not the same thing as the buyer pool being absent.

The buyers active in June and July in the inner east are disproportionately serious. They are not attending open homes for lifestyle entertainment. A buyer inspecting a Hawthorne property on a Saturday morning in July has made a genuine decision to be there. Competition at auction, when a property is well-prepared and well-priced, can still occur. Three or four genuine, unconditional-capable buyers arriving at the same property on the same auction day is enough to produce a competitive result, and that scenario is achievable in winter.

A property that launches in June or July enters a market with less advertising noise than it will face in September. The typical spring surge brings a higher volume of listings, more competition for buyer attention, and more choice for buyers weighing up several properties. A well-presented property that launches into a quieter June market does not have to compete with the same volume of comparable listings.

Winter is not the right answer for every vendor. A property that is not yet presentation-ready should not rush to beat the cold. But for a vendor who is prepared, the case for a winter launch is more rational than the seasonal advice usually suggests.
