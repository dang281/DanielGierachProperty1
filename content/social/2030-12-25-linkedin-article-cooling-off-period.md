## Caption

Queensland property buyers have a statutory five-business-day cooling off period on residential contracts. Most people know this exists. Fewer people understand its mechanics correctly.

The first misunderstanding is who controls it. The cooling off period is a buyer right. The vendor cannot remove it, waive it on the buyer's behalf, or include a clause that eliminates it. A vendor or agent who tells a buyer to "waive their cooling off" as a condition of the offer being accepted is asking the buyer to surrender a statutory right, which in Queensland cannot be done unilaterally.

The buyer can choose to waive the cooling off period themselves, as a negotiating concession to make their offer more attractive. That decision belongs entirely to the buyer.

The second misunderstanding is about auctions. Contracts signed at auction carry no cooling off period. If you buy a property under the hammer, you are contractually committed from the moment the auctioneer's hammer falls. That is why preparation before an auction day, including completed building and pest inspection, confirmed finance pre-approval, and a clear walk-away number, is essential.

The third is the cost to exit during cooling off. In Queensland, a buyer who withdraws within the cooling off period forfeits 0.25 percent of the purchase price. On an $800,000 property, that is $2,000. That figure is owed even if no formal exchange or settlement has occurred.

Knowing this before you sign avoids surprises that arrive at exactly the wrong moment.
