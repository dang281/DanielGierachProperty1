## Caption

A fixed-term lease that runs past your intended settlement date means the buyer takes over the lease. Most vendors don't realise this until the contract is already signed.

Selling an investment property in Queensland while it is tenanted is common, but the rules around tenant rights and lease obligations create constraints that need to be understood before you list, not discovered in the middle of a negotiation.

Here is what matters in practice:

A fixed-term lease cannot be terminated by the vendor simply because they want to sell. If a tenant has twelve months remaining on a fixed-term lease and the contract settles in two months, the buyer takes over as landlord with the lease in place and the tenant's rights fully intact.

For owner-occupier buyers, a tenanted property is typically less attractive than a vacant one. The buyer may be willing to purchase, but the price offered will often reflect the inconvenience of waiting for the lease to end or negotiating an early vacancy with the tenant.

A periodic lease (month-to-month) gives the seller more flexibility. The Queensland tenancy notice period for periodic leases allows the landlord to issue a notice to leave if the property is being sold to an owner-occupier. Timing this correctly requires planning.

Access for inspections during a tenancy is governed by Queensland tenancy legislation. Tenants must be given the correct notice period before inspections, and the frequency of inspections is restricted. This affects how a sales campaign is structured.

The most expensive mistake vendors make: listing the property and accepting an offer without knowing the lease expiry date and the buyer's intention to occupy or invest.
