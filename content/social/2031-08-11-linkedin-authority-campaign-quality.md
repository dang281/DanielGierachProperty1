## Caption

Every vendor going to market in spring wants a strong result. The ones who get it are usually not the ones who spent the most on marketing or hired the photographer with the best portfolio. The distinguishing factor is almost always the quality of the preparation before the campaign goes live.

That preparation has three components, and the most important is rarely the one vendors focus on first.

The first is pricing accuracy. A property priced accurately relative to current comparable sales will attract the right buyers from the start. A property priced optimistically attracts the wrong buyers or no buyers, produces feedback that stings, and faces the perception problem that comes from adjusting the price guide mid-campaign. A price reduction is public. Buyers notice it and draw conclusions, often unflattering ones.

The second is method of sale. Auction, private treaty, and expression of interest each suit different buyer profiles and different property types. A method that does not match the buyer demographic for the property works against the campaign regardless of how well everything else is executed.

The third is condition. A buyer who walks through a property in spring and finds deferred maintenance, unfinished work, or presentation issues has a reason to negotiate on price. Vendors who have systematically addressed those things before the campaign launches remove that negotiating tool from the buyer's hands.

Campaigns that launch right, with accurate pricing, the right method, and a well-prepared property, are materially different from campaigns that launch and then spend four weeks catching up. The gap between the two is measurable in price and in time.

Spring preparation is happening now. The vendors who use August to get these things right will have a different September than the vendors who wait until the last moment.
