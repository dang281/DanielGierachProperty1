## Caption

Most buyers open a building and pest report and go straight to the summary page. That is the wrong place to start.

The summary page treats every finding as roughly equivalent. An active termite infestation in a load-bearing structural element sits in the same colour-coded category as a recommendation to reseal the gutters. They are not the same finding. They require completely different responses from a buyer, and making decisions based on the summary alone means you are missing the information that actually matters.

The distinction that matters most is between active timber pest activity and general maintenance observations. Active pest activity in a structural element is a safety and liability issue that needs a pest management plan, professional advice, and potentially a renegotiation of the purchase price or conditions. A maintenance item like deteriorating weatherproofing is a cost, not a crisis. Treating them the same because the summary page lumped them together is how buyers end up either panicking unnecessarily or missing something serious.

Reading the report properly means going to the body, identifying what each finding actually is, understanding which section of the property it relates to, and then assessing what it requires. The inspector's recommendations in the body of the report are usually more specific and more useful than the summary.

The article covers how to read each section of a standard building and pest report, what the different categories of findings mean, and what questions to ask before you sign unconditionally.
