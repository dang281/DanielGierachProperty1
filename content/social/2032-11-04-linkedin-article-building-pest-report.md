## Caption

The summary page of a building and pest report is the wrong place to start.

Most buyers flip to it first, see a list of items flagged as requiring attention, and either panic or dismiss the whole thing as boilerplate. Neither reaction is useful, because active timber pest activity and routine maintenance items are completely different categories, and treating them as equivalent in severity is exactly how buyers make poor decisions in the conditional period.

Active termite activity, significant structural movement, or rising damp are findings that warrant serious follow-up, potentially a specialist report, and in some cases a renegotiation or exit. A broken gutter bracket, a sticking window, or minor surface cracking on an older home are not the same conversation. The report will flag both, often in the same section, in the same language.

There is a specific order for reading these reports that most buyers do not follow. It is not complicated once you know it, and it changes how the whole document reads. I have written through the approach in detail on my website, including what to look for in timber pest sections specifically, which is the area where buyers most often either overreact or underestimate.

If you are in the conditional period on a property right now, or preparing to make an offer where you plan to include a building and pest condition, this is worth 10 minutes before you decide anything.
