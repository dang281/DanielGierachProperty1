## Caption

The right response to the first offer in a campaign sets the tone for everything that follows. Vendors who reject without countering close the conversation. Vendors who counter, even at a figure well above the offer received, keep the negotiation alive and signal that a deal is possible. That signal is more valuable than most vendors recognise in the moment, because a buyer who receives a counter has been told the vendor is willing to engage. A buyer who receives a rejection has been told the opposite.

This matters most in the pre-spring period, when vendors who have been running a campaign through winter are most likely to have received offers below their asking price. The temptation to reject a low offer outright is understandable, but it removes the one outcome that a counter preserves: the possibility of closing the gap.

There is also a separate question about multiple offers and how to manage them. When more than one offer is received, the vendor's position changes significantly. The right process for handling competing offers is different from the right process for handling a single offer in a quiet campaign, and conflating the two approaches produces worse outcomes in both situations.

The timing and manner of a vendor's response is also a negotiation signal. A response that comes too quickly can signal desperation. A response that comes too slowly can signal disinterest and cause a buyer to move on. The window for a meaningful response in most inner east campaigns is narrower than vendors expect.

The full article explains how to read and respond to offers at each stage of a campaign.
