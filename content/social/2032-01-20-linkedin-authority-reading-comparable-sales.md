## Caption

Comparable sales data has a lag built into it that most people underestimate. A property that sold in October or November 2031 is recorded and publicly available, but the conditions that produced that result, the buyer pool, the competitive tension, the financing environment, may or may not reflect where the inner east market sits in January 2032.

The lag between a contract being signed and that sale appearing in aggregated market data is typically 60 to 120 days. That means the comparable sales available to agents and buyers right now, in mid-January 2032, largely reflect contracts signed in October and November. They predate the new year, the renewed buyer interest that comes with January, and the specific dynamics of an Olympic year opening.

This matters practically in two ways. First, vendors who price based purely on the most recent comparable sales may be working from a lagged reference point. Second, buyers who use October and November sales data to anchor their expectations may be underestimating what comparable properties are actually trading for now.

The honest position for an agent is to acknowledge the lag and work through it. What was the market doing in Q4 2031? What has changed since then? What can we observe from the open home activity and inspection numbers in the first two weeks of January? Anecdotal evidence from active agents is often more current than published data, and it is worth weighting accordingly when the lag is significant.

In early 2032, with Olympic year buyer dynamics playing out in real time, this calibration matters more than usual.
