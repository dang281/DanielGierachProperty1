## Caption

The standard REIQ contract used in Queensland property transactions is twelve pages long. Most buyers sign it and call their solicitor second.

That sequence is backwards, but it is understandable. In a competitive market, by the time a buyer has a solicitor review the contract, another buyer has already signed. The pressure to move quickly is real.

The clauses that matter most are the ones buyers are most likely to miss.

The finance condition and its deadline. If you have a finance clause in your contract, you must formally satisfy or extend it by the stated date. Miss the deadline and the clause lapses. A lapsed finance clause means you are effectively unconditional, even if your bank has not confirmed approval. This catches buyers more often than almost anything else.

The chattels and inclusions list. What you see at the open home is not necessarily what stays with the property. Light fittings are generally fixtures. Freestanding appliances, outdoor furniture, and anything the seller has indicated they will take needs to be listed in the contract. If it is not in writing, do not assume it is included.

The settlement date and adjustments. Settlement adjusts council rates, water, and body corporate levies to the settlement date. Understanding how those calculations work avoids surprises.
