## Caption

Most homeowners assume the main residence exemption covers them fully when they sell. For many, it does. But if you have rented the property at any point, the exemption becomes partial, and the calculation is not simple.

The ATO applies the exemption on a proportional basis. If you owned the property for 10 years and rented it out for three of those years, you may have a capital gain on roughly 30% of the total gain. On a well-appreciated inner east property, that number can be significant.

The six-year absence rule offers some relief. If you move out but do not nominate another property as your main residence during that period, you can treat the original property as your main residence for up to six years even while it is rented. This rule has conditions, and getting it wrong or missing the window entirely can cost considerably more than the cost of good advice.

The other factor worth knowing: the timing of settlement relative to 30 June determines which financial year the capital gain falls into. If you are close to the end of the financial year and your settlement date is negotiable, this is worth discussing with your accountant before contracts are exchanged.
