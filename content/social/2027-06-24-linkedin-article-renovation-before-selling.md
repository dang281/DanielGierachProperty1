## Caption

There is a question I get from almost every vendor before a campaign starts: how much should we spend before we list? The answer depends entirely on what you are trying to achieve, and there is a useful distinction that most people overlook.

Fixing defects and adding value are not the same thing. Fixing a leaking tap, repainting scuffed walls, replacing a broken screen door, resealing the deck, and addressing anything a building inspector would flag: these actions protect the value you already have. They prevent buyers from using visible problems as justification to negotiate down. A buyer who walks through and finds a dripping tap and a cracked window pane does not just price those items at cost. They price them at anxiety. They wonder what else has been deferred. Fixing the obvious things removes that doubt.

A full kitchen renovation is a different conversation entirely. A modern kitchen renovation in a Brisbane inner east home currently costs somewhere between $40,000 and $60,000 to do properly. That spend almost never returns its full cost at sale. The reason is straightforward: buyers apply their own taste. A kitchen that you designed and paid $50,000 for may not be what the buyer would have chosen. They might have spent the same money differently, and they will price your renovation accordingly, which is usually at a discount to what it cost you.

The vendors who come out ahead are the ones who fix what is broken, style what they have, and leave the cosmetic upgrades for the buyer to make their own. The link below works through this in more detail, including where spending is typically justified and where it is not.
