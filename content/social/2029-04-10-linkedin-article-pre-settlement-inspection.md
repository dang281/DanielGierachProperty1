## Caption

The buyer is entitled to one pre-settlement inspection. Most vendors know this. What fewer vendors know is that the inspection is not a formality.

The buyer is checking that the property is in the same condition as it was at the time the contract was signed. Not the same condition as when it was presented for sale. The same condition as on contract date.

That distinction matters because a lot can change between signing and settlement. Removalists cause damage to walls, floors, and doorframes. Appliances that were included in the contract get accidentally taken. Fixtures get removed during a renovation that the vendor started after signing. All of these create disputes at the worst possible time, often in the final 24 to 48 hours before settlement.

The standard vendor advice I give: walk through the property yourself the week before settlement with the contract in front of you. Check that every inclusion listed in the contract is present and in working order. Take photos. If anything has changed since contract signing, get ahead of it before the buyer discovers it at the pre-settlement inspection.

Settlement delays caused by pre-settlement disputes are almost always avoidable. The fix is preparation, not paperwork.
