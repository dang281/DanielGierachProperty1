## Caption

The finance condition deadline lapses on the date specified in the contract, even if the bank has not given formal approval yet. This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of the REIQ Contract of Sale, and it catches buyers in situations they did not expect.

When the deadline passes without a written extension agreed by both parties, the finance condition technically lapses. The buyer may no longer have a valid exit if finance is declined after that point. In practice, extensions are commonly granted, but they require the seller's agreement, and sellers are not obligated to provide one. If the seller's circumstances have changed, or another buyer has emerged, that extension may not come.

The implications of this are material. Buyers who choose a finance deadline based on what sounds like enough time, rather than what their specific lender actually needs, create a gap in their protection. Lenders with longer assessment timelines, complex income structures, or properties that require additional valuation work regularly need more time than the default deadline allows.

Inclusions and exclusions carry a separate set of risks. A fixture or fitting that was present during inspections is not automatically included in the contract. If it is not written in, it may not be there on settlement day, and there is limited recourse after signing.

Solicitor review before signing addresses both of these issues. The article covers the REIQ contract clause by clause, with a focus on the sections where buyers most commonly expose themselves to avoidable risk.
