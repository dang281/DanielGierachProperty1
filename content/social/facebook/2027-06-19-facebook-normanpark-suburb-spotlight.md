## Caption

Norman Park is one of the inner east's more consistently competitive markets, and it has been for some time. The combination of direct train access, strong school catchment appeal, and a housing stock dominated by well-maintained character homes gives the suburb a profile that draws buyers from a wide range of backgrounds. Supply is never abundant here, which keeps conditions in favour of owners when they do come to market.

Norman Park station on the Cleveland line provides direct city access, which reduces the car dependency that comes with a lot of Brisbane's middle ring. For dual-income households where at least one person commutes to the CBD, that train connection is a material factor in the suburb's desirability. The ridgeline streets along the main elevated spine offer the best views, with the north-facing slopes of the suburb being the most consistently sought after by buyers who understand the suburb's geography.

Whites Hill State College at the secondary level is one of the most significant factors in Norman Park's demand story. It's a catchment that families plan around, and the competition for homes within it is real. The character homes here, mostly Queenslanders and post-war weatherboards, many renovated to a high standard, reflect the owner-occupier commitment that keeps the housing stock in good shape.

The flatter sections south of the ridge are slightly less competitive but still well serviced by the suburb's amenity. If you own in Norman Park and you're thinking about what the current market could mean for you, I'd be glad to walk you through the data.
