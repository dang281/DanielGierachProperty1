## Caption

Norman Park is the kind of suburb where residents tend not to leave voluntarily. When homes do come to market, buyers pay attention.

The elevation is a defining feature. Streets like Belmont Road, Carawa Street, and the ridgeline running above Norman Creek offer views toward the river and the city that are genuinely rare at this distance from the CBD. Character homes, mostly Queenslanders and post-war timber houses, sit on blocks that have not been subdivided into oblivion, giving the suburb a spaciousness that the inner ring often loses.

The City Glider bus route along Old Cleveland Road gives residents a direct, frequent connection to the CBD without needing to drive. For families, the Whites Hill State College catchment is a significant drawcard that keeps demand for the right addresses elevated above the broader market.

The buyer profile is predominantly families and downsizers. Families target Norman Park for the school catchment and the neighbourhood character. Downsizers come from within the suburb or from nearby Morningside and Hawthorne, wanting to stay in the inner east but move into something more manageable.

Median house price sits around $1.75 million, with riverfront-adjacent and view homes pushing well above that. Stock is genuinely limited, and the suburb's constrained size means competition stays firm even when broader market volumes soften.
