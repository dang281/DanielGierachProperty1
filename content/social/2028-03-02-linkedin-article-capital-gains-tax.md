## Caption

Most homeowners assume that selling their primary residence is entirely free of capital gains tax. For the majority, that is correct. But the main residence exemption is not automatic in every situation, and the cases where it does not apply fully tend to catch sellers off guard.

If you rented out your home at any point before selling, the exemption is proportional. The years of private use are exempt. The years of rental use are not. If you used part of your home as a registered business premises or claimed a home office tax deduction against rental income, the exemption can be affected.

The six-year absence rule allows sellers to maintain the main residence exemption on a property they are not living in, but only if they have not nominated another property as their main residence during that period.

These are not unusual circumstances. They affect a meaningful number of inner-east sellers, particularly investors who at some point lived in the property they are now selling, and long-term owners who have rented for periods during interstate relocations.

The guidance here is not mine to give. A tax accountant with property experience should be consulted before you accept a sale contract, not after.
