## Caption

Queensland introduced mandatory seller disclosure obligations under the Property Law Act 2023. Most vendors who have not sold recently are not aware of what this requires.

The practical effect: before a buyer signs a contract for a residential property, the vendor must provide a completed Seller Disclosure Statement. This statement covers title information, lot and plan references, registered encumbrances, easements, and other matters that may affect the property.

The consequence of not providing this statement correctly is significant. A buyer can terminate the contract at any time before settlement if the disclosure was not properly provided. That termination right exists even if the buyer was already aware of the matters that should have been disclosed.

For vendors, the practical implication is that the disclosure statement should be prepared before the campaign launches, not scrambled together when a buyer makes an offer. Surprises in the disclosure document that emerge after a buyer has signed create uncertainty and negotiation risk at exactly the wrong point in the transaction.

The areas most likely to catch inner east vendors off-guard are easements, flood overlay designations, and any heritage or character overlay that applies to the property. These are not defects. They are registrations and designations that affect what can be done with the property, and buyers have a right to know about them before they are contractually committed.

Your solicitor should prepare the disclosure statement as part of their pre-campaign engagement, not as an afterthought at contract stage.
