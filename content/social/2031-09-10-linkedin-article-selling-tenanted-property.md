## Caption

Investors who decide to sell during spring face a specific operational challenge if the property is still tenanted. Every open home requires 24 hours written notice to the tenant. In a typical inner east spring campaign of four to six weeks with weekend open homes and midweek inspections, that is a significant notice obligation that needs to be managed consistently throughout the campaign. Vendors who have not discussed this with their property manager before launching often discover the friction mid-campaign, when it is difficult to address without disrupting the selling process.

Tenancy law in Queensland also governs what a vendor can and cannot require of a tenant during a sales campaign. A tenant is entitled to reasonable quiet enjoyment of the property. They are not obligated to keep the property in a particular presentation standard for inspections beyond what the tenancy agreement requires. Understanding the practical limits of what can be asked and what cannot is part of selling a tenanted property successfully.

Spring is when many investors decide to sell, whether to capitalise on strong buyer demand, respond to a lease expiry, or restructure a portfolio after reviewing EOFY figures. The decision to sell is often made quickly in spring. The operational planning required to run a smooth tenanted campaign is best done before the launch decision is made, not after.

Buyers also respond differently to tenanted properties. The full article explains the vendor's obligations, the tenant's rights, and the practical considerations that determine how a tenanted spring campaign is best run.
