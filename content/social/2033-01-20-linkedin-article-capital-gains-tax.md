## Caption

Most homeowners in Brisbane's inner east assume that when they sell their primary residence, there is no capital gains tax to deal with. For a property that has been their home and nothing else, that assumption is correct.

But a significant portion of inner east vendors are selling properties that were rented at some point. Perhaps it was an investment before they moved in. Perhaps they moved away for two years and leased it. Perhaps there was a gap between purchase and occupancy. In all of these cases, the main residence CGT exemption is either partial or, in some scenarios, unavailable entirely.

The calculation is not especially complex but the implications can be. The Australian Tax Office uses a time-based formula: the proportion of the ownership period the property was your primary residence versus when it was not. If you owned the property for ten years and rented it for two, you are looking at a 20% reduction in your CGT exemption on whatever capital gain you have made.

What makes this issue expensive is not the calculation. It is the timing. Most vendors discover it at campaign time, when the focus is on presentation, pricing and open homes, rather than twelve months earlier when there was time to plan around it.

A conversation with an accountant before you list, not after, is the relevant advice here.
