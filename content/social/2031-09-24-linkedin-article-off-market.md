## Caption

In a strong spring market, the argument for going off-market is weaker than at any other time of year. Spring buyer competition is at its seasonal peak. A well-run spring campaign creates the conditions under which buyer competition moves price. Off-market in spring removes exactly the mechanism that spring is designed to generate. The vendor who goes off-market in October is opting out of the most competitive buyer pool of the year in exchange for a quicker and quieter process.

That trade-off can be the right one in specific circumstances. A vendor who has a genuine reason to avoid a public campaign, or who has been approached by a buyer they believe has paid a genuine price, may have sound reasons for the off-market path. But the decision should be made on those specific grounds, not because an agent with a buyer made an approach and the conversation happened before the vendor had thought through the alternative.

Late September is when those approaches become common. Agents with buyer databases know spring is opening and begin reaching out to potential vendors with buyer interest before the market is fully active. Understanding what an off-market approach actually means for price outcome is the context a vendor needs before agreeing to proceed.

The full article explains how off-market sales work in Brisbane, when they are appropriate, and what vendors should weigh before agreeing to sell without a public campaign.
