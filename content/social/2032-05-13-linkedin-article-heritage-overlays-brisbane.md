## Caption

The thing most buyers and sellers get wrong about heritage overlays is assuming they automatically mean problems. That is not accurate, and acting on that assumption can cost you.

A heritage overlay does restrict certain types of work. You cannot simply knock down a front fence that forms part of the heritage character, repaint the exterior in colours that conflict with the original palette, or add a second storey that alters the roofline without going through council. That is real, and anyone buying or selling a property with an overlay needs to understand what it means for their plans.

But overlays also protect the character that makes a street desirable. The Queenslanders in Hawthorne and Balmoral, the federation cottages in certain pockets of Norman Park, the pre-war homes along the river: these are properties buyers compete for specifically because they cannot be easily replicated. The overlay is part of what maintains that.

What matters practically is knowing what you can and cannot do before you buy, and knowing how to present those constraints and opportunities clearly when you sell. A seller who has done their renovation within heritage guidelines and can document it is in a much stronger position than one who cannot.

I have written about how to check whether a Brisbane property sits under a heritage overlay, what the approval process looks like, and what it means for buyers assessing development potential. Worth reading before you make any assumptions.
