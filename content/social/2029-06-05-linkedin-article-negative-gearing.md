## Caption

Negative gearing is a holding strategy, not an exit strategy. The point at which most investors need to reassess is when they are deciding whether to continue holding or sell.

The holding case for a negatively geared property depends on capital growth outpacing the annual cash shortfall. When that growth assumption changes, or when other financial priorities shift, the decision to sell becomes more relevant. The tax position on that sale is not the same as the tax position during the hold period.

Settlement timing relative to 30 June is one of the more practical decisions an investor can make before launching a campaign. If the property has appreciated significantly, a capital gain realised in one financial year versus another can shift a meaningful amount of tax forward by twelve months. The amount does not change. The timing of when you pay it does.

The negative gearing claim itself ceases at settlement, which means the final portion of the financial year does not produce the same tax offset that the holding period generated. That transition is worth modelling with an accountant before setting the campaign timeline.

These are the kinds of questions I raise at the initial appraisal for investor-owned properties. The answers change the campaign strategy in some cases and always give the vendor a clearer picture of their net position.
