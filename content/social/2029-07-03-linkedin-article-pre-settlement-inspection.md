## Caption

The pre-settlement inspection is not a formality. Most sellers treat it like one, and that is where the problems start.

In Queensland, buyers are entitled to one pre-settlement inspection in the days before settlement. The purpose is straightforward: to confirm the property is in the same condition it was in at the contract date. When it is not, the buyer has grounds to delay settlement, request a price adjustment, or withhold funds in escrow until issues are resolved.

The most common triggers are things sellers do not think twice about. Removalists scraping walls and door frames. A fixture that was included in the contract being taken by the seller because they decided they wanted it. Air conditioning units that worked on contract day no longer functioning on inspection day. None of these feel significant in the moment, but each one gives the buyer something to raise at the worst possible time.

The smarter approach is to treat the pre-settlement inspection as a deadline to work back from. Walk the property yourself two to three days before. Check every inclusion listed in the contract is still in place. Confirm anything that was working on contract day is still working.

The article covers what actually constitutes a change in condition, how disputes play out when sellers are unprepared, and a practical checklist for getting ahead of the inspection rather than reacting to it.
