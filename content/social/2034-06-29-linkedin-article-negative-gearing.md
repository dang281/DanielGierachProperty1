## Caption

For investors selling a negatively geared property, settlement timing relative to 30 June is one of the most consequential decisions in the transaction. It determines which financial year the capital gain falls into, and that decision can shift a significant tax liability.

The date that matters is the settlement date, not the date the contract is signed. A contract signed in late May that settles on 1 July puts the capital gain into the following financial year, giving the investor an extra 12 months before the tax falls due and the opportunity to plan around it.

The inverse applies when an investor needs the capital gain to fall in the current financial year, for example to offset other losses. In that case, ensuring settlement occurs before 30 June is the priority.

The negative gearing claim on the property ceases at settlement, not at signing. For a property that has been negatively geared, the interest deductions stop when the property changes hands. This is straightforward, but it affects the investor's cashflow and tax position for the period between signing and settling.

These are decisions worth making deliberately, in consultation with an accountant, before the campaign launches rather than when an offer arrives and the settlement date is being negotiated under time pressure.
