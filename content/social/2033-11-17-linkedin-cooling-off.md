## Caption

Most sellers have this backwards. The cooling-off period is a right that belongs to the buyer, not the vendor. Sellers cannot waive it.

This is one of the most common misunderstandings I encounter from vendors who have heard about cooling-off periods and assume it is a mutual protection that either party can adjust. It is not. In Queensland, the 5-business-day cooling-off right is a statutory protection that exists for the buyer's benefit. A vendor who asks a buyer to give it up cannot legally make that request binding. The right exists by law, and the parties cannot contract out of it.

The position is entirely different at auction. Contracts formed at auction in Queensland carry no cooling-off period at all. When the hammer falls, the successful bidder is unconditionally bound. There is no 5-business-day window, no ability to exit without penalty. This is one of the most structurally important differences between buying at auction and buying by private treaty, and it is a difference that matters enormously for buyers who have not done their due diligence before bidding.

The exit cost is the third thing most people do not know. Exercising the cooling-off right is not free. The buyer forfeits 0.25% of the purchase price. On a $1.4M property that is $3,500. Buyers who treat the cooling-off period as a zero-cost way to test a purchase are mistaken about the actual cost.

Understanding these three points before you exchange contracts, whether buying at auction or by private treaty, puts you in a better position than the majority of buyers I deal with at the start of a transaction.
