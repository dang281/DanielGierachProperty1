## Caption

The biggest mistake sellers make when choosing between auction and private treaty is treating it as a personal preference rather than a strategic decision based on their property and their market.

Auction works well when you have a property that is genuinely hard to compare. A character home with unusual features, a block with development upside, a property that will attract buyers from multiple segments: these are situations where the competitive environment of an auction room produces a result that a fixed price negotiation often does not. Buyers who are uncertain about value will bid. Buyers who are anchored to a price on a private treaty listing will often just wait.

Private treaty works well when your buyer pool is narrow, when finance conditions are likely to be required, or when the property is straightforward enough that a market range can be established and buyers can make clean decisions within it. It also works better in markets where buyer urgency is lower and a longer campaign is needed to find the right person.

The third option, expressions of interest, sits between the two. It is common in the inner east for premium properties and works when you want the competitive dynamic of a deadline without the public transparency of an auction room.

In an Olympic year with a compressed pre-Games window, the method you choose also affects your settlement timeline. That is worth factoring in.

I have written a full breakdown of how the three methods work in Brisbane, when each one is most appropriate, and what questions to ask your agent before you decide.
