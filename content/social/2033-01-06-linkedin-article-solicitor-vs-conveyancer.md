## Caption

The standard sequence most Queensland property buyers follow: attend open homes, find a property, sign the contract, then call their solicitor. That sequence is backwards and it costs some of them dearly.

In Queensland, the contract of sale is a legally binding document the moment it is signed by both parties. The five-business-day cooling-off period gives buyers a window, but exercising it comes at a cost: the vendor can retain 0.25% of the purchase price. On a $1.5 million inner east property, that is $3,750 gone because the legal advice came too late.

The right sequence: identify a property you are serious about, engage a solicitor or conveyancer before the contract is presented, and have them review the contract conditions before you sign. This adds less than 24 to 48 hours to the process in most cases and it eliminates the scenario where you are bound to something your legal adviser later identifies as a problem.

The distinction between a solicitor and a conveyancer also matters in Queensland. Both can handle a standard residential transaction. But if your property has a heritage overlay, an easement question, or an unusual contract condition, the scope of advice a solicitor can provide is broader.

Most buyers do not know the difference until they need to.
