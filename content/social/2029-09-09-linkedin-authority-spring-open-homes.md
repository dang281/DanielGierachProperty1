## Caption

Open home attendance is one of the first things vendors ask about after an inspection weekend. It is a natural question. But raw numbers, taken in isolation, tell you very little about how a campaign is actually tracking.

What matters is not how many people walked through the door, but who they were and what they did while they were there. A Saturday inspection with fifteen groups that produces no contract requests and no follow-up calls is a worse result than one with six groups where three people ask for contracts and two come back for a second look. Volume is a comfort metric. Behaviour is the useful metric.

The pattern I watch across the opening weeks of a spring campaign is the registration quality. Are buyers providing genuine contact details? Are they asking specific questions about the property, the building and pest history, the flood status? Are they returning for second inspections? Each of these behaviours signals a buyer who is genuinely qualified and actively assessing, not just looking to see what is on the market.

If attendance is strong but contract requests are absent after the first two or three open homes, that is a clear signal. It means the right buyers are coming through but something in the property, the price, or the marketing is creating hesitation. The sooner that signal is acted on, the better the outcome. Campaigns that sit for three or four weeks hoping attendance will convert on its own rarely finish well.

A good agent interprets the attendance data from the first open home and has a conversation with the vendor the same evening. That conversation shapes what happens in the second week.
