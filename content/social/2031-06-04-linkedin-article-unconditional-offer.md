## Caption

A seller will often accept a lower unconditional offer over a higher conditional one. The reason is certainty and time, not sentiment. The conditional period carries two to three weeks of risk that unconditional removes entirely.

That two to three weeks is not a neutral period from the vendor's perspective. During the conditional period, the vendor cannot accept another offer without the contract falling over first. If a finance condition fails, or a building and pest inspection produces a result the buyer uses to exit or renegotiate, the vendor re-enters the market with a property that has now been under contract and released. That history affects buyer perception. The vendor who accepts a lower unconditional offer is not making a concession. They are often making a rational decision about risk and time.

The practical implication for buyers is that unconditional capacity is genuine negotiating leverage on price. A buyer who has a formal pre-approval from their lender has converted the finance condition from an open question to a near formality. Completing an independent building and pest inspection before making an offer removes the need for a condition entirely and allows the buyer to make a clean offer with full knowledge of the property's condition. Those two steps, pre-approval and a pre-offer inspection, are what convert a conditional buyer into an unconditional one.

Buyers who have been in the market since autumn and are targeting a transaction before the end of the year are the group most likely to have the knowledge and the preparation to go unconditional. This article explains exactly how to do it and what it costs.
