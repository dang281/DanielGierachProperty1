## Caption

The spring assumption is reasonable. More buyers, more competition, better prices. That is the general logic and it has a solid basis in how the market moves seasonally.

What the logic does not account for is competition on the supply side. The vendors who are reasoning that spring is a better time to sell are reasoning the same way at the same time. When the spring market opens in September, the number of properties coming to market in the inner east rises sharply. The buyer pool expands, but so does the listing pool. The buyer-to-listing ratio, which is what determines how much competition a property generates, does not always improve in the way the spring assumption suggests.

There is also a timing reality worth noting. Vendors who decide in September to go to market are typically not ready to launch until October. A property that launches in October and does not sell before mid-November is heading into the pre-Christmas wind-down with limited time before buyer attention shifts.

Winter campaigns, run with realistic pricing and solid presentation, often resolve in four to six weeks with motivated buyers who are active precisely because the market is quieter. That result, achieved in June or July, nets the same outcome without the spring supply competition.

This is not an argument against spring. It is an argument for calculating the actual risk of waiting, rather than assuming spring is automatically better.
