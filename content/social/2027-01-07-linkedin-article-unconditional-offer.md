## Caption

A seller reviewing two offers on the same property: one at $1,050,000 with a finance condition and a building and pest clause, the other at $1,010,000 unconditional.

Conventional logic says the higher number wins. In practice, many sellers choose the lower one.

The reason is risk. A conditional offer means the deal is not done. The buyer still needs bank approval. The building and pest inspection might surface something. The finance clause might lapse. Every day a conditional contract sits open is a day the seller is effectively off the market, turning away other potential buyers, with no guarantee the deal completes.

An unconditional offer removes that window entirely. The seller knows the property is sold. Settlement is a formality.

For buyers, understanding this shifts how you approach negotiations. Coming in unconditional is a meaningful statement of commitment. Sellers respond to it, often with price concessions that close the gap between what you offered and what a conditional buyer offered at a higher number.

The decision to go unconditional is not one to make lightly. It requires having your finance approved, your due diligence done, and your solicitor comfortable. But for a buyer who has those pieces in place, it is one of the most effective tools available.
