## Caption

The pre-settlement inspection is the buyer's final walkthrough before the funds transfer and title changes hands. Under Queensland contract law, the property must be in substantially the same condition as it was when the contract was signed. If it is not, the buyer has grounds to delay or dispute settlement.

In practice, most pre-settlement inspections go smoothly. But the ones that do not are almost entirely avoidable, and the problems usually follow one of three patterns.

First, removalist damage. Things that were fine when the property was listed and photographed but were bumped, scratched, or broken during the move. A vendor who has walked through the property 24 to 48 hours after the removalists have finished, before the buyer arrives, will catch these before they become a dispute.

Second, missing inclusions. If the contract lists specific items that were included in the sale, those items need to be there. The dishwasher, the outdoor blinds, the shed, the chandelier in the dining room: anything listed in the contract stays unless both parties agree to a change in writing.

Third, a property that has deteriorated since exchange. Maintenance that was deferred, damage from a storm, a pool that was not maintained. The seller's obligation is to hand over the property in contract condition.
