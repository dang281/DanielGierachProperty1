## Caption

The seller cannot waive the 5-business-day cooling-off period in Queensland. It is a buyer's statutory right. But auction contracts have no cooling-off at all.

This is one of the more commonly misunderstood aspects of Queensland property law, and the misunderstanding cuts both ways. Buyers sometimes think they have more protection than they do. Sellers sometimes think the cooling-off period can be negotiated away as a condition. Neither is correct.

Here is what the rules actually say:

For private treaty sales, the buyer has five business days after the contract is formed to terminate without penalty beyond the statutory amount, which is currently 0.25% of the purchase price. The seller cannot waive this period, and any attempt to do so in a contract is unenforceable.

For auction contracts, there is no cooling-off period. When the hammer falls and the contract is signed on auction day, it is unconditional. The buyer is committed. This is why preparing your finance and building inspection before auction day is not optional.

A Section 66W certificate allows a buyer to waive the cooling-off period voluntarily in a private treaty sale. This is usually done when a buyer wants to make their offer more attractive to a seller by removing the period, but it must be done by the buyer's solicitor, not the seller's.

The cooling-off period cannot be used to renegotiate price. It is a right to terminate, not a right to amend.

Understanding these rules before you sign, or before you list, avoids the kind of confusion that costs time and goodwill mid-transaction.
