## Caption

Off-market sales happen regularly in Brisbane's inner east. A buyer who has been looking for months hears about a property before it launches. A deal is done quietly, without a campaign, without open homes, without public exposure. For vendors who value privacy or want a straightforward process, it sounds appealing.

The thing being traded is buyer competition, and that trade is rarely in the vendor's favour.

A full marketing campaign exposes a property to the broadest pool of qualified buyers the market can produce. Competition between buyers produces results that no single motivated buyer can replicate alone. The inner east's most significant sale results, the ones that set suburb records or exceed the vendor's expectation, happen at public campaigns where multiple buyers compete to secure a property they want.

Off-market results reflect what one buyer is prepared to pay without competition. That number is usually lower than what a competitive campaign would have produced, sometimes by a small margin and sometimes by a significant one.

There are genuine situations where off-market makes sense: a vendor with medical or privacy concerns, a property with a sitting tenant situation that makes open homes difficult, or a case where the buyer is known to the vendor and both sides agree the price is fair. These are real circumstances.

The article below covers how to evaluate an off-market approach, what questions to ask, and when saying no to a convenient offer is the right financial decision.
