## Caption

When three or more registered bidders show up to an inner east auction, the dynamic in the room changes. It stops being a conversation about price and becomes something else entirely. It becomes a test of who is most committed.

The figure I watch most closely is not the final price. It is the gap between the opening bid and the reserve. When a first bid comes in fast and reasonably close to where the reserve sits, it tells me that the buyer who opened has done their homework. They know the comparable sales, they know what the property is worth, and they have made a deliberate decision to open strong rather than probe. That kind of bidder has usually attended multiple auctions in the area and has been outbid before.

A slow start to an auction tells a different story. When the auctioneer has to work hard to get the opening bid, it usually means there are fewer participants who have fully committed. They may be registered, but they have not yet decided they want the property enough to drive the bidding. That can change the moment competition develops, but in a slow-starting auction the vendor needs to have a clear view of their reserve and a clear plan for negotiation if the bidding stalls.

May auctions in the inner east tend to attract a buyer pool that has been active through autumn. These are not fresh participants. Most of them have been looking for six to twelve weeks, which means they are not easily deterred by competition. When that pool is deep, auctions in May can produce some of the year's strongest results.
