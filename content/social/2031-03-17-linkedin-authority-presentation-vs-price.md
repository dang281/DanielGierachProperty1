## Caption

The relationship between property presentation and final sale price is not about interior design. It is about photography, and then about competition. The chain runs in one direction: better presentation produces better photography, better photography produces more enquiries, more enquiries produces more buyer competition at open homes, and more competition is what moves price above the baseline.

Styling a property for a campaign is, in practical terms, a photography investment. Most buyers form their shortlist by scrolling through listings on their phone. A property that photographs well gets onto more shortlists. A property that photographs poorly, even if it is structurally excellent, will have fewer people walking through the door on the first Saturday. Fewer buyers through the door in week one means a thinner buyer pool for the whole campaign.

The inner east buyer is sophisticated. They are not easily impressed by cosmetic renovation that covers real problems, and they will find those problems before exchange. A fresh coat of paint over obvious structural movement, or styling furniture arranged to conceal a floor that needs attention, does not fool a buyer who has already paid for a building and pest report on three other properties. The presentation conversation is about making a good property look its best, not about making a poor property look like something it is not.

Where presentation investment tends to produce the clearest return is in decluttering, professional cleaning, minor repairs that are visible in photography, and a coherent furniture arrangement that helps buyers understand how the space works. Those improvements are modest in cost and direct in effect.
