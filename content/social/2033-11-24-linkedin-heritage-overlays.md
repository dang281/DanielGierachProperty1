## Caption

A heritage overlay in Brisbane does not prevent renovation. It requires BCC approval for street-facing changes. Most inner east buyers and sellers cannot tell which properties carry an overlay without checking.

This is something I cover with buyers and vendors regularly, because the assumption that heritage listing means a property is untouchable is simply wrong. The reality is considerably more workable, and understanding it changes how people think about buying or selling a character home.

Internal renovations at a heritage-listed or heritage-precinct property typically proceed without any heritage approval requirement at all. Brisbane City Council's protections are focused on the character that is visible from the street, including the facade, the verandah, the roofline, and the external materials that define the home's contribution to the streetscape. What happens inside the walls is generally a matter for the owner, not the heritage framework.

For buyers, the overlay often functions as a positive rather than a constraint. In suburbs like Balmoral, Hawthorne, and Norman Park, buyers who have spent months searching for a particular architectural character are buying not just one property but the expectation that the surrounding streetscape will remain intact. Heritage precincts protect that. The buyer who paid a premium for an elevated Queenslander on a tree-lined street has a reasonable expectation that the house next door will not be demolished for a two-storey rendered townhouse within eighteen months.

Vendors preparing a heritage property for sale should confirm with BCC what pre-sale work requires approval before starting. Some external repairs, while straightforward, may need a heritage approval that is easy to obtain but takes time to process. Doing that groundwork before the campaign starts avoids delays and cost surprises mid-campaign.
