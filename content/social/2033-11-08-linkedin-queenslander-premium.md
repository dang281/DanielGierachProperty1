## Caption

When a Queenslander in Balmoral or Hawthorne sells above median, the result is sometimes attributed to the market being hot or the street being good. Those things help. But they are not the real driver.

The buyer who purchases a well-positioned, well-maintained Queenslander in the inner east is not comparing that home to everything else available. They have typically been searching for months. They know the streets they want. They know what good condition looks like and how rare it is. By the time they walk through the right property, they are not asking whether this is better than other options. They are asking whether this is the one they have been waiting for. That is a very different decision-making state.

From where I stand at an open home, that buyer is identifiable. They slow down in rooms. They check the condition of the stumps, the roof line, the verandah boards. They are not doing the general browse that a first-inspection buyer does. They are confirming a decision they have almost already made. And when two or three buyers in that state find the same property at the same time, the result reflects their competition, not just the market's movement.

This is why character homes in Balmoral, Hawthorne, Norman Park, and Camp Hill often achieve results that look unusual next to the street's average. The average includes properties those buyers passed over. It does not capture the premium a motivated and specific buyer will pay when the right combination of suburb, architecture, and condition finally arrives on the market.

Presentation and timing still matter. A Queenslander that needs work sells to a renovator, not to the buyer who has been searching for a finished home. Condition and the right buyer need to arrive at the same time.
