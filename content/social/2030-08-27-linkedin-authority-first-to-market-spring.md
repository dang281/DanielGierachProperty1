## Caption

The conventional wisdom about spring property is that October is when buyers arrive. That is partly true. October brings volume. But volume cuts both ways.

The buyers who are active in the last two weeks of August and the first two weeks of September are, in my experience, among the most committed buyers in the market. They have been searching through winter. They have been to many open homes. They are not waiting for the calendar to say spring before they act. When a well-prepared property appears in their target suburb at a price that aligns with comparable sales, they move quickly. They write offers. They have finance ready. They do not need the social proof of a crowd at an open home to feel confident about their decision.

The risk vendors think about when considering a late August launch is whether enough buyers will show up. That is a fair question, and for most well-presented inner east properties with broad appeal, the answer is yes. The risk vendors tend not to think about is what happens when they launch in mid-October alongside ten other properties in the same suburb, all competing for the same pool of buyers, all trying to create urgency at the same time.

Competition concentrates buyer attention. When there are twelve comparable homes on the market simultaneously, buyers have options and they know it. The negotiating dynamic shifts. Days on market increase. Price alignment becomes harder to hold.

The first-to-market property in spring does not face that dynamic. It has the attention of committed buyers without the noise. That is not a small advantage.
