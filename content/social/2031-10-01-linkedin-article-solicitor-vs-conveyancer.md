## Caption

Most buyers call a solicitor after they have already signed the contract. That is the wrong sequence.

The moment a contract is signed, the buyer has locked in every term on that page. The finance deadline, the settlement date, the inclusions list, the special conditions. A solicitor reviewing the contract before signing can catch things that cost thousands to fix after the fact, and some things cannot be fixed at all once both parties have executed.

Missing inclusions are one of the most common issues. A buyer assumes the outdoor blinds, the garden shed, or the integrated dishwasher are coming with the property. They are not listed as inclusions. The contract is signed. Now it is a negotiation or a loss.

Finance deadline risk is another. A 14-day finance condition looks standard but may not give a buyer enough time if the lender requires a valuation or additional documentation. A solicitor reviewing the timeline against the buyer's actual lending situation can flag this before the contract is binding.

The difference between a solicitor and a conveyancer in Queensland comes down to scope. A licensed conveyancer handles the transfer of title. A solicitor can advise on the contract terms, identify legal risk, and give legal advice. For most purchases in the inner east, where properties carry heritage overlays, easements, or body corporate structures, having a solicitor review the contract first is worth the additional cost.
