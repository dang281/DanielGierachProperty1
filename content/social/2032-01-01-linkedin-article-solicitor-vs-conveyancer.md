## Caption

Most buyers in Queensland sign the contract first, then call their solicitor or conveyancer. That sequence is backwards, and it costs people money every year.

The five-business-day cooling-off period sounds like a safety net, but it is not a free exit. If you withdraw during cooling-off, you forfeit 0.25% of the purchase price. On a $1.2 million property that is $3,000 gone before you have even moved in. More importantly, the cooling-off period starts from the moment the buyer receives the signed contract, not from settlement. The window closes faster than most people expect.

What happens when you get legal review before signing? You catch missing inclusions. Air conditioning, dishwashers, solar systems and pool equipment are not automatically included just because they are in the property. If they are not listed in the contract, they go with the seller. You also catch risky special conditions, or the absence of conditions you should have requested.

The other question buyers ask is whether to use a solicitor or a licensed conveyancer. Both are legally qualified to handle property transfers in Queensland. The distinction matters most when a transaction has complications: easements, body corporate disputes, incomplete title issues, or unusual contract conditions. For straightforward purchases, a conveyancer is often sufficient. For anything complex, a solicitor with property law experience is worth the additional cost.
