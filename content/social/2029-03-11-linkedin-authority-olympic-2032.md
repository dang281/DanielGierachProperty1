## Caption

With three years until the Brisbane 2032 Olympics, the infrastructure conversation has moved from planning to delivery.

For the inner east specifically, the relevant developments are not primarily in the sporting venues themselves. They are in the transport and urban infrastructure upgrades that happen around major events and tend to stay long after the athletes leave.

Cross River Rail, now operational, changed the connectivity of the inner south and inner east in ways that are still being priced into the market. The stations at Woolloongabba and Boggo Road have already changed the equation for buyers looking at Coorparoo, Camp Hill, and the Kangaroo Point corridor. That effect compounds over time.

The 2032 precinct infrastructure, centred on Woolloongabba, brings additional investment to a part of Brisbane that was already seeing significant development pressure. For owners in suburbs adjacent to that precinct, the downstream effect on land values is a legitimate factor in long-term planning.

What is worth avoiding is the speculative framing that sometimes surrounds Olympic cycles. Not every suburb within 15 kilometres of Brisbane benefits uniformly, and the properties that perform best through these cycles are the ones with genuine underlying demand, not just proximity to a venue.

For the inner east, the fundamentals were strong before the Olympic announcement and remain so independently of it. The infrastructure is an additional tailwind, not the primary story.
