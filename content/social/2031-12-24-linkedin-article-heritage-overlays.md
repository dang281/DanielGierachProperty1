## Caption

A heritage overlay in Brisbane does not stop you from renovating or improving your property. Most buyers who discover an overlay on a property they are considering assume it means the property is frozen as-is. That is not correct.

What a heritage overlay does require is Brisbane City Council approval for changes to the street-facing elements of the building. The front facade, the roof form, the verandah, and the materials visible from the street are the elements the overlay primarily governs. Internal alterations, extensions to the rear, and additions that are not visible from the public domain are generally subject to a less restrictive approval pathway.

The distinction matters for buyers who are planning a renovation. A buyer who wants to open up the back of a heritage-listed cottage, add a contemporary extension, and redo the kitchen and bathrooms entirely can almost certainly do all of that. The front of the house, in its historic form, is the element that requires careful management. An experienced architect or town planner who works with heritage approvals in Brisbane can give you a clear picture of what is possible before you make an offer.

Many of the most sought-after homes in the inner east carry heritage overlays. The Queenslanders and federation-era properties in Bulimba, Hawthorne, Norman Park, and Balmoral that attract premium prices are often heritage-listed. The overlay does not suppress their value. In most cases it contributes to it.

The article below explains the overlay types, the approval process, and how to assess the implications for your renovation plans before making an offer.
