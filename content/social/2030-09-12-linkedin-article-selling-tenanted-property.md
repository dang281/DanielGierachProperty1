## Caption

If your tenant is on a fixed-term lease that extends past your proposed settlement date, the buyer takes over that lease. They do not get vacant possession. Most sellers do not realise this until after they have accepted an offer and the solicitor flags it.

The fix is simple, but it has to happen before the campaign launches: match your proposed settlement date to the lease expiry date, or have a clear plan for what happens if they do not align. Selling with a tenant in place is entirely manageable. Selling with a tenant and a mismatched settlement date is the kind of problem that costs vendors price, time, or both.

Beyond the lease term, there are two other things to sort before you go to market. First, inspection rights in Queensland require proper notice to tenants: 24 hours minimum, in writing, with a reasonable time of day. A tenant who is not cooperative about access can significantly reduce the quality and frequency of your open homes. Building that relationship before the campaign begins is worth the effort. Second, presentation matters even in a tenanted property. A brief conversation with your tenant about the condition of the property during the campaign, and whether staging or decluttering is appropriate, can have a real effect on the result.

Tenanted sales in the inner east are common. Done well, they work. The full process is at danielgierach.com/insights/selling-tenanted-property-queensland.
