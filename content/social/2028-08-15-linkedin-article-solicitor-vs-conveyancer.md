## Caption

Most buyers call their solicitor after they have signed the contract. That sequence is backwards.

The contract review phase is where most of the value in legal advice sits. By the time the contract is signed and the finance condition clock is running, the window to adjust inclusions, flag a problematic special condition, or renegotiate a finance deadline that is tighter than your lender can meet is largely closed. Getting a solicitor involved before you sign takes twenty-four hours and rarely costs more than the standard conveyancing fee.

The solicitor versus conveyancer question matters in specific situations. Both can handle a standard Queensland residential purchase. The difference is scope: a licensed conveyancer can prepare and review standard contracts, but cannot give legal advice on a contract dispute, a special condition you do not understand, or a complex title issue. For a straightforward owner-occupier purchase of an established home, an experienced conveyancer is a legitimate choice. For a property with an easement, unusual special conditions, or a complex settlement structure, a solicitor is the more appropriate choice.

The full article explains the distinction in practical terms, including when each is appropriate and what to ask when you are making the decision.
