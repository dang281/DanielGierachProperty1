## Caption

Clearance rates and median prices are the statistics that get the headlines. Days on market is the one that tells you what is actually happening.

When a well-presented property in a strong inner east suburb is sitting for six or seven weeks, the market is sending a signal. Either the price is wrong, the presentation is creating doubt, or both. The buyer pool has seen it, processed it, and chosen not to engage.

When comparable properties are clearing in 14 to 21 days, vendors who are sitting at six or seven weeks do not have a market problem. They have a pricing or presentation problem. Those are solvable. The mistake is treating the long days-on-market figure as evidence that the market is quiet when the evidence is the opposite.

Through the tail end of this autumn, the inner east properties selling quickly are sharing a profile: priced within the range that recent comparable sales support, presented without deferred maintenance creating doubt for buyers, and run by agents who communicate actively with the buyer pool.

Properties that are sitting share a different profile: priced above what recent comparable sales justify, with presentation issues that buyers are factoring into their offers, and listed long enough that buyers are now asking what is wrong with it.

Days on market does not lie. It accumulates publicly and it shapes buyer perception.
