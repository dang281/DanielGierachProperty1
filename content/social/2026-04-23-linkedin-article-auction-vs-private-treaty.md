## Caption

Choosing how to sell is one of the first strategic decisions in any property campaign. Most sellers leave it to the agent. That is not necessarily wrong, but you should understand what you are agreeing to and why.

The core difference between auction and private treaty is not the price you achieve. It is the certainty you have once a contract is signed.

An auction that sells under the hammer produces an unconditional exchange on the day. The buyer has done their building inspection, arranged their finance, and is committing without conditions. There is no finance clause, no cooling-off period, and no building-and-pest exit. For vendors, that certainty is real.

Private treaty contracts typically include finance and inspection conditions. Until those conditions are satisfied (usually seven to fourteen days after signing), the buyer can exit without penalty. That is not a reason to avoid private treaty. It is a reason to understand the contract you are selling under.

Expressions of interest sit in different territory again: no disclosed price, written offers by a set deadline, complete vendor control over who you negotiate with and in what order. Best suited to high-value or genuinely unique properties where a price range is difficult to establish.

The right method depends on three things: your buyer pool, the property itself, and where the market is sitting at the time you go to market. Those three factors should drive the recommendation, not habit or preference.
