## Caption

Selling a tenanted investment property in Queensland is not the same process as selling an owner-occupied home, and the differences are not minor. The Queensland Residential Tenancies and Rooming Accommodation Act sets out specific obligations that apply from the moment you decide to sell, and they affect how you can run your campaign.

The most immediate one is access. Before every inspection, the tenant must receive 24 hours written notice. That applies to every open home, every private inspection, every photographer visit. The tenant also has the right to be present. If you are planning a campaign with frequent access, you need to account for the fact that each visit requires its own notice and that the tenant can reasonably decline if the process becomes unreasonably disruptive.

The bigger issue, and the one that most vendors discover too late, is the lease position relative to settlement. If the tenant is on a fixed-term lease that extends past the settlement date, the buyer takes on that lease. Vacant possession is not something you can simply offer as a selling point if the tenancy agreement says otherwise. A buyer who wants to move in themselves, or who has plans that require the property vacant, needs to understand what they are actually buying.

Understanding the tenancy position before you list is not optional if you want the campaign to run properly. The link below covers the key requirements in detail. If you have an inner east investment property with a current tenant and you are thinking about whether or when to sell, this is a good starting point for the conversation.
