## Caption

Two properties on the same Morningside street can sell $200,000 apart, and the comparable sales data alone will not tell you why.

That gap is not unusual. It is the product of variables that raw sales records do not capture. One property sits on the high side of the street with a north-east aspect and morning light through the living areas. The other backs onto a stormwater easement and sits partly within a flood overlay. One has been renovated with the right finishes for the buyer demographic active in that suburb. The other has been maintained but not updated, and the buyer who wants to move in without spending money will not pay the same price. One falls cleanly within the Morningside State School catchment. The other does not.

Automated valuation tools pull sales records and apply broad adjustments for size, land area, and time elapsed. They do not read aspect. They do not cross-reference flood overlays against individual lots. They cannot account for the fact that the buyer pool in that street in September 2030 was dominated by young families who paid a premium for school catchment access, while the buyer pool in March 2031 had a higher proportion of investors and downsizers who weighted that factor differently.

They also lag. Most automated estimates reflect sales that settled 60 to 120 days ago. In a market that has moved, or in a street where a genuinely comparable property sold three weeks ago, that lag produces a number that is not current.

Comparable sales are the right starting point for establishing value. They become useful when someone who knows the street, the catchment boundaries, the flood mapping, and the active buyer pool at the time of sale applies judgement to what the numbers show.
