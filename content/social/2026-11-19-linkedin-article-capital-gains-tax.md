## Caption

Most homeowners selling their primary residence assume capital gains tax does not apply. For most, that is correct. The main residence exemption covers you in full, and the sale proceeds are not included in your assessable income.

The exception catches people by surprise.

If the property was rented at any point while you owned it, the exemption is applied proportionally. The ATO calculates the percentage of time it was your principal place of residence versus the time it was income-producing. The gain attributable to the rental period is taxable.

A property lived in for ten years, rented for three years, then sold is not fully exempt. The three-year rental period produces a partial CGT liability, calculated on the portion of the capital gain that corresponds to those three years. Whether the six-year absence rule applies depends on whether you moved back into the property after renting it, and whether you nominated another property as your main residence during that time.

These rules are not complicated, but they require you to have the right records, including the date you moved in, the date you first rented it, rental income declared each year, and any capital improvements made during the rental period.

If your situation involves any period of rental, talk to your accountant before signing a contract to sell. The answer may change your settlement timing.
