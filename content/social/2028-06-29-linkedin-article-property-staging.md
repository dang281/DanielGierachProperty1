## Caption

Staging is primarily a photography investment. The goal is not to make a property look appealing to people walking through on a Saturday morning. The goal is to make it photograph well, because most buyers form their shortlist before they ever set foot inside.

The logic runs in one direction: better photography drives enquiry. Enquiry drives open home attendance. Attendance drives buyer competition. Competition drives price. Staging influences the very first step in that sequence, which means its effect compounds through the entire campaign. A property that generates 40 enquiries from its photography will see better open home numbers than one that generates 20, regardless of how similar the properties are in person.

For a three-bedroom house in Brisbane's inner east, professional staging typically costs between $3,000 and $6,000 depending on the size of the property and the volume of furniture required. On a $1.2 million sale, that cost represents a fraction of a per cent, and the question is not whether you can afford it but whether the photography result justifies it, which in most cases it does.

The properties that benefit most from staging are those being sold by owner-occupiers who are still living in the home, where decluttering and selective furniture replacement changes the photographic result significantly. Vacant properties carry a different risk: empty rooms photograph poorly and make it difficult for buyers to understand scale and liveability. A vacant property going to market should almost always stage.
