## Caption

Most buyers read a building and pest report and look for the summary page. That is the wrong place to start.

The summary categorises issues as major defects, minor defects, or maintenance items. But what moves negotiations is the detail inside the report, specifically the distinction between structural problems, rising damp, and cosmetic issues that read worse than they are. A minor timber pest activity note on an older Queenslander is different from active termite damage. The report does not always make that clear without knowing what to look for.

The three things buyers in Brisbane's inner east consistently misread: rising damp in older homes (usually manageable), subfloor ventilation notes (common and not expensive to address), and general wear listed as maintenance. The things that genuinely matter: active movement in structural members, evidence of live termite activity, and any repairs that have been made over previous damage without full remediation.

A good building and pest inspector will walk you through the report verbally after completing it. If yours did not offer that, call them. Most will.

Understanding what is serious versus what is routine is the difference between a negotiation that is grounded in fact and one driven by anxiety.
