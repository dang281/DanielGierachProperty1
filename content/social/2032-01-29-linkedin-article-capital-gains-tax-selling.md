## Caption

Most homeowners assume the main residence CGT exemption covers them completely when they sell. For many, it does. But for the cohort of vendors who rented their property out at any point, there is a partial CGT liability that most of them do not know about until the campaign is already underway.

The exemption works on a time-based calculation. If a property was your main residence for the full period of ownership, the gain is fully exempt. If it was rented for any period, whether for a year before you moved in, or during an extended work relocation, or after you upgraded to a new home while holding the first one as an investment, the exemption applies only to the proportion of the ownership period that the property was your main residence.

There is a six-year absence rule that provides some relief. If you move out and rent the property but do not establish a new main residence, you can treat the property as your main residence for up to six years. This rule is not widely understood and many vendors who would have qualified for it did not elect it correctly at the time.

The other point that catches people is the post-1985 acquisition date requirement. CGT only applies to properties acquired after 19 September 1985. But a property purchased before 1985 that has been substantially improved since may have a more complex position.

The consequence of discovering a partial CGT liability mid-campaign is that the vendor is making a net proceeds calculation on incomplete information. The article covers the key rules, the common scenarios where partial liability applies, and why the conversation with your accountant needs to happen before you commit to a campaign.
