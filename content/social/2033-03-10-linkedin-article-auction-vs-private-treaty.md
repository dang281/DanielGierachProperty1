## Caption

Most sellers choose a method of sale based on familiarity rather than strategy. The neighbour sold at auction and it went well, so auction is the default. Or the last agent the vendor dealt with used private treaty, so that is what feels comfortable. Neither is a strategic framework.

The method of sale should follow the property and the buyer pool. For a character Queenslander in Hawthorne or Balmoral with a broad buyer pool, competitive interest, and buyers who are comfortable bidding publicly, auction is usually the right choice. It sets a deadline, creates urgency, and allows genuine competition to surface price.

For a property with a narrower buyer pool, or where the likely buyer is finance-dependent and needs a longer decision timeline, auction can work against the vendor. A buyer who cannot act quickly under auction conditions may simply not bid, even if they would have paid an acceptable price in a private treaty process.

Expressions of interest sits between the two. It creates a deadline without the theatre of a public auction. It works well for prestige properties or for markets where the buyer pool is small but high-quality. It requires careful management of the process to ensure genuine competition is not lost.

There is no universally correct answer. What matters is whether the chosen method fits the specific property and the specific buyers who are likely to want it.
