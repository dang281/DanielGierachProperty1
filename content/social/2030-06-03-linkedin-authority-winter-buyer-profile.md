## Caption

The idea that no one buys property in winter is a southern states export. In Brisbane's inner east, winter does reduce open home volumes. But the buyers who are through the door in June and July are, on average, considerably more motivated than the browsers who fill open homes in spring.

Here is the profile I am seeing at inner east inspections right now. Families with kids at school who need to be settled before the next school year starts. Couples who have sold their apartment and need to buy up before their settlement date. Investors who know that winter is when the competition between buyers drops and their negotiating position is strongest.

None of these buyers are in any hurry to wait until September. They have a specific need and a timeline. If the right property comes up, they will buy it.

For sellers, the question is whether your property is presenting well enough to capture a buyer who is going to inspect six properties in one weekend and make a decision quickly. Winter buyers do not browse in the same way spring browsers do. They are efficient. They come back for a second inspection faster, they ask for contracts sooner, and when they are ready to make an offer, they move.

That is a different kind of campaign dynamic, but not a worse one. The sellers who understand winter buyers tend to run shorter campaigns with better results than those who are simply waiting for the spring crowd.
