## Caption

The demand floor created by school catchments in Brisbane's inner east is structural. It does not move with sentiment, and it does not respond to interest rate decisions the way discretionary demand does.

A family who needs to be within the Whites Hill State College catchment to access the school their child is enrolled in, or is about to enrol in, has a constraint that other buyers do not have. They cannot decide that the market feels uncertain and buy in a different suburb instead. The catchment boundary creates a non-negotiable geographic requirement, and families who are in that position will pay to meet it regardless of what the broader market is doing.

The same is true for properties within the Coorparoo State School and Bulimba State School catchments. Buyers who have made a school decision based on enrolment and then need to find a property within the boundary are motivated in a way that is different from buyers who are simply looking for a home in a pleasant suburb. Their search area is defined by a map, not by preference.

The practical consequence of this is observable in how those markets price. A property on the preferred side of a catchment boundary commands a premium that cannot be explained entirely by the property itself. Two properties on streets that run parallel to each other, with similar land area, similar dwelling quality, and similar access to local amenity, will sell at different prices if one is within the catchment and the other is not. That difference reflects buyer demand, not property quality.

What this means for vendors in catchment suburbs is that the demand floor beneath their property is more stable than the broader market sentiment in any given month. And for buyers who are not constrained by a school decision, it is worth understanding that catchment premiums are real numbers they are competing against, not marketing positioning.
