## Caption

The sinking fund balance matters more than the levy rate when evaluating a body corporate property. A complex with a $6,000 per year levy and a well-funded sinking fund is materially better than one with a $3,500 levy and an underfunded fund that will need a special levy to repair the roof or repaint the building. Most buyers look at the levy figure first and treat it as the headline number. It is the wrong number to focus on.

The sinking fund is the long-term maintenance reserve held by the body corporate. It accumulates over time to cover major capital works: external painting, roof repairs, lift replacements, structural issues. When that fund is healthy, owners are protected from sudden out-of-pocket calls on top of their regular levies. When it is underfunded, which happens more often than buyers expect, a special levy becomes a matter of when, not if.

Reading the body corporate records properly before going unconditional takes time, but it is not complicated once you know what to look for. The 10-year maintenance plan, the current sinking fund balance measured against that plan, and the minutes of recent AGMs will tell you the financial condition of the complex more clearly than any other document in the contract.

In the inner east, body corporate properties span a wide range of types: small boutique complexes, older-style walk-ups, and larger modern buildings. The financial health of the body corporate varies significantly across all of them. The article below covers how to read the disclosure statement and what to actually focus on when assessing a body corporate property.
