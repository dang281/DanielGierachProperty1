## Caption

Body corporate levies are a cost most buyers notice on a listing. The sinking fund balance is the one most buyers do not check until they have already made their offer.

The levy is the annual amount that covers the building's day-to-day insurance, maintenance, and administration. For a small townhouse complex in the inner east, that typically runs from around $2,000 to $4,000 per year. For a mid-rise building with a lift, pool, and onsite management, it can be $6,000 to $14,000 or more.

The sinking fund is different. It is the reserve of accumulated contributions set aside for major capital works: roof replacement, lift upgrades, facade repairs, carpark resurfacing. A building with a healthy sinking fund can absorb a $300,000 lift replacement without a special levy. A building with a depleted sinking fund passes that cost to current lot owners.

The body corporate records are available before you sign. The key documents are the last two years of meeting minutes, the financials, and the sinking fund forecast. They tell you whether the building has been well-maintained, what major works are coming, and whether the current levies are going to hold or increase.

I ask for those documents on every inner east unit or townhouse purchase I advise on. The information is there. You just have to request it.
