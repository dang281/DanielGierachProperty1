## Caption

The main residence exemption for capital gains tax does not automatically apply to every home you sell. If the property was rented at any point during your ownership, the exemption is likely to be partial rather than complete.

Most vendors assume full CGT exemption because they lived in the property and think of it as their home. What they do not account for is the period it was tenanted. The ATO calculates the proportion of time the property was used as your main residence versus the proportion it was income-producing, and applies the exemption accordingly. The rental period creates a taxable component. That figure can be significant on an inner east property that has grown substantially in value, and most vendors find out about it at tax time rather than before they sign a contract.

There are a few related points worth checking before you launch a campaign. First: the six-year absence rule allows you to rent out your main residence for up to six years and retain the full exemption, but specific conditions apply and you need a tax professional to confirm your situation qualifies. Second: if you purchased the property before September 1985, different rules apply entirely. Third: holding costs during the rental period may be deductible against the capital gain, which affects the net liability figure.

None of this should stop a well-prepared vendor from selling with confidence. But it should be a conversation with your accountant before settlement, not after.

The full article covers the exemption rules, the absence provision, and the calculation method in plain terms.
