## Caption

Inner-east Brisbane has a supply problem that is structural, not cyclical. The suburbs between the river and the hills, Camp Hill, Hawthorne, Norman Park, Balmoral, Morningside, are established. There's no meaningful land release coming. New townhouse supply is limited by planning constraints and the appetite of existing residents who have consistently opposed density. What you have is what you have.

The other factor is tenure. The people who buy in these suburbs tend to stay. They're often owner-occupiers who have moved specifically for the schools, the walkability, or the character of the housing stock. They're not flipping. They're not speculating on short-term moves. That means turnover is low, and active listing counts in any given week are consistently below what you'd need to give buyers genuine negotiating power.

Layered on top of this is sustained interstate demand. Families relocating from Sydney and Melbourne are concentrated in exactly these suburbs because they recognise the price point relative to what they're coming from. They arrive pre-motivated, pre-approved, and aware that stock is limited.

What this means in practice, for a vendor who understands it, is that a well-prepared listing in Camp Hill or Hawthorne right now is operating in a fundamentally different environment to a listing in a high-supply outer market. The campaign strategy is different. The price conversation is different. The buyer audience is different.

If you're selling in the inner east and want to talk through what that means for your specific street and property type, the conversation starts at danielgierach.com.
